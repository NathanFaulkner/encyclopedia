In this chapter, as well as the next, we study various things we can do with powers, AKA, exponents. Even though the words "power" and "exponent" refer to same thing, there is an important distinction made in the vocabulary by using these two different but synonymous words: "power functions" and "exponential functions" refer to different classes of objects.

A power function \(f\) is any function whose outputs can be obtained by a rule of the form \[ f(x) = Ax^p \] where \(A\) and \(p\) are some real numbers.

The new and interesting topic of discussion in this chapter hinges on the fact that in the definition above, the power (or exponent) \(p\) can be any real number—even a negative or a fraction or \(0\), etc. What are we to make of this?

An exponential function \(f\) is any function whose outputs can be obtained by a rule of the form \[ f(x) = Ab^x \] where \(A\) and \(b\) are some real numbers.

So, you see, exponential functions have the variable plugged into the exponent (or power) whereas with power functions the variable is the thing being raised to the power (or exponent). We will study exponential functions in Chapter 7.

In the first series of sections, we learn various algebraic "rules" for manipulating powers—for simplifying and for rewriting them in various ways. This also motivates the definitions of some more "exotic" exponents: negatives, \(0\), and fractions.
