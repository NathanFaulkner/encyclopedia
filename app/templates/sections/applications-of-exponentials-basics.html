In this section, we will be studying exponential functions from three perspectives: from the graphical perspective, from an algebraic perspective, and from a real-world perspective. The last, of course, is the most vital. In the previous section, we got acquainted with exponential functions by studying their graphs—which we were able to do based on prior knowledge. However, as we move to the algebraic perspective (that is, to the task of solving equations and manipulating expressions) we will actually be using a new technique (albeit with connections to prior concepts); this will involve the study of "logarithms." Of course, at this point, you probably have no clue what a logarithm is. So, for that reason, we turn now to "the real world." We will soon encounter in a natural way the concept of logarithms (so, the task will really be one of assigning this name of "logarithm" to a concept or question that arises quite naturally).

Exponentials have many applications, in fact. Indeed, the period through which we are currently living is one in which the concept of exponential functions is of crucial importance: The concept is certainly a key part to analyzing the spread of infectious disease on the part of epidemiologists, even though greater tools than just exponential functions must also be brought to bear. This is also a period of economic turmoil: Imagine that for various financial institutions who are involved with everything from lending money at interest to providing life insurance to families in the event of a policyholders untimely death, various mathematics become important as they try—with varying success—to predict the future. Again, we find exponential functions appearing in this analysis: for instance, being precisely the elementary form in which one can express how investments and debts grow when a lender compounds interest (that is, charges interest on an outstanding loan and then charges interest on the interest, etc.) Exponential functions also have numerous other scientific applications involving everything from the decay of radioactive elements to the diffusion of chemicals in a fluid (picture a dye spreading in water) or heat through a room. We will touch on a few of these.

It is easiest to start by taking up the subject of charging or awarding interest and how debts or investments change over time. We'll start with a basic example.

Imagine that you have some money that you would like to invest. Maybe your grandparents gave it to you or you earned it on your own, but say you have \$1000 that you would like to set aside for the future. Of course, as you are well aware, banks and other lenders will actually pay you to hold on to your money in the present, with the expectation that you won't need it right away. In the mean time, they loan it out to other people (etc.) and also invest your money in different ways. And, indeed, they pay you for the right to do this in the form of interest. Let's say that a certain bank is offering to pay you 4% interest per year (often abbreviated 4% APR—which means "annual percentage rate"), which, furthermore, they agree to compound for you each year. Imagine, too, that you are not making any withdrawals on this account (to keep things simple). Under this scenario we can discover exactly what is meant by "compounding."
  1. As they are going to pay you 4% per year, at the end of the year you will have your \$1000 in the bank plus 4% more. Here is a handy way to calculate that: \[ 1000 + 0.04 \cdot 1000 = (1+0.04)1000 = (1.04)1000 = \$1040 \] Do you see how I used factoring (AKA, the reverse of distributing) to rewrite \(1000 + 0.04\cdot 1000\)? Look at that closely to make sure you understand it, because it is really the crux of the whole thing.
  2. So, you have your \$1040 throughout the second year of investing. But at the end of that year, you get another 4% added on. Now, maybe what that could have meant is that you just get another \$40. While that would be nice, if the bank is "compounding" your interest, it actually means that they are doing something a lot more sensible—which is to look at the situation quite realistically as if you are just starting anew with a now larger investment—\$1040 rather than \$1000—meaning they award you 4% of 1040, not just 1000. So, at the end of the second year, you have: \[ 1040 + 0.04 \cdot 1040 = (1.04)1040 = \$1081.60 \] Note, again, how I chose to "factor out" 1040. While you would get the same answer without doing that, I do it for a purpose. Watch here for another way to represent your total after 2 years. Recalling that \(1040 = (1.04)1000\), I get \[ (1.04)1040 = (1.04)(1.04)1000 = (1.04)^2 \cdot 1000 \] The important thing to note is that repeated multiplication is happening—AKA, exponentiation.

We already know that exponential functions can be given by formulas of the form \[ f(x) = A b^x \] We have also already named that real number \(b\) the "base" of the exponential. However, provided \(b\gt1\), the value of \(f(x)\) grows with increasing \(x\), so, in this special case, the base is instead often called the growth factor. For instance, in the formula \[ f(x) = 1000 \left(1.04\right)^x \] 1.04 is the growth factor. The value \(A\), which in this example is \(1000\), also has a special name. Since 1000 is what you get for \(y\) when \(x=0\), it is called the initial value of \(f(x)\). In general, if your exponential can be given by \(f(x) = A b^x\), \[ f(0) = A b^0 = A \cdot 1 = A, \] so \(A\) is called the initial value of \(f(x)\). From a graphical point of view, I hope you will realize, \(A\) is also the \(y\)-intercept—the \(y\)-value that gets paired with \(x=0\).

Now, there is something even fancier that a bank will typically do when it comes to awarding interest: they will choose to award interest not just once a year but many times a year. That enables you to earn interest a little earlier. For instance, they might award additional interest every month. Of course, they don't give you each month what you would have earned in a year. In the first month, for instance, they only give you a twelfth of what you would have earned. In the second month, they give you that twelfth again, plus a little more based on the extra interest that you already earned in the first month. This may get a a little confusing, as you can imagine, but, luckily, they actually do something quite simple: they just divide your interest rate by 12 and use that reduced rate as your compounding factor each month—or your "monthly growth factor."     In 5 years, there are \(5\cdot 12 = 60\) months, so you will end up earning \[ 1000 \cdot \left(1+0.04/12\right)^{5 \cdot 12} = 1000 \cdot \left(1+0.04/12\right)^{60} \approx \$ 1220.9965939421215 \] If they only compunded yearly, you would instead make \[ 1000 \cdot \left(1.04\right)^{5} \approx \$ 1216.6529024 \] So, you end up making a few dollars more, for whatever that's worth.

Now, for the big spoiler.

Formula for Investment Value Under Compounded Interest

If you invest \(P\) dollars at an APR of \(r\cdot 100%\) (so, \(r\) is expressed as a decimal), and the bank agrees to compound your interest \(n\) times per year—and you make no withdrawals—then after \(t\) years the current amount \(A(t)\) in your account will be \[ A(t) = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \]

The initial value of an investment (the initial investment) is often called the principal. For instance, if (or when?) you apply for a loan, your initial loan amount may also be called "the principal of the loan." Basically, "principal" is short for "principal amount," as in the "first amount."

Commonly the number of times that a bank compounds (awards) your interest per year is conveyed in English rather than numerically. Sometimes students have questions about how to do that, so let me clarify:

If the bank compounds "[entry]" ...... then [see entry]
Yearly\(n=1\)
Biannually\(n=2\)
Quarterly\(n=4\)
Monthly\(n=12\)
Daily\(n=360^*\)

You might wonder why the bank only compounds your interest 360 times a year instead of 365 times when they advertise that they are compounding "daily." The answer: for convenience; hence the point of the five bank holidays per year. (Don't ask me about leap day, because I don't know; I'm sure Google does.)

Explain how the formula in the box above works. What does each part of the formula "do"?

Answer:

Here is the formula re-copied for ease of reference: \[ A(t) = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \] The \(P\), which stands for "principal", is the initial dollar amount invested (the principal or initial amount). The \(r\) is the annual interest rate, expressed as a decimal—which, to be clear, means the rate, which they advertise, that forms the basis for how much interest they offer not only in the course of the year but in each compounding period. The \(n\) is the number of times the bank compounds (awards) interest each year. For example, if they advertise that they will compound monthly, then \(n = 12\), whereas if they compound quarterly, \(n = 4\). The \(t\) is the number of years over which the investment is observed to grow. Note that this model assumes that no withdrawals are made from the growing account.

The way the formula works is this. First, take \(t\) the number of years that you allow your investment to grow. Next, multiply that number by \(n\), the number of compounding periods in a year. This number \(nt\) tells you how many compounding periods have gone by during the time of the investment. That's a crucial thing to know since the bank increases the size of your balance after each compounding period. How does it do that? Well, it awards a fraction of the interest that you might have earned in a year on whatever your most recent balance was. It gives you precisely the fraction of interest represented by whatever fraction of the year your componding period represents (for instance, one twelfth of the interest if the compounding period is one month). Adding in that fraction of interest is obtained by multiplying the most recent balance by \(\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)\). It can be useful to note that \(\frac{r}{n}\) can also be described as "the fraction of the annual interest rate obtained when dividing by the number of compounding periods in one year". Growth is achieved by starting with the principal \(P\) and then multiplying by this growth factor to represent the balance at the end of one compounding period—and then multiplying that new, larger balance by the same growth factor at the end of the next compounding period; and so on in like manner! The repeated multiplication of this growth factor every compounding period is achieved by putting the total number of compounding periods \(nt\) in the exponent of this growth factor.

Say that you invest \$10,000 at 2% APR, compounded monthly.
  1. Write down a formula for \(A(t)\), the amount in your account after \(t\) years.

    2. Answer:

    \(A(t) = P\left(1+ \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} = 10,000 \left(1+\frac{0.02}{12}\right)^{12t}\)
  2. Find the amount in your account after 5 years. Round to the nearest cent.

    3. Answer:

    \(A(5) = 10,000 \left(1+\frac{0.02}{12}\right)^{12 \cdot 5} \approx \$11,050.79\)

Another common application of exponential functions studied in high school is that of "radioactive decay." The basic idea is that the nuclei or radioactive elements (or, to be more precese "isotopes" of radioactive elements—which is a word to refer to the fact that all atoms of a given elemen have the same number of protons, they may not all have the same number of neutrons) are unstable; they spontaneously decay over time. While the decay of a particular atom is regarded as random. This means two things: you cannot predict the exact moment at which a given nucleus will decay, but can predict, on average, what proportion will decay within a given stretch of time. This latter value is communicated by the number "half life."

The half life of a radioactive substance is the amount of time in which, on average, half of the radioactive atoms (in a "large enough" batch of atoms) will decay. This is merely an average and will not be as accurate if you are only looking at a few atoms as opposed to say, billions. We'll use the symbol \(h\) for this. For instance, Carbon-14 (\(^{14}\)C) is a rare carbon isotope, which happens to be radioactive. It has a hlaf life of 5,730 years, which means that if you have, say, a billion atoms of \(^{14}\)C sitting around, in 5,730 years you'll only have about 500 million left. What happens to the ones that "decayed"? They turned into the stable isotope \(^{14}\)N, Nitrogen-14. Pretty strange, right?

Here is how radioactive decay is modeled:

Formula for Radioactive Decay

If you begin with \(A_0\) units (grams, pounds, moles, atoms, etc.) of a radioisotope with half life \(h\), then after \(t\) years, you will have \[ A(t) = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\frac{t}{h}} \]

Explain how the formula in the box above works. What does each symbol "do"?

Answer:

Here is the formula re-copied for ease of reference: \[ A(t) = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\frac{t}{h}} \] The \(A_0\) is the initial amount (in whatever unit of measurement) of the radioactive substance that you begin with. This use of "subscript \(0\)" is a common way to symbolize "initial amount", with the \(0\) suggesting "initial time" or "zero hour". The \(h\) is the half life of the substance (explained above), measured in whatever unit of time. The \(t\) is the amount of time—expressed in the same units as the half life—over which the substance is observed to decay.

The way the formula works is this: First, take the amount of time \(t\) that the substance has been decaying. Express that time in the same units as the half life. Divide by the size of the half life (do \(\frac{t}{h}\)). This tells you how many half lives have passed in the time since the initial observation. Since (approximately) one half of the substance decays every half life, this number \(\frac{t}{h}\) tells you how many times to cut the initial value \(A_0\) in half. Of course, another way of saying "cut in half" is "multiply by \(\frac{1}{2}\)". Since repeated multiplication is expressed with an exponent, the number of times, \(\frac{t}{h}\), that you need to cut the amount in half thus appears in the exponent of \(\frac{1}{2}\).

As mentioned above, Carbon-14 (\(^{14}\)C) has a half life of 5,730 years. Say that there about 600,000 atoms of \(^{14}\)C in a particular sample of plant matter.
  1. Write down a formula for the (predicted) number of atoms you'll have after \(t\) years.

    2. Answer:

    \(A(t) = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\sfrac{t}{h}} = 600,000 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\sfrac{t}{5730}} \)
  2. Compute the (average) predicted number of atoms of \(^{14}\)C that will be present in the sample after 40,000 years. Round to a couple decimal places.

    3. Answer:

    \(A(t) = 600,000 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\sfrac{40000}{5730}} \approx 4750.29 \)

Some radioisotopes have very long half lives. Some have very short—even down to fractions of a second! One in the middle range is used to fuel space probes like Voyager I, namely a certain compound of plutonium-238, \(^{238}\)Pu. It has a half life of 87.7 years. To be clear, \(^{238}\)Pu is involved in fueling (through the heat generated through radioactive decay) the scientific and communications instruments onboard the craft rather than providing a means of propulsion.
  1. Voyager I was launched on September 5, 1977. Develop a formula for the percent of \(^{238}\)Pu that is predicted to remain after \(t\) years.

    2. Answer:

    \[ A(t) = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\sfrac{t}{h}} = 100% \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\sfrac{t}{87.7}} \]
  2. Use your formula to figure out what percent of \(^{238}\)Pu remains in Voyager I today.

    3. Answer:

    The only hard part here comes in if you want to worry about precisely how many years it has been since September 5, 1977. I am writing this April 28, 2020. Of course, the years gone by include the years \(y\) so that \(1978 + y = 2020\). Of course, solving for \(y\), you get \( y = 42\). But, if you want to get really precise (and no one says you have to) you might also include the extra days: \begin{align*} [\textrm{part of April}] &+ [\textrm{March}] + [\textrm{Feb.}] + [\textrm{Jan.}] + [\textrm{Dec.}] + [\textrm{Nov}] + [\textrm{Oct}] + [\textrm{part of Sept}] \\ = & 28 + 31 + 29 + 31 + 31 + 30 + 31 + (30-5)\\ = & 236 \end{align*} So in all, we might call it \(42 + \frac{236}{365} \approx 43.65\) years. Now, we just apply the formula: \[ A(t) = 100% \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\sfrac{42.64}{87.7}} \approx 71.38% \]
