In this section, we will be studying exponential functions from three perspectives: from the graphical perspective, from an algebraic perspective, and from a real-world perspective. The last, of course, is the most vital. In the previous section, we got acquainted with exponential functions by studying their graphs—which we were able to do based on prior knowledge. However, as we move to the algebraic perspective (that is, to the task of solving equations and manipulating expressions) we will actually be using a new technique (albeit with connections to prior concepts); this will involve the study of "logarithms." Of course, at this point, you probably have no clue what a logarithm is. So, for that reason, we turn now to "the real world." We will soon encounter in a natural way the concept of logarithms (so, the task will really be one of assigning this name of "logarithm" to a concept or question that arises quite naturally).

Exponentials have many applications, in fact. Indeed, the period through which we are currently living is one in which the concept of exponential functions is of crucial importance: The concept is certainly a key part to analyzing the spread of infectious disease on the part of epidemiologists, even though greater tools than just exponential functions must also be brought to bear. This is also a period of economic turmoil: Imagine that for various financial institutions who are involved with everything from lending money at interest to providing life insurance to families in the event of a policyholders untimely death, various mathematics become important as they tr—with varying success—to predict the future. Again, we find exponential functions appearing in this analysis: for instance, being precisely the elementary form in which one can express how investments and debts grow when a lender compounds interest (that is, charges interest on an outstanding loan and then charges interest on the interest, etc.) Exponential functions also have numerous other scientific applications involving everything from the decay of radioactive elements to the diffusion of chemicals in a fluid (picture a dye spreading in water) or heat through a room. We will touch on a few of these.
