In this section, we learn a handy way of symbolizing the set of all solutions to a linear inequality or to a compound inequality—as well as many other types of equations or systems. While a graph is certainly a way to represent these solution sets—and inequality notation (together with logical connectors "and" and "or") is yet another way—we learn this third way, called interval notation in this one. Why do we need a third way of representing these sets? It is for two reasons: For one, interval notation is compact; it takes little effort to write it in comparison with a graph. It will also be without the potential ambiguity or imprecision of a drawing. But what advantage does it have over inequality notation? Inequality notation consists in statements about \(x\). As such, it represents complete sentences or at least grammatical fragments of one depending on one's intention. As we have noted, sometimes the "secret narrative" is not expressed in math textbooks, leaving inequality possibly unclear to the reader. Interval notation, on the other hand, is much simpler. Its meaning is completely clear cut: What we will be devising in this section is symbols for nouns—for the objects themselves—the solution sets, which, really, will involve quite a bit less grammar and not potential for ambiguity.

What is an Interval?

An interval basically consists in (1) a left endpoint, (2) a right endpoint, and (3) ``everything in between''---that is, all intervening values. However, since \(-\infty\) and \(\infty\) are allowed as endpoints of an interval (which is a funny way of saying the interval is missing an endpoint), it can be more straightforward to say that an interval is a subset of the real line that has no breaks in it. But my first description with the (1), (2), (3) will help you understand a special notation for intervals (called interval notation). We'll take a look at interval notation, but, first, I have just referred to invervals as subsets of the real line. Now what does that mean? What are ``sets'' or ``subsets'' in math? If you don't know, just ask me (or your neighbor or the Internet---but please ask me if none of the answers you get make sense.) Interval notation works like this: you write the endpoints (possibly $-\infty$ or $\infty$) in order, left to right, separated by a comma. Then you surround that pair of numbers with some combination of parentheses or (square) brackets. Which you use depends on whether or not the endpoint is included in the interval or not (it may or may not be). Use a parenthesis next to an endpoint that is not included and a brace next to an endpoint that \emph{is} included. By convention, $-\infty$ and $\infty$ are never ``included'' (because ``you never get there.'')

It makes sense to start with a very basic one, so we can discuss the underlying meaning, before we start adding in the task of solving, don't you think?

Consider this very basic compound inequality: \[ -3 \lt x \lt 4 \]
  1. What does it mean?

    Answer:

    This is shorthand for the narrative "\(x\) is a real number that is greater than \(-3\) (to the right of \(-3\), on a horizontal real line) and less than 4". In fact, there is another important way that you can represent this statement (which you will have to use when answering the exercises, because of the way I programmed the answer-checking algorithm). This inequality can also be written \[ x \gt -3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad x \lt 4 \]

    The use of the word "and" is critical here. Actually, let me tell you that the most important concepts in this section are those represented by the words "and" and "or". The meaning and concept behind these words is a fundamental aspect of logic, which pervades not only mathematics but computer programming and philosophy and other systems of logic. We need to contemplate their meaning.

    Now, while you are, of course, familiar with the English words "and" and "or", this is math (and computer science and philosophy at the same time). You might think that's a bad thing—but I know you are also familiar with one of the strengths of math expression over-and-above common English. Or, maybe you are just beginning to understand the key strength (and, at the same time, limitation) of math: everything in math has a very precise, defined meaning (and anything not expressible in such terms is not amenable to mathematical reasoning.) In point of fact, the English words "and" and "or" carry some ambiguity in this and other contexts—an ambiguity which must be allayed by our careful definition of these words.

    Let me define the logical connector 'and' first by talking about in the context of the compound inequality above. The inequality \[ x \gt -3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad x \lt 4 \] should really be understood as describing or defining a set of real numbers—the set of real numbers that satisfies it (that make it a true statement when plugged in for \(x\)). Think of this set as a "club" and this expression as the means by which you decide whether a number is member or not; it is a test of membership. So, take some random real number, like 5. So, you test it against the criteria (with that special logical connector): you first observe that *yes* \(5 \gt -3\). However, it must have both that property and the next; it must also be less than 4. It isn't, so it is not a member of this set. This is pretty easy stuff, but you will want to compare this to what I say about the connector 'or' n the next example!

    Maybe the best way to realize the meaning is to shade on a real line where \(x\) could possibly be, as in the next question.

Consider this very basic compound inequality: \[ x \lt -3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x \gt 4 \]
  1. What does it mean?

    Answer:

    This is shorthand for the narrative "\(x\) is a real number that is (either) less than \(-3\) (to the left of \(-3\), on a horizontal real line) or greater than 4". It is critical that we contemplate what this means.

    Again, you ought to look at this inequality as a "test of membership" in a club. So, take some random real number, like 5. We test it for membership by considering each of the inequalities in the compound expression but also carefully processing the meaning of the logical connector. So, is \(x\) less than -3? *No*, of course not. Does that mean that \(x\) is out of the club? No! That's because of the 'or': to be in this "club", 5 only needs to meet one of the conditions in the compound expression---not both! So, "and" means "has to satisfy both" whereas "or" means "only needs to satisfy one of the conditions". Indeed, as \(5 \gt 4\), 5 is indeed in the club; it is part of the set defined by this compound expression.

Solve the inequality. Give your answer both in inequality notation and by graphing the solution on a number line. \[ -3 \lt 3x - 12 \leq 18 \]

Answer:

It's time to take a step back and consider the underlying narrative of the solving process; this is just something we need to do each time something new is introduced—to carefully consider the question, "What does it mean?"

In solving, we are first saying, "Suppose there is a real number \(x\) with the given property." In this case, we are imagining that if you take 12 away from the product of 3 times \(x\), you get something that falls between -3 and 18 but possibly equal to 18. Let me point out that the inequalities we are studying in this section are called "compound". That really means that they include more than one statement about \(x\)—added information, if you will!—which are joined together by "logical connectors", 'and' or 'or'. See the first two examples for a definition of these.

In the context of this problem, the 'and' connector (which is always implied when only a single \(x\) is used—\(x\) must satisfy both statements!) means that both, whatever \(x\) ends up being, it the value of \(3x - 12\) must be greater than \(-3\) but also less than or equal to \(18\). So, we have two separate inequalities that we can solve in turn: \begin{align*} & 3x - 12 \gt -3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad 3x-12 \leq 18 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x \gt 9 \quad \textrm{and} \quad 3x \leq 30 & \textrm{For each, add 12 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \gt 3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad x \leq 10 & \textrm{For each, divide both sides by 3.}\\ \end{align*}

Of course, there is a certain amount of redundancy in this process—in that we end up doing the same thing to both sides of each inequality at each step. So, there is a more streamlined way of writing this:

\begin{align*} & -3 \lt 3x - 12 \leq 18 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9 \lt 3x \leq 30 & \textrm{I added 12 to all three columns.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3 \lt x \leq 10 & \textrm{I divided all three columns by 3.}\\ \end{align*}
