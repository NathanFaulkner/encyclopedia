In this section, we learn a handy way of symbolizing the set of all solutions to a linear inequality or to a compound inequality—as well as many other types of equations or systems. While a graph is certainly a way to represent these solution sets—and inequality notation (together with logical connectors "and" and "or") is yet another way—we learn this third way, called interval notation in this one. Why do we need a third way of representing these sets? It is for two reasons: For one, interval notation is compact; it takes little effort to write it in comparison with a graph. It will also be without the potential ambiguity or imprecision of a drawing. But what advantage does it have over inequality notation? Inequality notation consists in statements about \(x\). As such, it represents complete sentences or at least grammatical fragments of one depending on one's intention. As we have noted, sometimes the "secret narrative" is not expressed in math textbooks, leaving inequality possibly unclear to the reader. Interval notation, on the other hand, is much simpler. Its meaning is completely clear cut: What we will be devising in this section is symbols for nouns—for the objects themselves—the solution sets, which, really, will involve quite a bit less grammar and not potential for ambiguity.

What is an Interval?

An interval basically consists in (1) a left endpoint, (2) a right endpoint, and (3) ``everything in between''---that is, all intervening values. However, since \(-\infty\) and \(\infty\) are allowed as endpoints of an interval (which is a funny way of saying the interval is missing an endpoint), it can be more straightforward to say that an interval is a subset of the real line that has no breaks in it. But my first description with the (1), (2), (3) will help you understand a special notation for intervals (called interval notation). We'll take a look at interval notation, but, first, I have just referred to invervals as subsets of the real line. Now what does that mean? What are "sets" or "subsets" in math? Interval notation works like this: you write the endpoints (possibly \(-\infty\) or \(\infty\)) in order, left to right, separated by a comma. Then you surround that pair of numbers with some combination of parentheses or (square) brackets. Which you use depends on whether or not the endpoint is included in the interval or not (it may or may not be). Use a parenthesis next to an endpoint that is not included and a brace next to an endpoint that is included. By convention, -\infty and \infty are never "included" (because "you never get there."")

\[ x \leq -4 \; \textrm{ or } \; x \geq 7 \] {% set svg = 'smallsvg8' %} {% set points = [{'x': -4, 'type': 'empty'}, {'x': 7, 'type': 'empty'}] %} {% set intervals = [[-20,-4], [7,20]] %} {% include "_static-real-line.html" %}
But this and other sets like it will be the solution (set) of certain compound inequalities—"or inequalities" to be exact! Since they are not intervals, there is no interval notation for them, exactly. But there is a special symbol that we will use with the interval notation to symbolize the notion that "the solution is the two (disconnected) subsets combined."" Basically, we will use a symbol that functions very much like "addition for sets." The concept is called "union," and it is symbolized by the union symbol \(\cup\). For example, the set shaded just above is the union of two intervals, symbolized like so: \[ (-\infty, -4] \cup [7,\infty) \]

Here's another quick example of a union. \[ \textrm{North Carolina} \cup \textrm{South Carolina} = \textrm{The Carolinas} \]
