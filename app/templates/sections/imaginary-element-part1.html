In this section, we'll touch on a subject which has struck hosts of Algebra 2 students who came before you as useless. One problem for us is that its usefulness lies beyond our immediate scope of interest—but I will still touch on it.

As a quick intro, we return here to the claim that any polynomial (f) (of degree (n>0), say) can be factored into the following form: \[ f(x) = a(x-z_1)(x-z_2)\cdots(x-z_n) \] where the (z_i)'s are numbers and (a) is too. By the way, this fact is called the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. Fancy-sounding, isn't it? We also argued that if you can factor a polynomial, then you can find its zeroes (aka "solutions")—and vice versa. But, here's the thing. Sometimes we found that there was no solution to certain quadratics.

(Motivation)

For instance, what happens when you perform the quadratic formula on this: \[ x^2 + x+ 1 = 0 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} x &= \frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\\ & = \frac{-1\pm\sqrt{1 - 4}}{2}\\ & = \frac{-1\pm\sqrt{-3}}{2}\\ \end{align*} We know that \(\sqrt{-3}\) isn't a real number, so we have "no solution."

In this section, we invent a solution to this equation. (Some might say "discover" a solution, but I leave that up to you.) Why on Earth would we want to do that? Well, for one thing, it will enable us to factor this polynomial like so \[ x^2 + x + 1 = (x-z_1)(x-z_2) \] where \(z_1\) and \(z_2\) are as some sort of spurious "numbers" whose nature I have yet to disclose in this narrative. So, read on, brave student!

What we study here is "imaginary numbers," also called complex numbers, of which the primary example is \(\sqrt{-1}\), which we will mostly denote by the symbol \(i\). What does all this mean? For ine thing, it means that we are inventing solutions to the equation \[ x^2 = -1 \] whereas, before, we would have just noted that there is no solution. First, note why it is quite sensible to say that there is no solution: what real number times itself comes out to be \(-1\)? Noting that a "positive times a positive" comes out positive and a "negative times a negative" comes out positive as well, we see there is no real number \(x\) that can be multiplied by itself to come to "negative anything," in particular to \(-1\). That is to say, \(-1\) doesn't have a square root. So, you will be asking yourself—as you should!—What is the point of going on to write this: \begin{align*} x^2 &= -1\\ \Rightarrow \quad x = \sqrt{-1} \quad & \textrm{or} \quad x = -\sqrt{-1} \end{align*} In fact, we'll actually express the "answer" as \[ x = i \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -i \] where it is understood that \(i\) represents \(\sqrt{-1}\). Let me show you what sort of knowledge concerning \(i\) is traditionally expected of Algebra 2 students, and then we will go on to try to understand more. The key to it all is the following:

It is common to call \(i = \sqrt{-1}\) an "imaginary number." You can also refer to it as the imaginary element. While I'm happy if you dispute the use of the word "number" in this case, there is a purpose in calling it a number. The point is to suggest that "all the familiar rules of arithmetic apply;" it's just that we have a new rule, namely \[ i^2 = -1 \]

Hey, by the way, what are "the rules of arithmetic"?

List all the rules of arithmetic that you can think of. I'll get you started:

So, we will call \(i = \sqrt{-1}\) a number in the sense that we are declaring that we intend to keep all the other "rules of numbers" the same, only to add one more in: \(i^2 = -1\). That is, we'll call it a number because we are going to treat it like one.

Simplify the following expressions by using all the usual rules of arithmetic together with the added rule that \(i^2 = -1\).
  1. \(i^3\)

    Answer:

    \(i^3 = i^2 \cdot i = (-1) i = -i\)
  2. \(i^4\)

    Answer:

    \(i^4 = (i^2) (i^2) = (-1)(-1) = 1\)
  3. \(i^{18}\)

    Answer:

    \(i^{18} = (i^2)^9 = (-1)^9 = -1\)
  4. \(i^{19}\)

    Answer:

    \(i^{19} = (i^2)^9 \cdot i = (-1)^9 \cdot i = -i\)
  5. \((4i)^2\)

    Answer:

    \((4i)^2 = (4i)(4i) = 16i^2 = -16\)
  6. \((-2i)^2\)

    Answer:

    \((-2i)^2 = (-2i)(-2i) = 4 i^2 = -4\)
  7. \((i\sqrt{5})^2\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} (i\sqrt{5})^2 &= (i\sqrt{5})(i\sqrt{5}) \\ &= i^2 \cdot 5 \\ &= -1\cdot 5 = -5 \end{align*}
  8. \(\left(-i\sqrt{7}\right)^2\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} \left(-i\sqrt{7}\right)^2 &= \left(-i\sqrt{7}\right)\left(-i\sqrt{7}\right) \\ &= i^2 \cdot 7 \\ &= -1\cdot 7 = -7 \end{align*}
  9. \(\left(-i\sqrt{7}i\right)^{ 3 }\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} \left(-i\sqrt{7}\right)^{ 3 } &= \left(-i\sqrt{7}\right)\left(-i\sqrt{7}\right)\left(-i\sqrt{7}i\right) \\ &= -i^3 \sqrt{7}\cdot\sqrt{7}\cdot\sqrt{7} \\ &= -i^2 \cdot i \left(\sqrt{7}\sqrt{7}\right)\sqrt{7} \\ &= -(-1)i\cdot 7 \sqrt{7} \\ &= 7i\sqrt{7} \end{align*}
  10. \(i(3 + 4i)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} i(3 + 4i) &= 3i + 4i^2 \\ &= 3i - 4 \\ &= -4 + 3i \end{align*}
  11. \(-i (9-2i)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} -i (9-2i) &= -9i + 2i^2 \\ &= -9i - 2 \\ &= -2 - 9i \end{align*}
  12. \(-4 i \left(8 + 5i\right)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} -4 i \left(8 + 5i\right) &= -32i - 20i^2 \\ &= -32i - 20\cdot(-1) \\ &= -32i + 20\\ &= 20 - 32i \end{align*}
  13. \((2-i)(3 + 4i)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} (2-i)(3 + 4i) &= 6 + 8i - 3i - 4i^2 \\ &= 6 + 5i + 4\\ &= 10 + 5i \end{align*}
  14. \((9+2i)(9-2i)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} (9+2i)(9-2i) &= 81 - 18i + 18i - 4i^2 \\ &= 81 + 4 \\ &= 85 \end{align*}
You may have noticed that I chose to rewrite various answers in the form \(a+bi\). That is a pretty standard thing to do. In fact,...

A complex number is any combination of \(i\) and regular numbers via arithmetic. For instance, \[ -1+i\sqrt{3} \] is a complex number. A complex number written in the form \[ a+bi \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad a +ib \] where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers is said to be in standard form. Furthermore, you call \(a\) the real part of the number and \(b\) the imaginary part of the number.
