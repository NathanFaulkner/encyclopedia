As I argued in the section on absolute value equations, absolute value is a pretty simple concept. So, why does it become difficult to work with it sometimes? I argued that the reason is that, all too often, either by training or inclination, the habit in math is to "turn one's brain off" and to just try to "follow the rules" instead of the critical alternative: to always think about the meaning of what one is doing. You see, while, ideally, "following the rules" is exactly what you want to do, the problem is that, if one isn't thinking carefully about a problem, one might not actually remember what the rules are or might wantonly misapply "rules" that really have nothing to do with the situation at hand and so really aren't rules at all—but rather the appearance of rules! (I hope you will agree that there might be some life lessons here, too!)

To further put this advice or philosophy to the test, I offer you some absolute value problems that are a little more challenging than the ones addressed in the section linked to above. These actually just involve a simple change but one which again forces you to take a step back and consider the basic meaning of absolute value in order to solve. That is, the situation will be novel enough that your previous schema or "rule" that you may have developed to solve equations in the section on absolute value equations, will no longer apply. You'll have to either develop new rules or schema or, much better yet, stop continually developing rules and instead think at a fundamental level about the very simple meaning and definitions of the concepts being expressed in a given problem and use those to build in a creative way your own ways of solving (while also thinking in fundamental terms about how to check the truth of your answers.) The small tweak (that causes relatively big problems) that we consider in this section is just to introduce more than one term involving \(x\) in addition to the absolute value term. Actually, it isn't so much harder to go ahead and consider more than one absolute value term as well. So, in this section, we will consider equations with two or 3 terms involving \(x\) at least one of which and at most two of which also involves absolute value terms. But as a further challenge to yourself, you should see if you can solve equations that involve three absolute value terms—to thereby begin to assay your general agility and creativity in problem-solving rather than merely testing your ability to regurgitate somebody else's methods all the time.

Solve the equation. (That is, give a simple description of the set of all real numbers \(x\) that satisfy the given equation.) \[ |x + 1| = |x - 7| \]

Answer, via "Visual" Argument:

What can we do here? Well, the best advice is to just think about the meaning of the symbols and of the task at hand. Recall that "solving the equation..." always involves starting off with an often unstated narrative declaration:
Suppose that \(x\) is a real number with the given property...
In other words, we are assuming that \(x\) is an honest, real number, meaning that it is sitting somewhere on the real line. Our task in solving is to deduce various ("necessary") properties about \(x\) starting from the given. We apply very basic steps of logic along the way to make sure that we don't trick ourselves into believing something false. In a way, what we are doing is doing our best not to tell a lie! In the end, it is also not only prudent but necessary to check one's answers. After all, we will try to deduce the only possible solutions but we must check them (either directly or by some other argument of logic) that they are actual soltions. In all, you have to remain faithful to the meaning of the given. So, what does the given statement mean in this case? Accepting that \(x\) is a real number, we have that the absolute value of 1 more than this number comes out to be the same as the absolute value of 7 less than this number. Is this even possible? It may not be clear from the start that there is any possible value for \(x\); the solution set could be "empty" (no solution), after all. But, giving it a try, the only possible step of logic we can take is to think about the meaning of absolute value. As noted in the section on absolute value equations, the concept of absolute value couldn't be simpler: if a number \(x\) is negative, the absolute value of the number is what we get by just stripping off the negative sign, and if the number is positive, the absolute value of the number is just itself. Put another way, absolute value just gives the plain, old distance of \(x\) from 0.

Now, the given doesn't tell us about the absolute value of just \(x\). Instead, it talks about about 1 more than \(x\) (which is also a number!) and about 7 less than \(x\). But we can also translate the absolute value of these numbers into a meaning or fact about \(x\). You see, if you do \(x - 7\), you are finding the difference between \(x\) and 7, are you not? And by taking the absolute value, you are actually just making sure it comes out positive (after all, \(x - 7\) might come out positive if \(x\) is to the right of 7 but negative if \(x\) is to the left). So, in that sense, the expression \(|x-7|\) actually can be translated into meaning as

\(|x - 7|\) = the distance of \(x\) from \(7\)
Similarly, even though you could say that \(x + 1\) is a sum not a difference, you can also interpret it as a difference: \[ x + 1 = x - (-1) \] So, \(x + 1\) represents the difference of \(x\) and \(-1\), which might come out negative or positive. But then taking the absolute value makes it come out positive no matter what, so \[ |x + 1| = |x - (-1)| \] represents the "distance of \(x\) from \(-1\) on the real line".

Thus, in all, our equation \[ |x + 1| = |x - 7| \] says "the distance from \(x\) to \(-1\) equals the distance of \(x\) to \(7\)". So, is there a number that is at an equal distance to both \(-1\) and \(7\)? Well, how about the number that lies halfway between them? There is certainly a number at the halfway point. I bet we could find it. And if you were any other number, wouldn't you have to be closer to \(-1\) than \(7\) or vice versa? So, it will be a unique solution. Of course, the full distance between \(-1\) and \(7\) is \(8\), so the halfway point will be at a distance of \(4\) from each (in other words the distance represented by both the left and right hand sides—which are equal after all!—will be \(4\).) So, the solution is \(-1 + 4 = 3\). You can easily chek your answer. Does \(x = 3\) satisfy both equations? Yes. After all, if you substitute in \(x = 3\), you get \[ \textrm{LHS} = |x + 1| = |3 + 1| = |4| = 4 \] and \[ \textrm{RHS} = |x - 7| = |3 - 7| = |-4| = 4 \]

Answer, via Algebraic Method:

Now, here is the dirty secret of mathematicians: When he or she finds a solution, there always remains some doubt about the correctness of their solution: "Did I make a mistake?"; "Did I fool myself in logic in one step or another?"; "Was there some oversight in my logical process?" So, the concept of "proof" was invented—as a disciplined method for saving one to the greatest extent possible from the occasional embarassment of "being wrong". That's maybe nothing to hide, since the concept of "proof" is pretty famous and vaunted, after all. I actually haven't gotten to the "dirty secret" yet! The secret is that, quite often, a mathematician will have one method of checking the truth of their conjectures but will often write down another more careful way for the scrutiny of other mathematicians and authors. That is, quite often, a mathematician is not assured by a single proof but instead looks for a second. By the time they have developed and written down the second proof, they are feeling pretty confident, so they will then publish this proof for others to consider, even though lots of the juicy insights that really help one understand how to come up with the solution at all are really in the first proof, which was the genesis of it all, which remains withheld to the public and, sometimes, even forgotten by the mathematician him or herself. So, what I have done is given you only one proof. But there may still be some room for doubt about it! So, to be completely honest, I will offer you a second proof—which is to say—a second argument as the correctness of my solution. This method will be what I use exclusively in the harder examples below.

For the second method, we still think about the meaning of absoute value, but, to be different, we don't think about the "picture" on the real line of what absolute value means but instead think about how to write down in symbols what absolute value means. Also, be sure to keep in mind the fact that we are assuming \(x\) to be a real number. Therefore, \(x + 1\) is also a real number, and so is \(x - 7\)—and \(|x+1|\) and \(|x - 7|\) are too for that matter. In fact, we are assuming that \(|x + 1|\) and \(|x- 7|\) come out to be the same real number, though we don't know what that number that will be yet (at least apeaking hypothetically). Regarding the meaning of this, also recall that absolute value just means "make it positive if it was negative and otherwise leave the number alone". So, here is how we can deal with it. The number \(x + 1\) might be negative or it might be positive depending on what \(x\) turns out to be. After all, if \(x\) is some "big" negative number like \(-100\), then \(x + 1\) would be like \(-100 + 1 = -99\), a negative number. If \(x + 1\) comes out positive, then actually, \(|x + 1| = x +1 \). After all, again, the absolute value of a positive number is just itself. So, if \(x +1 \) is positive, then its absolute value is just itself. So, when does it come out positive? Well, our mode during this method of solution is to write down in symbols what we mean. So, here is "\(x + 1\) is positive" in symbols: \[ x + 1 \gt 0 \] Of course, we can "solve" (i.e.: simplify) that statement to this: \[ x \gt -1 \] So, \(x + 1\) is positive precisely when \(x + 1 \gt 0\). So, in all, we can say that if \(x > -1\), then \[ |x + 1| = x + 1 \] That could be a handy way of simplifying our equation!—but be careful, we can only do that if \(x \gt -1\), which we don't know to be the case: after all, we don't know what \(x\) is! We are only assuming it is a real number and nothing beyond that. And, yet, there is nothing stopping us from imposing further assumptions, so long as we are careful about keeping track of them and also of checking all the different possibilities out to make sure we don't let something fall through the cracks. What I am talking about is a problem-solving approach called "solving by cases". That is, we will first consider the possibility that \(x \gt -1\) to see what values for \(x\) will work that are greater than \(-1\) and then check out the other "case" separately of \(x \lt -1\). So, to get started we could write (trying to keep track of things carefully):

Case: \(x \gt -1\):

\begin{align*} & |x + 1| = |x - 7|\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 1 = |x - 7| \end{align*} So, that's an improvement, maybe, but it still isn't clear what to do from there. Well, it might be helpful to consider a simpler example of "solving by cases" first, but this ain't it. You see, the bold insight to move forward with here is that we can just keep imposing or refining our cases! We can make a further assumption! How about we look at the case that \begin{align*} & x - 7 \gt 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \gt 7 \end{align*} In that case, it will also be true that \(x \gt -1\) (after all, a number which is to the right of \(7\) is also to the right of \(-1\), is it not?), so it will fall under but refine the earlier case that we are already considering. So, we refine our "case logic":

Case: \(x \gt 7\):

Then, \(x \gt -1\), too, which means that \(x + 1 \gt 0\) and hence \(|x + 1| = x + 1\) and also \(x - 7 \gt 0\), which means \(|x - 7| = x - 7\). Thus, \begin{align*} & |x + 1| = |x - 7|\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 1 = x - 7\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 1 = -7 \end{align*} which is not true. Thus, we conclude that, under the assumption that \(x \gt 7\) there is no solution. We are getting somewhere! We have just eliminated a huge chunk of the real line from consideration. And we can proceed by just checking out the other cases. Actually, to be very thourogh, you should also note that we can easily dispatch with the possibility that \(x = 7\): You can just plug in \(x = 7\) to see that the equation will not be satisfied with this value.

Case: \(-1 \leq x \lt 7\):

What am I up to now? Well, we have already ruled out \(x \geq 7\) from consideration. So, we want to consider the case that \(x \lt 7\). But, in the discussion above, we also talked about the helpfulness of considering separately the case of \(x \gt -1\) and \(x \lt -1\) separately. Indeed, if I know that \(x \gt -1\), then I know that \(x + 1 \gt 0\) and hence \(|x + 1| = x + 1\) which could, potentially, make our equation easier to solve. Actually, we could broaden our assumption a little here, since if \(x = -1\) then \(x + 1 = 0\) and hence \(|x + 1| = x + 1\), because both equal 0. So, that's why I wrote \(-1 \leq x\) in the description of the current case in the header above. In all, \(-1 \leq x \lt 7\) just means "\(x\) is above or equal to \(-1\) and yet below \(7\)"; it is between \(-1\) and \(7\). Great! So, what can we do under this assumption? Well, assuming that \(x \geq -1\) we also know that \(x + 1\geq 0\) and hence \(|x + 1| = x + 1\). That's nice. But what about \(|x - 7|\)? Can we simplify this statement? Note that this does NOT equal: \(|x - 7|\) does not equal \(x - 7\). After all, if \(x \lt 7\), then \(|x - 7|\) will come out to be positive whereas the expression \(x - 7\) would be negative. So, what do we do? Well, what can we do to a number to change it from negative to a positive? You might answer, "take its absolute value", and you'd be right, but that wouldn't be getting us anywhere, since we are trying to get rid of the absolute value signs in the interest of simplifiying or changing our given equation. So, think of something else: what can you do to a number to change it from negative to positive? Well, you could negate it! That is, as you know, "the negative of a negative is a positive", no? (By the way, "negative" on the real line just means "go in the opposite direction"; it is about direction. "Positive" means "go right", so "negative of positive" means "go the opposite of right", meaning to the left, whereas, "negative of negative" means "go the opposite of left", which means, "right", of course.) So, \(x \lt 7\) means \(x - 7 \lt 0\) means "\(x-7\) is negative" which means that \(-(x - 7) = 7 - x\) will be the positive version of this number. Thus, under this case, we can write \(|x - 7| = -(x - 7) = 7 - x\). In all, assuming that \(-1 \leq x \lt 7\), we can write (recalling also that \(|x + 1| = x + 1\) under this assumption) \begin{align*} & |x + 1| = |x - 7|\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 1 = 7 - x\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x = 6 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 3 \end{align*} So, look, we have found the sole solution that I argued for in the first method above! All that remains is to consider the final case of \(x \lt -1\) (and, according to our answer above, rule out any solutions in that part of the real line).

Case: \(x \lt -1\):

This time, \(x + 1\) is negative, which means \(|x + 1| = -(x +1) = -x - 1\) (see the discussion in the previous case for explanation). Also, \(x \lt -1\) means that \(x \lt 7\), too, so \(x - 7 \lt 0\) and hence \(|x - 7| = - (x - 7) = 7 - x\). Thus, we get \begin{align*} & |x + 1| = |x - 7|\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -x - 1 = 7 - x\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 1 = 7 \\ \end{align*} This is a false statement, which confirms that there is no solution in this case. That means that the only solution is that of \(x = 3\) that we already noted.

Answer, via a New Graphical Perspective:

I think the solution in this case (of "no solution") may be unsettling enough that is worth considering it from another perspective. Thinking the private, concealed thoughts of a mathematician: well, there might just be a little room for doubt about the correctness of the proof just given. When that happens, you look for another proof or at least another way to represent the situation, even if you keep this other way of thinking secret from your friends.

This other perspective takes just a little explaining in case it is new to you. It involves viewing the left and right hand sides of our equations as "\(y\)"-values—outputs from two different functions when you plug in a given \(x\). Put another way, we are looking for the special \(x\) value that produces the very same \(y\)-value from each of the functions \[ y = -x - 1 \] and \[ y = |x - 7| \] Put yet another way, we are trying to find the \(x,y\)-pair (even though we really only care about the \(x\) with the \(y\) just being what the two sides both come out to equal for that special \(x\)) that satisfies the system \[ \begin{cases} y = - x - 1\\ y = |x - 7| \end{cases} \] Now, as we discussed in the section on systems of equations the set of \((x, y)\)-pairs that solve a particular system can be realized graphically as the points of intersection of the graphs of the two equations in the system. After all, the graph of an equation is just (a picture of) the set of pairs that satisfy that equation. And if what we want is the set of pairs that satisfy both equations, then what we want is the set of pairs that lie on both graphs—which, visually-speaking are just the points of intersection.

So, we graph both equations and look for intersections—or, as it turns out in this case, use it to help explain why it won't have any!

There is actually quite a variety of things that can happen with the solution set of equations like these. For instance, we saw in the previous example that one side of the equation might represent an absolute value function while the other side represents a linear function—with a line for its graph. Solve the equation. (That is, give a simple description of the set of all real numbers \(x\) that satisfy the given equation.) \[ 0 = 9 x + 7 \left|{x}\right| + \left|{2 x + 4}\right| + 4 \]

Answer:

I am going to consider by cases. Also, in preparation for adopting a method of uniformly representing all possible solutions to these problems, I will be using interval notation to express each case I am considering. As demonstrated in examples above, considering by cases means to consider separately regions of the real line where the expressions inside each absolute value term will come out negative versus coming out positive. For instance, we have the term \(|x|\) in the equation above. I know that when \(x\) is positive, \(|x|\) is just \(x\). So, if I restrict myself to consider only possible solutions where \(x \geq 0\), I can literally substitute \(x\) in place of \(|x|\). Then, hopefully, I can just solve in the usual way (after also having dealt with the other absolute value term in a similar way.) But when \(x\) is negative, \(|x|\) won't be \(x\), since \(|x|\) has to come out positive whereas \(x\) is negative. On the other hand, I can also make a negative value positive by taking the negative of it. Therefore, for \(x \lt 0\), I have \(|x| = -x\). Make sense? So, when I consider the case of solutions with \(x \lt 0\), I can literally make the substitution of the expression \(-x\) in place of \(|x|\). This could be very helpful! Actually, it is also worth noting that, if \(x = 0\), then \[ |x| = |0| = 0 = -0 \] So, when \(x = 0\), \(x = x\) and \(x = -x\). So, you could make any of those inequalities in this paragraph into "or equal to" inequalities and they would still be true.

I also need to treat the other absolute value term in like fashion. I know that when \(2x + 4\) comes out positive, then \(|2x + 4|\) will just equal \(2x+4\). However, when \(2x+4\) comes out negative, then \(|2x+4|\) will not equal \(2x+4\). On the other hand, it will equal the negative of that number, since the negative of a negative is a positive! So, when \(2x+4\) is negative, you'll have that \(|2x+4| = -(2x + 4) = -2x - 4\). Now, when is \(2x+4\) positive versus negative? Presenting this in symbols can help. The statement "\(2x+4\) is positive" can be written \[ 2x + 4 \gt 0 \] And, of course, you could just solve this expression to figure out what \(x\) values will make this happen: \begin{align*} 2x + 4 & \gt 0\\ 2x & \gt -4 \\ x & \gt \frac{-4}{2} = -2 \end{align*} Thus, when \(x \gt -2\), the number \(2x + 4\) comes out positive, and, plainly, when \(x \lt -2\), the number \(2x + 4\) comes out negative. So, for \(x \lt -2\), \[ |2x + 4| = -(2x+4) = -2x - 4 \] and for \(x \gt -2\) \[ |2x + 4| = 2x + 4 \] Actually, if \(x = -2\), then \(2x+4 = 2 \cdot(-2) + 4 = -4 + 4 = 0\), which is the same whether it is negative or positive, so you could make those inequalities into "or equal to" inequalities and they would still be true.

In all, we can rewrite the given equation without any absolute value signs at all provided we worry about where \(x\) is. In particular, we have to keep track of whether \(x \leq -2\) or not and whether \(x \leq 0\) or not. In all, then, we need to rewrite the equation differently depending on whether \(x\) falls into the following 3 cases: \[ x \leq -2; \quad \quad -2 \lt x \leq 0; \quad \quad 0 \lt x \] Put another way, we need to partition the real line in the following way: \[ (-\infty, \infty) = (-\infty, -2] \cup (-2, 0] \cup (0, \infty) \] Do you recall interval notation? Do you recall the meaning of the union symbol? If not, then follow this link to review. I will be using interval notation moving forward to express both the cases that I will be breaking the analysis up into and to express my final answer. The answer checker on the exercise pages will also require the use of interval notation and, more broadly, set notation—which is also explained in the section I just linked to above.

Case: \(x\) is in \((-\infty, -2]\):

In this case, \(2x+4\) is negative, as just explained, so \(|2x + 4| = -2x - 4\). Also, since \(-2 \lt 0\), we have that \(x \lt 0\), so \(|x| = -x\). Thus, we can rewrite our equation after making those substitutions and then solve. \[ 0 = 9 x + 7 \left|{x}\right| + \left|{2 x + 4}\right| + 4\\ \] becomes... \begin{align*} & 0 = 9 x - 7 x - 2x - 4 + 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 0 = 0 \\ \end{align*} What are we to do with that? What does this mean? Well, as noted in the section on solving for \(x\), strictly speaking, "\(0=0\)" tells us nothing. The important observation comes out of reversing the steps. You can narrate what I mean by saying, "Consider any \(x\) that falls into the interval \((-\infty, -2]\)." Isn't it true that for such an \(x\), \[ 0 = 9 x - 7 x - 2x - 4 + 4 \] Of course! But we also know that the right hand side of this equation equals \[ 9x + 7|x| + |2x+4| + 4 \] for \(x\) in that interval. Thus, since we were considering an arbitrary \(x\) in that interval, it must be that each and every \(x\) in that intervals satisfies the given equation. That is, the interval \[ (-\infty, -2] \] is part of our solution set! What remains to find out is if there are any solutions on the rest of the real line. So, we have to continue considering our cases.

Case: \(x\) is in \((-2, 0]\):

In this case, \(x \gt -2\), so \(2x+4\gt0\), so \(|2x+4| = 2x+4\). On the other hand, \(x \leq 0\) means that \(|x| = -x\). So we make these substitutions and then solve. \[ 0 = 9 x + 7 \left|{x}\right| + \left|{2 x + 4}\right| + 4\\ \] becomes... \begin{align*} & 0 = 9 x - 7 x + 2x + 4 + 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 0 = 4x + 8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 4x = -8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{-8}{4} = -2 \end{align*} Great! So we got another solution! Or did we? No, in fact not. After all, we had already noticed that \(-2\) is a solution since it falls in the previous case. It is, in fact, on the border of these two cases.

Case: \(x\) is in \((0, \infty)\):

In this case, \(x \gt 0 \gt -2\), so \(2x+4\gt0\). Therefore, \(|2x+4| = 2x+4\). Also, \(|x| = x\). So we make these substitutions and then solve. \[ 0 = 9 x + 7 \left|{x}\right| + \left|{2 x + 4}\right| + 4\\ \] becomes... \begin{align*} & 0 = 9 x + 7 x + 2x + 4 + 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 0 = 18x + 8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 18x = -8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{-8}{18} = -\frac{4}{9} \end{align*} Is this a solution? Well, note that to have made progress to this possible solution, we were operating under the assumption that \(x\) falls into the interval \((0, \infty)\). The value \(x = -\frac{4}{9}\) does not fall into that interval, so we actually have a contradiction. So, this is not an actual solution!

Since our cases exhaust the entire real line, we can conclude that our solution set is \[ (-\infty, -2] \]

Solve the equation. (That is, give a simple description of the set of all real numbers \(x\) that satisfy the given equation.) \[ 2 x + 11 = \left|{3 x - 6}\right| + \left|{5 x + 5}\right| \]

Answer:

As demonstrated in the examples above, I will consider by cases. You better look at the previous examples if you want more detail! The cases involve partititioning the real line into parts, which are determined by the points at which the term inside each absolute value expression would equal zero. These points are important because they are those which the term changes from negative to positive or vice versa. (See above for more explanation.) I know that \(3x - 6\) will change from negative to positive at \(x = 2\). That's because \begin{align*} & 3x - 6 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x = 6 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 2 \end{align*} Similarly, I know that \(5x + 5\) will change from negative to positive at \(x = -1\) because that is the solution when I set it equal to 0. So, the cases I need to consider are represented by the following partition of the real line: \[ (-\infty, -1] \cup (-1, 2] \cup (2, \infty) \] Actually, to streamline my process, I choose to pull the absolute value terms over to the left side and then reorder them according to the place where they change from negative to positive. This looks like so: \[ \left|{5 x + 5}\right| + \left|{3 x - 6}\right| = 2x + 11 \] Now, because of the way I have written these absolute value terms, so that the expression inside each has a positive coefficient on \(x\), I know that my first case of \(x\) in \((-\infty, -1]\) will mean that each of the absolute value terms evaluates to the negative of the number inside—because each of the expressions inside the absolute value terms would evaluate to a negative number for \(x\) in this region. So, for my first case, I just drop the absolute value signs and instead negate the expression inside. But, then, because of the way I ordered the terms, each successive case I consider will be equivalent to removing that negative sign one at a time. To show you what I mean, I will use a black \(-\) and a green \(+\) to highlight the successive changes as I move through the cases.

Case: \(x\) is in \((-\infty, -1]\):

\begin{align*} & \left|{5 x + 5}\right| + \left|{3 x - 6}\right| = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & {\color{black}-}\left(5 x + 5\right) \mathbin{\color{black}-} \left({3 x - 6}\right) = 2x + 11 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & -5x - 5 - 3x + 6 = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -8x + 1 = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -10x = 10 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -1 \end{align*} The value \(x = -1\) is on the border of the region considered in this case, but it's still in! So, this is part of our solution set. (One could also check this by plugging \(x = -1\) into the initial, given equation.)

Case: \(x\) is in \((-1, 2]\):

\begin{align*} & \left|{5 x + 5}\right| + \left|{3 x - 6}\right| = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & {\color{green}+}\left(5 x + 5\right) \mathbin{\color{black}-} \left({3 x - 6}\right) = 2x + 11 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5x + 5 - 3x + 6 = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x + 11 = 2x + 11\\ \end{align*} This is an indication that all \(x\) within the region considered in this case will be part of the solution set. This can be confirmed formally by thinking through the process of "reversing" the solving steps we just undertook. (Basically, you can just say to yourself, "Take any \(x\) in the in the interval \((-1, 2]\). Certainly it is true that \[ 2x + 11 = 2x + 11 \] But then it is also true that \[ 5x + 5 - 3x + 6 = 2x + 11 \] Finally, since for any \(x\) in the interval considered in this case, we have that \[ 5x + 5 - 3x + 6 = (5x + 5) - (3x - 6) = |5x+5| + |3x - 6| \] it follows that every \(x\) in this interval solves the given equation.) Therefore, so far we now that our solution set includes \[ [-1, 2] \]

Case: \(x\) is in \((2, \infty)\):

\begin{align*} & \left|{5 x + 5}\right| + \left|{3 x - 6}\right| = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & {\color{green}+}\left(5 x + 5\right) \mathbin{\color{green}+} \left({3 x - 6}\right) = 2x + 11 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5x + 5 + 3x - 6 = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 8x - 1 = 2x + 11\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 6x = 12 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 2 \end{align*} This is not a new solution, as it fell under the earlier case.

Since our cases exhaust the entire real line, we can conclude that our solution set is \[ [-1, 2] \]

Solve the equation. (That is, give a simple description of the set of all real numbers \(x\) that satisfy the given equation.) \[ 0 = 8 x + \left|{x + 3}\right| - \left|{9 x + 7}\right| + 4 \]

Answer:

As demonstrated in the examples above, I will consider by cases. You better look at the previous examples if you want more detail! The cases involve partititioning the real line into parts, which are determined by the points at which the term inside each absolute value expression would equal zero. These points are important because they are those which the term changes from negative to positive or vice versa. (See above for more explanation.) I know that \(9 x + 7\) will change from negative to positive at \(x = -\frac{7}{9}\). That's because \begin{align*} & 9 x + 7 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x = -7 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -\frac{7}{9} \end{align*} Similarly, I know that \(x + 3\) will change from negative to positive at \(x = -3\) because that is the solution when I set it equal to 0. So, the cases I need to consider are represented by the following partition of the real line: \[ (-\infty, -3] \cup \left(-3, -\frac{7}{9}\right] \cup \left(-\frac{7}{9}, \infty\right) \] Actually, to streamline my process, I choose to pull the absolute value terms over to the left side and then reorder them according to the place where they change from negative to positive. This looks like so: \[ - \left|{x + 3}\right| + \left|{9 x + 7}\right| = 8 x + 4 \] Now, because of the way I have written these absolute value terms, so that the expression inside each has a positive coefficient on \(x\), I know that my first case of \(x\) in \((-\infty, -3]\) will mean that each of the absolute value terms evaluates to the negative of the number inside—because each of the expressions inside the absolute value terms would evaluate to a negative number for \(x\) in this region. So, for my first case, I just drop the absolute value signs and instead negate the expression inside. But, then, because of the way I ordered the terms, each successive case I consider will be equivalent to removing that negative sign one at a time. To show you what I mean, I will use a black \(-\) and a green \(+\) to highlight the successive changes as I move through the cases.

Case: \(x\) is in \((-\infty, -3]\):

\begin{align*} & - \left|{x + 3}\right| + \left|{9 x + 7}\right| = 8 x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -\left({\color{black}-}\left(x + 3\right)\right) \mathbin{\color{black}-} \left({9x + 7}\right) = 8x + 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 3 - 9x - 7 = 8x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -8x - 4 = 8x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -16x = 8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -\frac{8}{16} = - \frac{1}{2} \end{align*} The value \(x = - \frac{1}{2}\) is not in the region considered in this case, so we cannot yet conclude that it is a solution (it doesn't seem like a solution from this case, but maybe it will turn out to be a solution in a later case.)

Case: \(x\) is in \(\left(-3, -\frac{7}{9}\right]\):

\begin{align*} & - \left|{x + 3}\right| + \left|{9 x + 7}\right| = 8 x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -\left({\color{green}+}\left(x + 3\right)\right) \mathbin{\color{black}-} \left({9x + 7}\right) = 8x + 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & -x - 3 - 9x - 7 = 8x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -10x - 10 = 8x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -18x = 14 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -\frac{14}{18} = - \frac{7}{9} \end{align*} The value \(x = - \frac{7}{9}\) is on the border of the region considered in thie case, but it is in all the same! So, it is part of the solution set.

Case: \(x\) is in \(\left(-\frac{7}{9}, \infty\right)\):

\begin{align*} & - \left|{x + 3}\right| + \left|{9 x + 7}\right| = 8 x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -\left({\color{green}+}\left(x + 3\right)\right) \mathbin{\color{green}+} \left({9x + 7}\right) = 8x + 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & -x - 3 + 9x + 7 = 8x + 4\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 8x + 4 = 8x + 4\\ \end{align*} This is an indication that all \(x\) within the region considered in this case will be part of the solution set. Certainly it is true that \[ 8x + 4 = 8x + 4 \] But then it is also true that \[ -x - 3 + 9x + 7 = 8x + 4 \] Finally, since for any \(x\) in the interval considered in this case, we have that \[ -x - 3 + 9x + 7 = 8x + 4 = -(x+3) + (9x + 7) = -|x+3| + |9x + 7| \] it follows that every \(x\) in this interval solves the given equation.) Therefore, we now know that our solution set includes \[ \left(-\frac{7}{9}, \infty\right) \] Actually, included in the former case (and, really, we could have included it in this case as well), we had that \(-\frac{7}{9}\) is also part of the solution set. So, we know at this point that \[ \left[-\frac{7}{9}, \infty\right) \] is included in our solution set. And, since our cases have now covered the entire real line, we know this is the entire solution set.
