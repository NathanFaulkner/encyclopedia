This section concerns a skill of sort of "intermediate" importance—that is, we will mostly just use it as a step to something more useful.

Completely factor the following. \[ x^3 - 3x^2 - 4x + 12 \]

Answer:

Allow me to demonstrate the (new) technique of "factoring by grouping." Basically, observe that the first two terms have a factor in common. Factor out the GCF! \[ x^3 - 3x^2 = x^2(x-3) \] Now, the trick will be to get that second factor—that is, \(x-3\)—to be a factor of the sum of the second two terms (grouped.) But, you'll have to factor out a negative to make it happen! So, here is what it will look like: \[ x^3 - 3x^2 - 4 x + 12 = x^2(x-3) - 4(x-3) \] See it? Now, to "finish the job," you make the observation that \((x-3)\) is a common factor of both \(x^2(x-3)\) and \(4(x-3)\). Factor it out. \[ x^2(x-3) - 4(x-3) = (x^2-4)(x-3) \] Now, it is "factored"—but mind the directions here! It says to "completely factor." What does that mean? It means to first write it as a product (check!) But it also means to scrutinize any of the factors to see if they could be broken down into yet simpler factors. Since \(x^2-4 = (x-2)(x+2)\), we have one final step to the final answer, which is \[ (x-2)(x+2)(x-3) \]

Here are a couple more examples without all of the narrative.

Completely factor the following. \[ x^3 + 5x^2 + 6x + 30 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} x^3 + 5x^2 + 6x + 30 & = x^2(x+5) + 6(x+5) \\ & = (x^2+6)(x+5) \end{align*} Since we don't have any tools for factoring \(x^2+6\) we can stop there.

Completely factor the following. \[ 4x^3 - 6x^2 + 6x - 9 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} 4x^3 - 6x^2 + 6x - 9 &= 2x^2(2x-3) +3(2x-3)\\ &= (2x^2+3)(2x-3) \end{align*} Since we don't know how to factor \(2x^2+3\) we'll stop there.
