In the last section, we analyzed from an algebraic or numerical perspective what goes on with the \(y\)-values of a polynomial function when \(x\) gets large in either the positive or negative direction. This concerns what the graph of the function will look like. In this section, we will learn about more predictable features of the graph. Then we will combine all these features into one rough "sketch" of the graph. Again, by calling it a "sketch" we are warning people that our pictures won't be exact and may contain some inaccuracies—btu that they will be failthful at least to the skills that we are building in these two sections.

Specifically, in this section, we will contemplate the zeroes of the polynomial and what happens to the graph there. Well, for starters, a "zero" of a polynomial is just another word for an \(x\)-intercept—and you already know what that means: the graph will touch the \(x\)-axis there. But, the interesting question will be whether the function crosses the \(x\)-axis at that point or "bounces."

Provide a rough sketch of the following polynomial. \[ f(x) = (x+1)^2(x-3)(x-4) \]

Answer:

Even though in practice you will only draw the one graph, I have drawn my answer over multiple windows so you can see my process of solving. Note that the \(x\)-intercepts of the graph are \(x=-1\), \(3\), and \(4\). So, we could plot those as dots, as I did below.
Next, we'll check the end behavior—which tells us how the graph should exit the viewing window. To do so, you need to know about the leading term of the polynomial—meaning, the term with the highest power. Remember that that highest power is called the degree. To figure out what the leading term is, imagine multiplying the original, factored formula all out—but you only need to worry about the leading term: \begin{align*} f(x) &= (x+1)^2(x-3)(x-4) \\ &= (\underline x+1)(\underline x+1)(\underline x-3)(\underline x-4)\\ &=xxxx + \cdots \\ &= x^4 + \cdots \end{align*} So, the leading coefficient is \(x^4\). That means the end behavior is as follows:
  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
This means that the \(y\)-values should get larger for \(x\)-values far out to the left and right, so I have part of the graph extending upwards at the left and right edges.
Next, we know that the graph has to continue down from that left side on its way to meet the first zero, which I have plotted here. But now we have something new to consider; while we know the graph has to touch zero there, there is a question: Does the graph cross through the \(x\)-axis there or does it "bounce"?
To answer that last question, look at the original formula for the function: \[ f(x) = (x+1)^2(x-3)(x-4) \] Contemplate with me the meaning of the zeroes: They are where the graph touches the \(x\)-axis—the only places it does so. That means that the \(y\)-values coming out from this function can only change from negative to positive as you pass by another zero. The question of whether or not the graph punches through the \(x\)-axis at a zero or "bounces" off of it is the same as the question of whether the \(y\)-values can change from positive to negative at that zero. We are considering here the zero \(x=-1\), which concerns the factor \((x+1)^2\). Note that the sign (\(+/-\)) of the other factors \((x-3)\) and \((x-4)\) does not change as \(x\) changes from just below \(-1\) to just above it: \((x-3)\) and \((x-4)\) will be negative for all \(x\)-values near \(-1\). But, also, while \(x+1\) changes from negative to positive as \(x\) changes from below \(-1\) to above \(-1\), \((x+1)^2\) does not change sign, because it is a square—which is always positive. So, \(y\) doesn't change sign there and the graph bounces. The moral: a graph will bounce at any zero associated with a factor raised to an even power. \((x+1)^2\) is a factor raised to the second power, which has to come out positive no matter what, so the \(y\)'s won't change sign near \(-1\) and the graph bounces. On the other hand, at any zero associated with a factor raised to an odd power—such as \((x-3)\), which is raised to power \(1\) or, say, \((x-7)^3\), which is rasied to power \(3\)—the graph will "punch through." So, plot the graph bouncing at \(x=-1\), but then note that it will have to turn and come back down at some point to hit the next zero at \(x=3\).
Noting the discussion about "bouncing" versus "punching through" just above, the graph will punch through at \(x=3\), and then will have to turn back up to reach the zero at \(x=4\).
Again, the graph punches through at \(x=4\) (because the associated factor \((x-4)\) is of odd degree, namely degree \(1\)), which concludes our analysis:

Here is a synopsis of an important point from the last example:
"Punch Through" or "Bounce?": A graph will "punch through" the \(x\)-axis at any zeroes associated with a factor of odd degree but will "bounce" at any zeroes associated with factor of even degree. For example, the graph of \[ f(x) = (x+5)^3(x+1)^2(x-3)^4(x-4) \] will "bounce" at \(x=-2\) and \(x=3\) but will "punch through" at \(x=-5\) and \(x=4\).

Provide a rough sketch of the following polynomial. \[ f(x) =-\frac{1}{3} (x+2)(x-3)^2 \]

Answer:

    Work flow:

  1. Plot the zeroes, \(-2\) and \(3\) as dots.
  2. Check the end behavior, by first figuring out the degree and leading coefficient. The leading term is found as follows: \begin{align*} f(x) &=-\frac{1}{3} (x+2)(x-3)^2 \\ &=-\frac{1}{3} (\underline x+2)(\underline x-3)(\underline x-3)\\ &= -\frac{1}{3} xxx + \cdots \\ &= -\frac{1}{3} x^3 + \cdots \end{align*}
    3. So, the degree is \(3\) and the leading coefficient is negative. Therefore:
    • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
    • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
  3. Plot little pieces of the graph at the edges—to indicate how the graph should exit the window.
  4. Decide whether the graph bounces or punches through at each zero.
