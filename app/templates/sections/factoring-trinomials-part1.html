We now turn to factoring. Let's recall the basic meaning of the word "factor" with a warm-up.

Completely factor the expression. \[ 3x^2 - 12x \]

Answer:

"Factoring" is simply reversing the distributive property. It could be called "undistributing." What we are looking for is any common factors that could be moved "out front" (or behind) in a manner such that, if they were distributed back in, the result would be the given expression. Like this: \[ 3x^2 - 12x = 3x(x - 4) \] What have I done here? I have done two things: I have first observed that both terms have a factor of \(3\) and second that both terms have a factor of \(x\). So, I pulled "out" \(3x\). Notice that if I distributed the \(3x\) back in, I would indeed get the expression I started with.

That's factoring! The point of it is to take an expression involving a sum of terms and then rewrite it as a product of terms. What's the point of that, you may ask? We shall see in later sections. Let's do one more warm-up.

Completely factor the expression. Also, factor out any negative leading coefficients. \[ -3x^2 + 12x \]

Answer:

Mind the specific request to factor out any negative leading coefficients! What does that mean? A leading coefficient is the number (coefficient) on the term with the highest power; this term is called the leading term. The leading term in this case is \(-3x^2\). Of course, it does indeed have a negative coefficient, so let's factor it out. What's the point of doing so? Again, this will become apparent in a later section. For now, treat it as just something arbitrary I am asking of you. Here is what I got: \[ -3x(x - 4) \] Notice that I had to "leave behind" a negative on the \(4\)! Why? Again, you have to imagine distributing the term that you factor out back inside the parentheses. I would need a negative on the \(4\) waiting for the negative on the \(3\) to create a positive \(12\). Make sense?

Here's a few more without any talk:

Factor each of the following. Also, factor out any negative leading coefficient.
  1. \(4x^2 + 16x\)

    Answer:

    \[ 4x^2 + 16x = 4x(x + 4) \]
  2. \(-3x^2 - 24\)

    Answer:

    \[ -3x^2 - 24 = -3(x^2 + 8) \]
  3. \(36x - 54\)

    Answer:

    \[ 36x - 54 = 18(2x - 3) \]
  4. \(-5x^2 + 25x\)

    Answer:

    \[ -5x^2 + 25x = -5x(x - 5) \]

Now, what gets a little more complicated is when you have to run in reverse two distributions which have been done in succession—and even combined with other steps. What I am talking about is distribution of a form that you may have already practiced in the section entitled Warm-up with Quadratics. Here are a few more examples of these warm-up problems, which, you will do well to note, involve the process that we are going to figure out how to reverse in the examples that follow.

(Warm-up)

Multiply everything out and combine like terms.
  1. \((x-3)(x+4)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} (x-3)(x+4) &= (x - 3)x + (x - 3)4 & \textrm{Distributed } (x-3)\\ &= x^2 - 3x + 4x - 12 & \textrm{Distributed \(x\) and \(4\)}\\ &= x^2 + x - 12 & \textrm{Combined like terms} \end{align*}

    In the section, Warm-up with Quadratics, we already noted a way to skip some of those steps. You may want to review that now. In what follows, we will be reversing this process. That is, imagine being given the end result of the computation we just undertook and trying to "reverse engineer" the factored form that we just started with.

  2. \((x-6)(x-7)\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} (x-6)(x-7) &= (x - 6)x - (x - 6)7 & \textrm{Distributed } (x-6)\\ &= x^2 - 6x - 7x + 42 & \textrm{Distributed things}\\ &= x^2 - 13x + 42 & \textrm{Combined like terms} \end{align*}

Now, we turn to the main tast: factoring trinomials. Note that a trinomial is just a formula that involves the sum of three terms. (Terms are made of symbols, which many logicians call "names"; the word fragment "nom" means "name"; so there you have it.) That is, our goal will be to take something of the form \[ x^2 + {\color{blue} b}x + {\color{orange}c} \] and write it in the form \[ (x+{\color{green}p})(x+{\color{purple}q}), \] where \({\color{blue} b}\) and \({\color{orange}c}\) are given numbers but \({\color{green}p}\) and \({\color{purple}q}\) are what you have to figure out. This general example is very instructive. Imagine what it would take for the factored form to multiply out to give the original. You would need that \(x^2 + {\color{blue} b}x + {\color{orange}c}\) would equal \begin{align*} (x+{\color{green}p})(x+{\color{purple}q}) &= (x+{\color{green}p})x + (x+{\color{green}p}){\color{purple}q}\\ &= x^2 + {\color{green}p}x + {\color{purple}q}x + {\color{green}p}{\color{purple}q}\\ &= x^2 + ({\color{green}p} + {\color{purple}q})x + {\color{green}p}{\color{purple}q} \end{align*} So, in all, if it's true that \[ x^2 + {\color{blue} b}x + {\color{orange}c} = (x+{\color{green}p})(x+{\color{purple}q}) \] then you also have to have that \[ x^2 + {\color{blue} b}x + {\color{orange}c} = x^2 + ({\color{green}p} + {\color{purple}q})x + {\color{green}p}{\color{purple}q} \] Equating the coeffients, you can see that all of this will work out if you can find \({\color{green}p}\) and \({\color{purple}q}\) so that \[ {\color{blue} b} = {\color{green}p} + {\color{purple}q} \] and \[ {\color{orange}c} = {\color{green}p}{\color{purple}q} \] Put another way, based on your given of \({\color{blue} b}\) and \({\color{orange}c}\), you have to figure out two numbers \({\color{green}p}\) and \({\color{purple}q}\) that multiply together to give you \({\color{orange}c}\) and add together to give you \({\color{blue} b}\). Time for some examples!

Factor. \[ x^2 - 6x - 16 \]

Answer:

As explained in the paragraph just above, I need two numbers that multiply together to give me \(-16\) and add together to give me \(-6\). Those numbers are \(-8\) and \(2\). Therefore, \[ x^2 - 6x - 16 = (x-8)(x+2) \] You should always check your work of factoring. The question is, if you multiplied everything back out and combined resulting like terms would you go back to the given? You can mentally check this provided you make an observation about how to quickly multiply polynomials together, which was made in the section on Warm-up with Quadratics: Every term in the first parentheses will hit every term in the second—but just once. Mentally, I note this by starting with the first term of \(x\) in \(x - 8\), noting that it will multiply the terms in the second parentheses to yield \[ x^2 + 2x \] and then I proceed with the next term in the first set of parentheses, namely, \(-8\), noting that it will multiply with the terms in the second set of parentheses to give me \[ -8x - 16 \] I then mentally check off that this produces the \(x^2\) and \(-16\) of the given and that also the \(-6x\) is produced after you combine the like terms of \(2x\) and \(-8x\). Anyway, that's a rough description of what I would do to mentally verify my answer!

There is a type of question that can occur of this type that might throw you off, namely, where the middle term is missing (or, if you like, is \(0\).) We should take a look at one of those.

Factor. \[ x^2 - 49 \]

Answer:

You can view this binomial as a "trinomial" of the form \[ x^2 + 0x - 49 \] Now, I can see how it fits the pattern of this section. As explained above, I need two numbers that multiply together to give me \(-49\) and add together to give me \(0\). Those numbers are \(-7\) and \(7\). Therefore, \[ x^2 - 49 = (x-7)(x+7) \] You should always check your work of factoring. The question is, if you multiplied everything back out and combined resulting like terms would it go back to the given? You can mentally check this provided you make an observation about how to quickly multiply polynomials together, which was made in the section on Warm-up with Quadratics: Every term in the first parentheses will hit every term in the second—but just once. Mentally, I note this by starting with the first term of \(x\) coming from \(x - 7\), noting that it will multiply the terms in the second parentheses to yield \[ x^2 + 7x \] and then I proceed with the next term in the first set of parentheses, namely, \(-7\), noting that it will multiply with the terms in the second set of parentheses to give me \[ -7x - 49 \] I then mentally check off that this produces the \(x^2\) and \(-49\) of the given and that also you get \(0x\) after you combine the like terms of \(-7x\) and \(7x\).
