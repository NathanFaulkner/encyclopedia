In this first part, we will contemplate what's called the "end behavior" of the function, which is maybe a funny way of saying this: What do the \(y\)-values do when you put in very large \(x\)-values—positive or negative. In fact, there are only two possibilities—but let's a consider a specific example before saying anything further. Per usual, this first example will contain a lot of explanation, not all of which needs to appear in your answers (rather, it represents what might be going on in your head while your figure out what the answer is.)

Fill in the blanks to describe the end behavior of the function \[ f(x) = x^3 + 3x + 4 \]
  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow\) mmmmmmmmmmmmm
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow\) mmmmmmmmmmmmmm

Answer:

This problem asks you to think about what happens to the \(y\)-values (represented here by the symbol \(f(x)\)) when you plug in very large \(x\)-values— both very large negative and positive values—and, really, to say what the basic trend is. For instance, if you were to plug in one billion for \(x\), i.e., \(x=1,000,000,000 = 10^9\), what happens to \(f(x)\)? While you definitely don't need to actually write all this out, it would look like this: \begin{align*} f\left(10^9\right) &= \left(10^9\right)^3 + 3 \cdot 10^9 + 4\\ & = 10^{27} + 3 \cdot 10^9 + 4 \\ & = 1,000,000,000,000,000,003,000,000,004 \end{align*} Now, that big long answer isn't quite to the point. Instead, we need to contemplate "the trend." The trend is sufficiently understood by considering a rounded version of this number. Note that, when rounding, you might as well neglect the '3' and the '4', which are 9 and 18 decimal points away from the leading digit of '1', respectively! What I mean is that this would round to \(1,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000\). All that to say: the trend is that when you plug in a very large number the only thing that matters is the largest power. The others will round away. We can now answer the second bullet as
As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
It means that "As \(x\) gets really large in the positive direction, \(y\) does, too."

Now, to figure out the first bullet, think what happens when you plug in a very large but negative number: Again, you can make the argument that the only thing that matters is the largest power—that \(x^3\)—with the other terms "rounding away" when you plug in big numbers. So, the only thing you need to note is that if you cube a big negative number, it comes out negative—and that it comes out very large (but negative)! So the answer to the first bullet is

As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)

Next, let's look at a quicker example. After that, I will give you a synopsis of all possible examples.

Fill in the blanks to describe the end behavior of the function \[ f(x) = -2x^4 + 3x^2 - x + 9 \]
  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow\) mmmmmmmmmmmmm
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow\) mmmmmmmmmmmmm

Answer:

  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
Quick explanation: As \(x\) gets really large in the positive direction, \(-2x^4\) gets very large, too, but will be negative—and the other terms would "round away", representing much smaller numbers. Also, if you raise a negative number to the fourth power, it comes out positive. So, whichever direction you go in, really large (negative or positive) \(x\)-values lead to really large negative \(y\)-values for this function.

Possible End Behaviors for Polynomials

For \(f(x) = ax^n + \cdots\)

Case 1: \(n\) is even, \(a\) is positive

  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)

Case 2: \(n\) is even, \(a\) is negative

  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)

Case 3: \(n\) is odd, \(a\) is positive

  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)

Case 4: \(n\) is odd, \(a\) is negative

  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
  • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)

Explain how each of the cases work in the synopsis just above.

Answer:

  1. Case 1: \(n\) is even, \(a\) is positive

    With an even power, \(n\), it doesn't matter if you plug in a (large) negative or a positive value for \(x\); in either case \(x^n\) would come out positive—that is, because if you multiply an odd number by itself an even number of times it comes out positive, does it not? Next, since the coefficient \(a\) is positive the term \(ax^n\) would come out positive. For very large (positive or negative) values, the other terms (which are not shown here, rather only indicated by the ellipsis \(\cdots\)) could be rounded away, since the value coming from the leading term, \(ax^n\), would be so much larger. Thus, for this case, you get
    • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
    • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)

  2. Case 2: \(n\) is even, \(a\) is negative

    With an even power, \(n\), it doesn't matter if you plug in a (large) negative or a positive value for \(x\); in either case \(x^n\) would come out positive—that is, because if you multiply an odd number by itself an even number of times it comes out positive, does it not? Next, since the coefficient \(a\) is negative the term \(ax^n\) would come out negative. For very large (positive or negative) values, the other terms (which are not shown here, rather only indicated by the ellipsis \(\cdots\)) could be rounded away, since the value coming from the leading term, \(ax^n\), would be so much larger. Thus, for this case, you get
    • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
    • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)

  3. Case 3: \(n\) is odd, \(a\) is positive

    With an odd power, \(n\), if you plug in a negative value into the expression \(x^n\) you get a negative value (a number times itself an odd number of times comes out negative) whereas if you plug in a positive number, \(x^n\) comes out positive. Next, since the coefficient \(a\) is positive the term \(ax^n\) would come out positive when \(x^n\) is positive and negative when \(x^n\) is negative. For very large (positive or negative) values, the other terms (which are not shown here, rather only indicated by the ellipsis \(\cdots\)) could be rounded away, since the value coming from the leading term, \(ax^n\), would be so much larger. Thus, for this case, you get
    • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
    • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)

  4. Case 4: \(n\) is odd, \(a\) is negative

    With an odd power, \(n\), if you plug in a negative value into the expression \(x^n\) you get a negative value (a number times itself an odd number of times comes out negative) whereas if you plug in a positive number, \(x^n\) comes out positive. Next, since the coefficient \(a\) is negative the term \(ax^n\) would come out negative when \(x^n\) is positive and positive when \(x^n\) is negative— the sign would switch! For very large (positive or negative) values, the other terms (which are not shown here, rather only indicated by the ellipsis \(\cdots\)) could be rounded away, since the value coming from the leading term, \(ax^n\), would be so much larger. Thus, for this case, you get
    • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\)
    • As \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\)
