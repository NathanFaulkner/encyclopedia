We start off with a brief review of something that should be well within your comfort zone: "solving for \(x\)". But, don't worry, we'll be heading to some new items soon!

You may not need to hear much instruction about this topic matter, but I think it's a great opportunity to talk about something which is essential in math---even if it is a harder thing to "test" or grade: the meaning of it all. I would love to hear from you in response to such questions as "What does \(x\) represent?" and "What are you actually doing---in the sense of what does it mean or represent---when you are solving for \(x\)?" Of course, you might try to describe the process or procedures you undertake when you solve. That might be very helpful, too, but it isn't actually what I mean here. I mean to contemplate the meaning. Think of this way: Math consists foremost in a bunch of symbols, strung together according to certain rules. But, as always, symbols have a meaning. The letters on this page are symbols, and strung together according to certain rules (and just what are the rules of language??) they have a meaning. They give rise to a meaning in your mind as you read them. That meaning might be a little different for each person and is dependent on so many different, complicated things, such as how exactly you view me, the writer, even while you may have just only barely gotten to know me. In the same way, mathematical symbols are intended to have a certain meaning. Actually, the meaning of the symbols is a much stricter thing than the meaning of words, but, nevertheless, the way those meanings manifest themselves in a different peoples' minds can vary considerably. They may evoke different images and different associations for each person.

Allow me to continue this discussion as I demo a (probably easy) problem.

Imagine that you are cracking open your math textbook for the first time. Here goes problem #1:
Solve for \(x\): \[ 3x - 7 = 8 \]
  1. What does this all mean?

    Answer:

    The math textbook prompt is actually---let's be critical here!---a shorthand for something that might be more clearly understood if it were just spelled out for the reader---something that would go like this:
    Suppose that \(x\) is a real number (so, could be plotted on the real line) with the property that if you subtract 7 from 3 times that number it comes out to be 8. Your job is to find the identity of that number---if it even exists!
    Of course, the number does exist in this case, and it isn't hard to find. Still, let's go ahead and do so.
  2. Go ahead and solve for \(x\).

    Answer:

    I'm still in the mood for talking a lot, so, in this case, I am going to include descriptions of what I am doing at each step. \begin{align*} & 3x-7= 8 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x = 15 & \textrm{I added 7 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 5 & \textrm{I divided both sides by 3.}\\ \end{align*} Now, to be clear, I don't plan on asking you to narrate your problems in this way (at least not for a grade). On the other hand, I'm not entirely satisfied with the partial narration that I just gave! If our task is to contemplate the meaning of it all (and that is indeed my primary motive with this easy problem), then I'll have to be even wordier. It goes like this:
    If \(x\) is a real number, then \(3x-7\) is too. Indeed, we are given that \(3x-7\) is 8. Now, we note that \(8+7=15\) but also that adding 7 to \(3x-7\) takes you back to just \(3x\). That means that \(3x=15\). Of course, since 3 times 5 is 15 it must be that \(x\) is 5. Looking back, we see that the substitution of 5 in for \(x\) does indeed satisfy the original equation (it makes it true!)---that is, when \(x = 5\): \[ \textrm{LHS} = 3x - 7 = 3 \cdot 5 - 7 = 15 - 7 = 8 = \textrm{RHS}, \] where I have used the abbreviations "LHS" and "RHS" for the original left- and right-hand sides, respectively.

Now, before you jump to the exercises, you should know that I do expect you to do harder problems at this point. In fact, we'll have to look at 3 more examples to see three interesting cases in which this narrative can take us---and you really have to look at these more complicated examples to see the special cases emerge.

Solve for \(x\). \[ 9(x - 9) = 9x + 6(x-4) \]

Answer:

Now, I talk "strategy" or "procedure" a little bit. I know (and you know) from past experience and learning that the game to play here is to try to "isolate \(x\)". Note that that may also include "combining" like terms to try to get the number of occurrences of \(x\). Down to just one. For instance, the following equation would not quite be considered "solved" wven though, in one sense, it is "solved for \(x\)": \[ x = 3x - 12 \] The problem with this "solution" is that it has not served to answer the true goal, which, as we noted in the previous example, is really not explicity explained in the brief prompt "solve for \(x\)": the goal is to find the identity of the real number \(x\) that satisfies the given equation (that makes it true)---if that number actually even exists. We shall have more to say about this goal in the next couple examples, too! So, on this one, I am going to "collect like terms" and then "isolate \(x\)." To that end, the first step I'll undertake is to distribute all the places where a number is multiplied onto a binomial. \begin{align*} & 9(x - 9) = 9x + 6(x-4) & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 81 = 9x + 6x - 24 & \textrm{Distributed}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 81 = 15x - 24 & \textrm{Simplified a little}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x = 15x + 57 & \textrm{Added 81 to both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -6x = 57 & \textrm{Subtracted \(15x\) from both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{57}{-6} = -\frac{57}{6} & \textrm{Divided both sides by \(-6\)} \end{align*} Now, it is also prudent to check your answer. You could even use a calculator to do this. You would type
9(-57/6 - 9)
which yields -166.5. That's the value of the left-hand side when our answer is substituted in. Next, we plug in the right-hand side and hope it comes out the same:
9*-57/6 + 6(-57/6 - 4))
which yields -166.5! So, we found the solution.

Solve for \(x\). (Find the solution set.) \[ 9(x - 9) = 3x + 6(x-4) \]

Answer:

Something different happens on this one!! It provides a good chance to go back and contemplate the secret "narrative" that lies behind your typical textbook problems and their procedural solution. \begin{align*} & 9(x - 9) = 3x + 6(x-4) & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 81 = 3x + 6x - 24 & \textrm{Distributed}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 81 = 9x - 24 & \textrm{Simplified a little}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x = 9x + 57 & \textrm{Added 81 to both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 0 = 57 & \textrm{Subtracted \(9x\) from both sides}\\ \end{align*} Now what? Well, you might already know the answer to this as well: It means that there is not solution; there is no real number with that satisfies the original equation. So, what happens from the point-of-view of "narrative" or "meaning"? The narration would go something like this:
Suppose that \(x\) is a real number (you can plot it on the real line) with the property that \[ 9(x - 9) = 3x + 6(x-4). \] (In English, this means that 9 times 9 less than the number is the same as 3 times the number added to the result of 6 times 4 less than the number.) From there, step-by-step (see the steps above, which are little painful to reproduce in English), we learn that "\(0 = 57\)". Of course, 0 doesn't equal 57, so, assuming we didn't make a mistake along the way, that means our original assumption was untenable: that is, there is no real number with property just described.
When this sort of thing happens, you can just record it as "no solution". Note the directions I put in parentheses in the prompt! Do you know what a "solution set" is? It is the set of all real numbers that satisfies a given statement (equation, inequality, or what-have-you). In this case, the solution is set is "empty". For completeness, mathematicians declare that there is such a thing as an "empty set". So, you could still say there is a solution (set) in this case: the solution set is the empty set. You are probably also familiar with the fact that mathematicians use the symbol ∅ for the empty set. This is actually a version of a letter from Danish and Norwegian alphabets, chosen by André Weil in 1939 for this purpose! (So, it isn't a very old symbol. For that matter, few of our math symbols are.)

Solve for \(x\). (Find the solution set.) \[ 18 \left(x + 3\right)=3 \left(x + 3\right) + 15 x + 45 \]

Answer:

We get some more variety on this one. \begin{align*} & 18 \left(x + 3\right)=3 \left(x + 3\right) + 15 x + 45 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 18x + 54 = 3x + 9 +15x + 45 & \textrm{Distributed}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 18x + 54 = 18x + 54 & \textrm{Simplified a little}\\ \end{align*} Now we could continue at this point, but what's point? Look at it this way, strictly speaking, we haven't learned anything from this result---I mean, I could have told you before we did anything that \(18x + 54\) equals \(18x + 54\). In fact, that's true no matter what \(x\) is. And, herein lies the central point. You may realize with me here that the solution set is "all real numbers"; that is, plugging in any real number for \(x\) will satisfy the equation (make it true). (Try it!) But, to really prove that that is the case, the thing to do is certainly not keep plugging in real numbers until you give up. Rather, the key to the logic here is that you can run those steps in reverse. The narrative goes like this:
Let \(x\) be any real number you like! Now, agree with me that \[ 18x + 54 = 18x + 54 \] From this (and sort of "desimplifying", if you like), you can deduce that \[ 18x + 54 = 3x + 9 +15x + 45 \] and finally that \[ 18 \left(x + 3\right)=3 \left(x + 3\right) + 15 x + 45 \] Since \(x\) was arbitrary at the first, we see that any \(x\) will do. Any real number "makes this equation true".
You can just report this as "all real numbers" or "all reals" and I will accept that you were talking about the solution set but didn't feel like using a lot of words. Of course, you might also be familiar with the mathematician's symbol for this, which is just an 'R', but written in a special font called "blackboard bold", which should be self-explanatory. Here it is: ℝ .
