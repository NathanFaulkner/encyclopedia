We now turn to a skill concerning parabolas which has some nice real-world applications. The basic observation in this section is that, depending on whether a parabola opens up or opens down, there will always be a maximum \(y\)-value attained (if it opens down) or a minimum \(y\)-value (if it opens up). Let's practice this skill in a "pure" setting (and with easier numbers) before we jump into the applications.

Figure out whether the function has a maximum or a minimum, and figure out what that (\(y\)) value is and where (on the \(x\)-axis) it attains that value. \[ f(x)=-x^2+2x-12 \]

Answer:

To answer this, I am going to think a little bit about what I know the graph will look like, but mostly I will be applying my "Alternative Method to Find Vertex Form, II" from the section on quadratics in standard form. Since the coefficient on \(x^2\) is negative, we know the parabola opens down; so, it will have a maximum (\(y\)) value. This max value is, of course, attained at the vertex. I will use the "short-cut" method for finding the coordinates of the vertex. The vertex is at \[ x = \frac{-b}{2a} = \frac{-2}{2(-1)} = 1 \] and \[ y = f(1) = -1^2 +2\cdot 1 - 12 = -1 + 2 - 12 = -11 \] So, its max value is \(=-11\), attained at \(x=1\).

Figure out whether the function has a maximum or a minimum, and figure out what that (\(y\)) value is and where (on the \(x\)-axis) it attains that value. \[ f(x)=\frac{1}{2}x^2-7x+19 \]

Answer:

To answer this, I am going to think a little bit about what I know the graph will look like, but mostly I will be applying my "Alternative Method to Find Vertex Form, II" from the section on quadratics in standard form. Since the coefficient on \(x^2\) is positive, we know the parabola opens upwards; so, it will have a minimum (\(y\)) value. This min value is, of course, attained at the vertex. I will use the "short-cut" method for finding the coordinates of the vertex. The vertex is at \[ x = \frac{-b}{2a} = \frac{-(-7)}{2\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)} = \frac{7}{1} = 7 \] and \begin{align*} y = f(7) &= \frac{1}{2}(7)^2 - 7\cdot 7 + 19\\ &= \frac{1}{2}\cdot 49 - 49 + 19 \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}\cdot 49 + 19\\ &= -24.5 + 19\\ &= -5.5 \end{align*} So, its min value is \(=-5.5\), attained at \(x=7\).

We now turn to some applications of this technique.

After rounding some intermediate calculations that are not displayed here, the vertical height \(y\) above the ground of a cannonball shot from an initial height of \(24\) feet with an initial velocity of \(644\) feet per second and initial launch angle (measured from the ground) of \(63^\circ\) can be given as a function of the horizontal distance \(x\) it has traveled from the launching point (if we neglect air resistance and the coriolis effect!) by \[ y(x) = - 0.00019 x^{2} + 1.963 x + 24 \] Find the maximum height \(y\) attained by the cannonball and the horizontal distance \(x\) from the launching point at which this max height is attained.

Answer:

Note that, if you graphed this, it would be an "upside-down" parabola, meaning that it will indeed have a max \(y\)-value! The maximum happens at the vertex, with the \(y\)-coordinate the max height attained and th \(x\)-coordinate representing the distance away from the launching point at which the maximum is attained. The vertex is at \(x = h\) and \(y = k\), where \begin{align*} h &= -\frac{b}{2a}\\ &= -\frac{1.963}{2 \cdot -0.00019} \\ &\approx 5165.79 \textrm{feet} \end{align*} (See the link in the answer to the previous example for details.) Next, I'll use this value to get the max height \(k\) attained at this point: \begin{align*} k &= y(h) \\ &= - 0.00019 (5165.79)^{2} + 1.963 (5165.79) + 24 \\ &\approx 5094.22 \textrm{ feet} \end{align*}

Say that it has been determined through market research that the number \(u\) of millions of yo-yos sold can be given as a function of the price \(x\) at which they are sold (in dollars less than $22.70) using the following formula: \[ u = 26.51 - 1.168 x \] Find the price \(x\) that maximizes the revenue.

Answer:

The first observation you need to make is that if \(u\) represents the number of units sold (in millions) and \(x\) represents the price—which is precisely the revenue brought in for each unit sold—then the total revenue brought in will be \[ r(x) = x\cdot u = x(26.51 - 1.168 x) = 26.51x - 1.168 x^2 \] in millions of dollars. So, all that remains is for us to try to maximize this function \(r\). This function is certainly quadratic, with \(a = -1.168\) and \(b = 26.51\), so we can use the same technique demonstrated in the examples above. After all since the coefficient on \(x^2\) is negative, its graph will be an "upside down" parabola, which means it will indeed have a max \(y\)-value. This max value is attained at the vertex, which has coordinates \((h, k)\) where \[ h = -\frac{b}{2a} \] and \[ k = r(h) \] (If you want an explanation of this, just follow the link given in the answer of the first example.) Thus, the maximum revenue is attained when the price is \begin{align*} x = -\frac{b}{2a} &= -\frac{26.51}{2 \cdot -1.168}\\ &\approx 11.35 \textrm{ dollars} \end{align*} That maximum revenue is \begin{align*} y &= r(h) \\ &\approx 26.51 \cdot 11.35 - 1.168 (11.35)^2 \\ &\approx 50.42 \textrm{ million dollars} \end{align*}
