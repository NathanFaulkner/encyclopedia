There are two "special cases" of standard form that are worth treating separately. Recall that standard form is: \begin{equation}\tag{Standard Form} ax + by = c \end{equation} The two special cases are (i) where \(a=0\) but \(b\neq 0\) and ( ii) where \(b=0\) but \(a\neq 0\). For instance, \(y= 5\) and \(x=-1\) are examples of the special cases I am talking about.

How do you graph the equation when it is given in standard form?

Consider the equation \(y=5\).

  1. List some pairs that satisfy this equation. Graph them on the \((x,y)\)-plane.

    Answer:

    Just to pick a random few: \((-3,5)\), \((0,5)\), and \((7, 5)\) all satisfy the equation. Why?—because for each of those pairs \(y\) equals 5!

  2. Graph the equation \(y = 5\) on the \((x,y)\)-plane.

    Answer:

    Recall that the the graph of an equation—in the context of the \((x,y)\)-plane!—is the set of all pairs that satisfy the equation. As seen in the previous part of the problem, the set of all pairs that satisfy the equation is just the set of pairs in which the second coordinate (the \(y\)-coordinate) is 5! That includes all the inifinitely many points that lie on the horizontal strip or line running through the "\(y=5\)" point on the \(y\)-axis:

As you'll see in the next example, we can represent vertical lines this way, too.

Graph the equation on the \((x,y)\)-plane: \[ x = -2 \]

Answer:

Again, the graph of an equation (on the \((x, y)\)-plane) is (a picture of) the set of all pairs that satisfy the equation (that make it true). What pairs satisfy this equation? Well—all the pairs that have a first coordinate (\(x\)-coordinate) of -2! Where are those pairs located?—all along a vertical strip that runs through the point marked \(-2\) on the \(x\)-axis:
