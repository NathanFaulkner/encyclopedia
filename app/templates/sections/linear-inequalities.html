In this section, we solve basic inequalities. You will see in the title of this section that these can be classified as "linear" inequalities. What does that mean? It isn't terribly important to discuss here, but we will in a later section.

So, how does one go about solving these? You may already know, and you may be a in a rush to just jump into the exercises. But let me warn you---naively approaching inequalities will quickly (in subsequent sections) lead you to a misconception and to a struggle to master these topics. It can be hard to unlearn mistaken ideas, sometimes! The key here (and to all of math) is to think about the meaning of each statement.

So, consider with me the "meaning of it all"!

Imagine now you are on problem #1, section #2 of your math textbook and it says this:
Solve for \(x\): \[ 3x - 7 \lt 8 \]
  1. What does this mean?

    Answer:

    As we learned in the section on linear inequalities there is a "secret narrative" behind such deceptively brief prompts as "Solve." The narrative ought to be expressed thusly:
    Suppose that \(x\) is a real number (so, could be plotted on the real line) with the property that if you subtract 7 from 3 times that number it comes out to be less than 8. This is properly regarded as an indirect description of a set of numbers. Your job when told to "solve" is to find a clear, direct description of this set.
    Put another way, we are looking for the set of all real numbers that makes the inequality true---or, as mathematicians would put it, that satisfy the inequality. (By the way, you might wonder whether there is a word that encompasses both equations and inequalities; an appropriate word is "relation.") The set of solutions is, of course, called the "solution set".
  2. Does 4 satisfy the inequality?

    Answer:

    Yes, it does! After all, if \(x = 4\), then \[ \textrm{LHS} = 3x - 7 = 3 \cdot 4 - 7 = 12 - 7 = 5 \lt 8 = \textrm{RHS}, \] where, again, I am using "LHS" to represent the left-hand side and "RHS" to represent the right-hand side.
  3. Does 5 satisfy the inequality?

    Answer:

    No, it doesn't. After all, if \(x = 5\), then \[ \textrm{LHS} = 3x - 7 = 3 \cdot 5 - 7 = 15 - 7 = 8 \not\lt 8 = \textrm{RHS}. \] So, as you can see, there are some real numbers that are part of the solution set and some that are not!
  4. Go ahead and solve for \(x\). That is, find a clear, direct description of the solution set.

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & 3x-7 \lt 8 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x \lt 15 & \textrm{I added 7 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \lt 5 & \textrm{I divided both sides by 3.}\\ \end{align*} Again, there is a narrative behind all of this:
    The narrative begins by supposing that \(x\) is a real number. If that's the case, then \(3x-7\) is a real number, too. Indeed, we are given that \(3x-7\) is less than 8. What real number it is, we do not know. All we know is that it is less than 8! The number symbolized as \(3x-7\) might be 5 or might be \(-1,000\). But no matter what it is, we know that if we add 7 to it, then its value will not exceed what we'd get if we added 7 to 8, no? In math symbols: \begin{align*} & 3x-7 \lt 8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x - 7 + 7 \lt 8 + 7\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x \lt 15\\ \end{align*} (Picturing all of this on the real line is key! Sorry that I don't yet have some handy visuals on this page!) Similarly, we next reason that if \(3x\) is some number less than (to the left of) \(15\) on the number line, then dividing this number by 3 will result in a number that is to the left of 15 divided by 3. Thus, we learn that---whatever the real number \(x\) is, which we really cannot know---it is less than 5. Since \(x\) was arbitrary to begin with, we thus reason that any real number \(x\) that satisfies this inequality must be less than 5. Since, too, we can "run these steps backwards", we also know that any real number \(x\) that is less than 5 will also have the given property. That means that the solution set is "the set of all real numbers that are less than 5."
    Of course, you aren't expected to give such extensive narratives (though it might be good for you). Instead, you are invited to communicate your answer by printing the symbols "\(x \lt 5\)". This is your description of the solution set. In subsequent sections, though, we will use other ways of representing this set.

Of course, you might be saying to yourself right now that I am making out of something very easy something now overly complicated. On the other hand, you might also be aware of the fact that there is a subtle change in "the rules" when you go from solving equations to inequalities: there is something about "dividing by a negative", you might recall. So, we need to consider an example.

Solve the inequality. That is, give a clear, direct description of its solution set. \[ - 12 x - 6 \lt - 8 x \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & - 12 x - 6 \lt - 8 x & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 4 x - 6 \lt 0 & \textrm{I added \(8x\) to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -4x \lt 6 & \textrm{I added 6 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \gt \frac{6}{-4} & \textrm{I divided both sides by -4.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \gt -\frac{3}{2} & \textrm{I simplified.}\\ \end{align*}

Look out! Do you see what "happened" at that last step where I "divided by -4"? The inequality sign "reversed"---from "less than" to "greater than". Now, let's get away from this passive talk and get right to the meat of it: why did I do that? What is the logic here? What is the meaning of it all? (The alternative is to just passively accept that "that's just the way things are." There is much to say about this attitude toward math, and I'm very sorry if your past experience in the subject has led you to its temptations! I promise a much richer life if you reject that stance on things as soon as possible!)

To find out why "the inequality reversed", we go through the narrative step-by-step. It begins with the assumption that \(x\) is a real number---so, it can be located on the real line somewhere! For that matter, \(-12x - 6\) and \(-8x\) are thus also real numbers located somewhere on the real line (The positions of \(-12x - 6\) and \(-8x\), you will do well to conceptualize, are determined by wherever \(x\) is and the actions of, for instance, multiplying by 12, changing to negative, and then subtracting 6. You will do very well for yourself if you imagine what all of those actions do to a number! For instance, you might pick some random number for \(x\), like 5, plotting it on the real line, and then perform all those actions to it. If you can do that, you have a good understanding of everything. If you can't, you don't. Again, accept my apologies for the lack of visuals or interactives on this page at present!) So, aside from the fact that everything in sight is a number, the other thing we know (are given) is that the number \(-12x - 6\) is less than---that is, to the left of---the number \(-8x\). But we can also reason about what happens when we do various things to these numbers. Imagine some number \({\color{red}A}\) and some number \({\color{blue}B}\) with \[ {\color{red}A} \lt {\color{blue}B} \] That is, imagine that \({\color{red}A}\) is to the left of \({\color{blue}B}\) somewhere. (You will do very well to sketch this on paper or at least---or maybe veen better!---sketch it in your mind!) Of course, that is the very situation we are considering here. You can regard \({\color{red}A} = {\color{red}-12x - 6}\) and \({\color{blue}B} = {\color{blue}-8x}\). Now, what happens when you add any number to both these numbers? Well, if you add a positive number \({\color{orange}C}\) to a number \({\color{red}A}\), then you get a new number \({\color{red}A} + {\color{orange}C}\)---which is \({\color{red}A}\) shifted \({\color{orange}C}\) units to the right. Of course, when you add \({\color{orange}C}\) to \({\color{blue}B}\) it too shifts to the right \({\color{orange}C}\) units. So, since adding \({\color{orange}C}\) to both \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) shifts them the same amount---"in formation"---you know that if \({\color{red}A}\) is to the left of \({\color{blue}B}\) before adding \({\color{orange}C}\) then that order won't change after adding \({\color{orange}C}\). In math symbols, \begin{align*} {\color{red}A} & \lt {\color{blue}B} \\ \Rightarrow \quad {\color{red}A} + {\color{orange}C} &\lt {\color{blue}B} + {\color{orange}C} \end{align*} Now \({\color{red}A}\), \({\color{blue}B}\), and \({\color{orange}C}\) were completely arbitrary---except that we said we'd imagine \({\color{orange}C}\) to be positive---so we can see that this will be true no matter what! What id \({\color{orange}C}\) is negative? Well, adding a negative number (AKA, subtracting) to \({\color{red}A}\) shifts it to the left on the real line, does it not? So, that doesn't make a big difference in everything we just considered. Thus, we an conclude that it is "adding a number to both sides of an inequality does not change the direction of the inequality". Do you see what I mean? In particular, even though we don't know what the number \({\color{orange}C} = {\color{orange}8x}\) is, we know that if we add it both \({\color{red}A} = {\color{red}-12x - 6}\) and \({\color{blue}B} = {\color{blue}-8x}\) the "direction" of the inequality does not change: \begin{align*} {\color{red}-12x - 6} & \lt {\color{blue}-8x} \\ \Rightarrow \quad {\color{red}-12x - 6} + {\color{orange}8x} & \lt {\color{blue}-8x} + {\color{orange}8x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad -4x - 6 & \lt 0 \ \end{align*}

Of course, next we can add 6 to both sides without switching the inequality, deducing that \[ -4x \lt 6 \] And next comes the subtle part.

Instead of speaking of "dividing by a negative number", I am going to narrate this differently. You see, we are in the business here of giving meaning to math---and, you may be pleased to find that I regard "dividing by a negative number" as without meaning on its own; rather, it is better to conceive it---in this discussion of taking actions on points on the real line---as two separate actions: the act of negation (of taking the negative of both sides) and then the act of dividing by plain, old 4.

So, what happens when you have real numbers \[ {\color{red}A} \lt {\color{blue}B} \] and you take the negative of both sides? All you have to do is to draw a picture! Pick two "random" points on the real line \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) on the real line with \({\color{red}A}\) to the left of \({\color{blue}B}\). It doesn't matter where these are, and, really, it is better if you don't bother with adding a bunch of tick marks---except those for \({\color{red}A}\), for \({\color{blue}B}\), and fpr one more point which is relevant to the concept of negation, 0. What happens (on the real line) when you take the negative of a number like \({\color{red}A}\)? What if \({\color{red}A}\) is positive; what if \({\color{red}A}\) is negative? As you may know, "the negative of a negative is a positive" and "the negative of a positive is negative". On the real line, we see the whole point of this: negation is just about swithing the direction of travel. After all, if you can travel to the east (to the righ on the real line), then sometimes you might want to travel west instead, no? The real number system is one that has been devised to represent and manage traveling in two different directions in one-dimensional space like a line---or a road, for that matter!

After consulting your picture, hopefully, you will see that after taking the negatives of \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) their order reverses. You might even try this experiment again, starting with \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) in different positions relative to 0 (what happens if both \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) are positive? what happens if \({\color{red}A}\)is negative and \({\color{blue}B}\) is positive? what happens if \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) are both negative?) Thus, \begin{align*} {\color{red}A} & \lt {\color{blue}B} \\ \Rightarrow \quad -{\color{red}A} & \gt -{\color{blue}B} \end{align*} In particular, \begin{align*} {\color{red}-4x} & \lt {\color{blue}6} \\ \Rightarrow \quad -({\color{red}-4x}) & \gt -{\color{blue}6} \\ \Rightarrow \quad 4x & \gt -6 \end{align*}

The final step isn't so complicated. We divide both sides by 4. What happens to two numbers \({\color{red}A} < {\color{blue}B}\) when we divide both by 4? Well, again, you should try drawing a picture of this: they both are pushed 75% closer to 0, you could say. That is, the distance of the new numbers is one quarter to 0 of the distance of \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B} \), respectively. In particular, the order of these two new numbers doesn't change. Thus (we don't switch the inequality because of dividing by 4), we get \[ x \gt \frac{-6}{4} = -\frac{3}{2} \]

Now, you might not need any more instruction on this, but it won't hurt for me to demo one more problem---one that is a little harder---while also skipping most of the narrative.

Solve the inequality. That is, give a clear, direct description of its solution set. \[ - 7 \left(x - 1\right) - 35 \gt - x - 35 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & - 7 \left(x - 1\right) - 35 \gt - x - 35\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 7x + 7 - 35 \gt - x - 35 & \textrm{Distributed.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 7x -28 \gt - x - 35 & \textrm{Simplified.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 7x \gt - x - 7 & \textrm{Added 7 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 6x \gt - 7 & \textrm{Added \(x\) to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 6x \lt 7 & \textrm{Negated both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \lt \frac{7}{6} & \textrm{Divided both sides by 6}\\ \end{align*}
