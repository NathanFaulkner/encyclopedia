Our basic goal in this section will be similar to previous sections: to come up with the equation for a line given some information about its graph. The difference in this section will be that you will be told that the line is either (i) parallel to or (ii) perpendicular to another line.

Here is one basic fact that you'll need to keep in mind:

Lines which are parallel have the same slope.

You have to earn the next fact:

The slopes of perpendicular lines are related, but how? To answer this, carry out the following steps:

  3. Then draw a "rise over run" triangle extending from your original line, with one corner at the intersection of your two lines. This will help you reason about the slopes. Say that the slope of your "generic" line is \(m\). So, label the rise of your triangle \(m\) and the run \(1\).

    Answer:

  4. Next, think about how the perpendicular line is just your original line rotated \(90^\circ\), say, clockwise, about that point of intersection. A key insight is got by actually drawing a rotated version of your "slope triangle."" This rotated slope-triangle will give you information about the slope of the perpendicular line. So—what is the slope of the new line; what is its "rise over run"? Draw the rotated "slope triangle" to figure it out.

    Answer:

    Note that the new slope is negative. Furthermore, whereas the original "slope triangle" had a run of 1, the rotated slope triangle has a fall of 1 (or a rise of \(-1\)). And, while the original slope triangle had a rise of \(m\), the new slope triangle has a run of \(m\). In all, the slope of the perpendicular line is: \[ \textrm{rotated slope} = \frac{\textrm{rise}}{\textrm{run}} = \frac{-1}{m} \] Since the act of taking a number and doing "1 over that number" is equivalent to "taking a reciprocal", the perpendicular slope is often referred to as "the negative reciprocal". What does "reciprocal" mean? It means "to flip a fraction upside down". For instance, the reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{2}\) is \(\frac{2}{1} = 2\), and the reciprocal of \(\frac{3}{5}\) is \(\frac{5}{3}\). Of course, if you take a reciprocal of a reciprocal, you end up where you started. Note, in particular, that since \(2 = \frac{2}{1}\), the reciprocal of \(2\) is \(\frac{1}{2}\).

Let's collect or summarize the work we did in the last example in the following:

The slope of a line perpendicular to a given line will be the "negative reciprocal" of the slope of the given line. Or, in math symbols, if you start with a line with slope \(m\), then the slope of any line perpendicular to it will be \(\frac{-1}{m}\). Actually, there is a little fine print here: this fact is not true if you start with a horizontal line—one with slope of 0. After all, what happens when you divide by 0? Nothing? Is it undefined? Well, yes and no: because something definitely happens from a geometric point of view! What happens when you rotate a horizontal line \(90^\circ\)? You get a vertical line of course! So, just keep your wits about you and you'll be fine.

Find an equation for the line that passes through \((-1,7)\) and is parallel to the line with equation \[ y = -\frac{2}{5}x + 0.7734 \]

Answer:

Since the line described is parallel to the one with the given equation and parallel lines have the same slope, we know the slope of the described line is \(m = -\frac{2}{5}\). We can use point-slope to develop an equation. As a reminder, point-slope form is \[ y = m(x - x_0) + y_0 \] Thus, an equation that will work is \[ y = -\frac{2}{5}(x - (-1)) + 7 \] You could also have written this: \[ y = -\frac{2}{5}(x + 1) + 7 \] You could also put the equation in slope-intercept form (or "\(y = mx + b\)" form), but it isn't necessary. Notice that if you did put your equation in slope-intercept form (by distributing out the \(-\frac{2}{5}\)), it would have the desired slope of \(-\frac{2}{5}\). But the nice thing about point-slope form (as a review) is that it is easy to see from it that the graph of our equation will have to pass through the point \((-1, 7)\). After all, the graph of an equation is the set of \((x, y)\)-pairs that satisfy the equation, and you can do some easy mental math to verify that if you plug in \(x = -1\), you'll get \(y = 7\).

Find an equation for the line passes through \((6, -3)\) and is perpendicular to the line with equation \[ y = 2x - 1000 \]

Answer:

We will again use point-slope form: \[ y = m(x - x_0) + y_0 \] This time, since the line we want an equation for is perpendicular to the line with the given equation, we take the negative reciprocal of the slope of the given line. The slope oour line will be \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\). Of course, we're also given a point the requested line passes through, so just plugging in the particulars to point-slope form completes the task: \[ y = -\frac{1}{2}(x - 6) - 3 \] Note that, if we plug \(x = 6\) into our equation, then we get \(y = -3\), which means our line definitely passes through the point \((6, -3)\). By the reasoning in example 1, we also know it has the desired slope. We're done! Was this suprisingly easy? Well, that's just what it is!

  2. Develop an equation for the line described.

    Answer:

    It may help to review the examples in the section on horizontal and vertical lines. An equation of a graph is an equation that has only those those points that lie on the graph that "make the equation true"—that satisfy the equation. In that way, it captures something special about the graph. So, what is special about a horizontal line? What could you say about it that could be written down in the format of an equation? Well, the only thing you can say about it is that, in being horizontal, all the points lying on it have the same \(y\)-coordinate; after all, to figure out the \(y\)-coordinate of a point, you just trace over horizontally until you hit the \(y\)-axis. So, how about this equation: \[ y = 3 \] You can see that every \((x, y)\)-pair that satisfies this equation will certainly have a \(y\)-coordinate of 3 and will hence lie along the graphed line. Likewise, every point on the line will satisfy the equation. So, that's it!

    Notice, of course, that \(x\) doesn't even appear in the equation. That means that the equation puts no restriction on \(x\). Put another way, \(x\) can just be whatever it wants. Mathematicians have a particular wasy of saying that; they would say that \(x\) is free. It's very much like considering the set of all real numbers \(x\) that satisfy such equations as \[ 0 = 0 \] This equation makes restriction on \(x\); it leaves \(x\) free to be whatever it wants—any real number—so its solution is "all reals".

    Again, you might want to take a look back at the section on horizontal and vertical lines. Recall that the graph of an equation is (a picture of) the set of all pairs that make the equation true. So, in that sense, an equation must say something very special and specific about the graph to capture it in that way. So, what can we say about a veritical line—answer: all of its \(x\)-coordinates are the same. After all, to find the \(x\)-coordinate of a point, you'd just trace up or down until you hit the \(x\)-axis and observe what tick marks you are on or within. The \(x\)-coordinates along this line are all \(-1\), so there you have it: \[ x = -1 \] That says, "all the \(x\)-coordinates of points that satisfy this equation have to be \(-1\)." That's exactly the same thing as what we can say about our vertical line, so we've got it (and no one can take it away from us!)
