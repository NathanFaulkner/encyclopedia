Next, we learn how to graph a quadratic when it is given in a pretty "generic" form: standard form.

A quadratic written in the form \begin{equation*}\tag{Standard Form} ax^2+bx+c \end{equation*} is said to be in standard form.
For example, \[ y = 2x^2 - 8x + 11 \] is in standard form.

So, what are we supposed to do with that? Recall that it is pretty straight-forward to graph a function in the form \(a(x-h)^2+k\). Note that this is called "vertex" form because you can just read the vertex off from it; it is \((h,k)\). Our strategy is actually to figure out how to put the standard form equation into vertex form! I'll train you on how to do that in the following example. My claim to you will be that, if you can put vertex form into standard form (which is something you would have done in the section Warm-Up with Quadratics ) then you can do the reverse, as demonstrated next.

We are going to put \(y=2x^2 - 8x+11\) into vertex form, i.e., \(y=a(x-h)^2+k\). The procedure is to follow these basic steps:

  1. We want to find \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) that will make the following equation true: \[ 2x^2 - 8x + 11 = a(x-h)^2+k \] The trick is to realize that, if this equation is true, then we should be able to multiply the right side all out and it will still equal the left side. Do that now, using the given letters as stand-ins for the unknown numbers we seek (in other words, in the usual manner of algebra!)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} a(x-h)^2+k &= a(x-h)(x-h) + k & \textrm{Getting ready to ``FOIL''}\\ &= a\left(x^2 - hx - hx + h^2\right) + k & \textrm{Did it.}\\ &= a(x^2 - 2hx + h^2) + k & \textrm{Collected like terms}\\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k & \textrm{Distributed the \(a\).} \end{align*}
  2. Using the previous step, identify what \(a\) will have to be.

    Answer:

    So, we have learned that \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will have to satisfy the following equation: \[ 2x^2 - 8x + 11 = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] Now, just look at it: What \(a\) will do the trick? Of course, if we took \(a = 2\), we'd be making progress, no? That way, at least the '\(x^2\)' terms would match up.
  3. Use your answer from the previous step to find what \(h\) would seem to work.

    Answer:

    We can fill in our value of \(2\) for \(a\) and then see what \(h\) will seem to work: \begin{align*} 2x^2 - 8x + 11 &= 2x^2 - 2\cdot 2 \cdot h x + 2h^2 + k \\ & = 2x^2 - 4h x + 2h^2 + k \end{align*} I'm already happy with the \(x^2\)-term. Now, I just keep going "down the line", comparing coefficients as I go. What value for \(h\) will make the \(x\)-term work out? Here is a side-by-side comparison of what we are after, with some color-coding so you can see what I am looking at: \[ 2x^2 \; {\color{cyan}- 8x} + 11 = 2x^2 \; {\color{cyan}- 4h x} + 2h^2 + k \] So, we need an \(h\) that makes \[ -8x = -4h x \] or, in other words, we need that \[ -8 = -4h \] So, of course, \(h\) ought to be \(2\)!
  4. Use your answer from the previous step to find what \(k\) would seem to work.

    Answer:

    We can fill in our value of \(2\) for \(h\) and then see what \(k\) will seem to work: \begin{align*} 2x^2 - 8x + 11 &= 2x^2 - 4 \cdot 2 \cdot x + 2\cdot 2^2 + k \\ & = 2x^2 - 8 x + 8 + k \end{align*} We already knocked out the \(x^2\) and \(x\) terms. Now, we just figure out what \(k\) will make the "constant" terms match up. Here is a side-by-side comparison of what we are after, with some color-coding so you can see what I am looking at: \[ 2x^2 - 8x \; {\color{cyan}+ 11} = 2x^2 - 8 x \; {\color{cyan} + 8 + k} \] We need that \[ 11 = 8 + k \] So, of course, \(k = 3\)!
  5. Write down your final answer—the vertex form of the given quadratic.

    Answer:

    Just fill in the values for \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\): \[ y = a(x - h)^2 + k = 2(x - 2)^2 + 3 \]
  6. In all, this method could be described as "figure out what \(a\) \(h\), and \(k\) would have to be to make the answer check out right." That is, if we multiply our vertex form back out, it ought to take us back to the standard form. Just double check that now. (This step is quite redundant, but I am just including it in case it helps a light bulb turn on for you!)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} 2(x - 2)^2 + 3 &= 2(x-2)(x-2) + 3 & \textrm{Getting ready to ``FOIL''}\\ &= 2(x^2 - 2x - 2x + 4) + 3 & \textrm{Did it} \\ &= 2(x^2 - 4x + 4) + 3 & \textrm{Collected like terms}\\ &= 2x^2 - 8x + 8 + 3 & \textrm{Distributed the \(2\)}\\ &= 2x^2 - 8x + 11 & \textrm{Simplified} \end{align*} Look! We did it! The vertex form we supplied is indeed equivalent to the given standard form. (By the way, this might be a good time to ask yourself what it means for two formulas to be "equivalent" in math: it means that they are the same in a special sense: they look different, but no matter what values you plug in for the variables, they would come out the same. In other words, they define the same function/input-output relationship.)

Rewrite in vertex form and then graph the function \[ f(x) = -x^{2} + 4 x - 6 \]

Answer:

First, write an equation that expresses your goal: to find \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) such that \[ -x^2 + 4x - 6 = a(x - h)^2 + k \] Multiply the right side all out, and then, one-by-one, deduce what values of \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will work: \begin{align*} -x^2 + 4x - 6 &= a(x - h)^2 + k\\ &= a(x-h)(x-h) + k & \textrm{Getting ready to ``FOIL''}\\ &= a\left(x^2 - hx - hx + h^2\right) + k & \textrm{Did it.}\\ &= a(x^2 - 2hx + h^2) + k & \textrm{Collected like terms}\\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k & \textrm{Distributed the \(a\).} \end{align*} Here it is rewritten in a single line for easier visual comparison: \[ -x^2 + 4x - 6 = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] Now, I figure out what \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will work by considering terms one at a time, left to right. First I see that if I choose \(a\) so that \[ -x^2 = ax^2 \] I'll be doing good. Of course, that means choosing \(a = -1\). So, I fill that in: \begin{align*} -x^2 + 4x - 6 &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k\\ &= -x^2 - 2(-1)h x + (-1)h^2 + k \\ &= -x^2 + 2h x - h^2 + k \end{align*}

Next I find \(h\) and \(k\) by continuing to compare the coefficients, one-by-one. I'm already happy with the \(x^2\)-term. Now, I just keep going "down the line", comparing coefficients as I go. What value for \(h\) will make the \(x\)-term work out? Here is a side-by-side comparison of what we are after, with some color-coding so you can see what I am looking at: \[ -x^2 \; {\color{cyan}+ \; 4x} - 6 = -x^2 \; {\color{cyan}+ \; 2h x} - h^2 + k \] So, we need an \(h\) that makes \[ 4x = 2h x \] or, in other words, we need that \[ 4 = 2h \] So, of course, \(h\) ought to be \(2\)! So, I fill that in as well: \begin{align*} -x^2 + 2h x - h^2 + k &= -x^2 + 2\cdot 2 x - 2^2 + k \\ & = -x^2 + 4x - 4 + k \end{align*} It helps to recopy this side-by-side with the given. \[ -x^2 + 4x - 6 = x^2 + 4x - 4 + k \] Here it is color-coded, so you can see what I am looking at next: \[ -x^2 + 4x \; {\color{cyan}-\; 6} = x^2 + 4x \; {\color{cyan}-\; 4 + k} \] So, \(k\) will have to satisfy \[ -6 = - 4 + k \] Solving, I get \(k = -2\). In all, I get this vertex form expression by filling in the values of \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\): \begin{align*} f(x) &= a(x - h)^2 + k\\ &= -1(x - 2^2 - 2 \\ &= -(x - 2)^2 - 2 \end{align*} From this, I can graph the function. If you need to review how to do that, you should head on over to the section on graphing from vertex form But, as a brief review, I interpret the vertex form as a transformation of \(y = x^2\): Because of the "\(+2\)" next to \(x\), I know that the graph involves a translation left 2 units. Because of the negative in front of the squared term, I know the graph involves a reflection across the \(x\)-axis—making the \(y\)-values head towards the negatives. Because of the "\(-2\)" added on there, I know the graph will involve a translation down 2 units. So, this is my graph:

Alternate Method to Get Vertex Form, I:

You may find the process I just demonstrated to be kind of tedious and overly-involved. So, you are the type of person to ask, "Isn't there an easier way?" And, yes, there is perhaps an easier way—an easier way that pretty much amounts to the same thing but is less trouble to write down. Here goes:

Recall that our goal is to find \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) so that \[ -x^2 + 4x - 6 = a(x - h)^2 + k \] You have to be able to at least partially imagine what it would be like to multiply the right side all out and then to compare coefficients. The right side would end up looking like this: \[ a(x^2 - 2h x + h^2) + k \] The way this method proceeds is to leave some blanks where the terms involving \(h\) and \(k\) go and then fill them in bit-by-bit. But first, you have to factor out the coefficient on \(x^2\) from both the \(x^2\) and \(x\) terms. While you are doing that, go ahead and leave yourself some blank space, like so: \[ -x^2 + 4x - 6 = -(x^2 - 4x + \phantom{44}) - 6 \] Next up, you ask yourself what to add into that blank space within the parentheses. The idea is that, for this right side to follow the pattern set by the generic format of \[ a(x^2 - 2h x + h^2) + k \] you'll need that the number that fills in the blank will have to be the square of half the coefficient of \(x\) in the middle. After all, what should go in the "blank" is \(h^2\), and \(2h\) actually appears in the middle, as that coefficient on \(x\). So you take half that coefficient (to get \(h\)) and then square it to get what goes in the blank. In this case, the middle coefficient is \(-4\), so \(h\) must be \(-2\), and therefore \(h^2 = (-2)^2 = 4\). You can go ahead and fill in the blank, but BE CAREFUL, your expression won't be equivalent to what you started with just yet: some tweaking will have to be done to the numbers outside the parentheses: \[ -x^2 + 4x - 6 = -(x^2 - 4x + 4) - 6 - \textrm{one further tweak} \] Now, at this point, I have just inserted a '\(4\)' just because I felt like it. Is that legit math? No, no exactly: our expression will not be equivalent to the given if we change in any old, arbitrary way like that. So, we have to tweak it. We like the '\(4\)' we inserted, but we have to pay for it somehow; we can't get it for free. Really, since the \(4\) is inside parentheses with a minus out front, I have really arbitrarily added \(-4\) to the expression we started with. So, to make it right—to make it equivalent to the given—I just need to take away \(-4\) outside the parentheses. Of course, taking away \(-4\) means adding \(4\), if you like. So, the following statement is true: \begin{align*} -x^2 + 4x - 6 &= -(x^2 - 4x + 4) - 6 - (-4) \\ &= -(x^2 - 4x +4) - 6 + 4 \\ & = -(x^2 - 4x + 4) - 2 \end{align*} Now, the point of inserting that \(4\) to begin with was to make an expression that conforms to the pattern seen when multiplying out a square: \[ a(x - h)^2 + k = a(x^2 - 2h x + h^2) + k \] We now have something on our hands of the form of the right-hand side of this equaiton, which means we can rewrite it according to the form of the left-hand side. In other words, we can now make the observation that \(x^2 - 4x + 4\) is a square. It is what you get by expanding \((x-2)^2\). Thus, \[ -(x^2 - 4x + 4) - 2 = -(x-2)^2 - 2 \] The expression on the right is our final, vertex form of the given.

By the way, this "fill in the blank" process that I am demonstrating here is called completing the square. It refers to the fact that you have left a blank inside the squared term and thus have to fill it in or "complete it". This is a favorite buzzword of math teachers! There are different ways of narrating how to accomplish this, but they all amount to about the same thing. Perhaps you will like my first method better, as it is more step-by-step. The first method involves a little less mental juggling, but, as a trade-off, has farmed that mental juggling to the paper, so it involves more writing. So, you'll just have to decide which method gets better outcomes for you—slow and steady (tortoise beats hare) or quick like a bunny. Or perhaps you will like a kind of cheater's method, which involves a formula. That's what the next method is about.

Alternate Method to Get Vertex Form II:

Finally, let me show you a formula or algorithm for accomplishing this task. (By the way, as noted in the explanation of Alternate Method I, this task is called completing the square by math teachers.) I am going to first derive the formula, but, in the end, the point is just to know the formula whether you know how to derive it or not. You can derive or come up with this formula simply by doing my original method to the "generic" problem. That is, take a generic quadratic written in standard form: \[ f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are some given numbers. Also, \(a \not = 0\), because that would make it linear—which we already know how to graph and don't need to bother with any fancy formulas for. Then, to put it into vertex form, we need to find \(h\) and \(k\) so that: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = a(x - h)^2 + k\\ \] Spoiler alert: \(a\) is \(a\)! Same on both sides. What does that mean for our solving process? It means that whatever number is the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the standard form expression will also be the coefficient of the squared term in the vertex form expression. Now, for the rest of it, we expand out the right side and compare coefficients: \begin{align*} ax^2 + bx + c &= a(x - h)^2 + k\\ &= a(x-h)(x-h) + k & \textrm{Getting ready to ``FOIL''}\\ &= a\left(x^2 - hx - hx + h^2\right) + k & \textrm{Did it.}\\ &= a(x^2 - 2hx + h^2) + k & \textrm{Collected like terms}\\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k & \textrm{Distributed the \(a\).} \end{align*} Here is what we deduced, written in a single line: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] So, what about \(h\)? Compare the coefficients on \(x\): it will work out if \(h\) satisfies \[ b = -2ah \] So, \[ h = -\frac{b}{2a} \] I solved for \(h\)! So, this formula will work every time. In words, it says to find the coefficient (\(b\)) of \(x\) in the standard form presentation. Make it negative, and then divide by twice the coefficient of \(x^2\). That will be your \(h\), which has to go after the negative in the vertex format. So, for example, if you are given \[ f(x) = -x^2 - 4 x - 6 \] then your \(a = -1\) and your \(b = -4\). Thus, \[ h = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{-4}{2 (-1)} = \frac{4}{-2} = -2 \] Thus, the vertex form is partially determined: \[ a(x - h)^2 + k = -(x - (-2))^2 + k = -(x + 2)^2 + k \]

Now, what about \(k\)? Well, we could continue in the same fashion. But, the formula you get isn't all that nice. I don't think it is fun or very useful to memorize formulas, but you might tend to disagree with me on that. So, let's check it out just to see. Going back to the task of comparing coefficients, I have copied our template from above: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] So, looking at the "constant" terms (the parts without \(x\)), we see that \(k\) needs to satisfy \[ c = ah^2 + k \] Solving for \(k\) is actually quite easy: \[ k = c - ah^2 \] So, there you have it! First, deduce what \(h\) is, and then perform this formula to get \(k\). Of course, you also have to remember the formula. Are you sure that you will? How long will you remember the formula? Well, let's see it demonstrated. Again, working from the given of \[ f(x) = -x^2 - 4 x - 6 \] we have \(a = -1\) and \(c = -6\). We also already figured out that that \(h = -2\). Therefore, \[ k = c - ah^2 = -6 - (-1)(-2)^2 = -6 - (-1)\cdot 4 = -6- (-4) = -6 + 4 = -2 \] Mind your order of operations! So, there you have it. Filling in the values in our final answer, we have the vertex form is \[ a(x - h)^2 + k = -1(x - (-2))^2 - 2 = -(x+2)^2 - 2 \] Now, I have never met anyone (that I am aware of) that memorized that formula for \(k\). It just doesn't seem that fun to me. Lucky for you, there is another way to figure out what \(k\) is. You see, \(k\) is just the \(y\)-value that gets paired with \(x\) value of \(x = h\). That is, since \(h = -2\), the \(k\) will just be what you get when you plug in \(-2\) for \(x\), like so: \begin{align*} k = f(h) = f(-2) &= -(-2)^2 - 4 (-2) - 6 \\ &= - 4 + 8 - 6 \\ &= -2 \end{align*} So, in all, you certainly might consider memorizing the facts that if \[ f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \] (fine print: \(a \not = 0\)) then the format \[ f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k \] can be obtained by computing \[ h = -\frac{b}{2a} \] and \[ k = f(h) \]

I am going to demonstrate just one more example, demoing each of the 3 methods again, but with a lot less talk:

Rewrite in vertex form and then graph the function \[ f(x) = -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} \]

Answer:

First, write an equation that expresses your goal: to find \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) such that \[ -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} = a(x - h)^2 + k \] Multiply the right side all out, and then, one-by-one, deduce what values of \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will work: \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} &= a(x - h)^2 + k\\ &= a(x-h)(x-h) + k & \textrm{Getting ready to ``FOIL''}\\ &= a\left(x^2 - hx - hx + h^2\right) + k & \textrm{Did it.}\\ &= a(x^2 - 2hx + h^2) + k & \textrm{Collected like terms}\\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k & \textrm{Distributed the \(a\).} \end{align*} Here it is rewritten in a single line for easier visual comparison: \[ -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] Now, I figure out what \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will work by considering terms one at a time, left to right. Equating the coefficients of the \(x^2\) terms, I see that \[ a = -\frac{1}{2} \] So, I fill that in: \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k\\ &= -\frac{1}{2}x^2 - 2(-\frac{1}{2})h x + (-\frac{1}{2})h^2 + k \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + h x -\frac{1}{2} h^2 + k \end{align*}

Next I find \(h\) and \(k\) by continuing to compare the coefficients, one-by-one. I'm already happy with the \(x^2\)-term. Now, I just keep going "down the line": What value for \(h\) will make the \(x\)-term work out? Here is a side-by-side comparison of what we are after, with some color-coding so you can see what I am looking at: \[ -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} \; {\color{cyan} +\; 3 x} - \frac{7}{2} = -\frac{1}{2}x^2 \; {\color{cyan}+ \; h x} -\frac{1}{2} h^2 + k \] Comparing the coefficients of \(x\), I see that \[ h = 3 \] So, I fill that in as well, to learn that: \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} &= -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + h x -\frac{1}{2} h^2 + k & = &= -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + 3 x -\frac{1}{2} \cdot 3^2 + k \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + 3 x -\frac{9}{2} + k \end{align*} It helps to recopy this side-by-side with the given: \[ -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x - \frac{7}{2} = -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + 3 x -\frac{9}{2} + k \] Here it is color-coded, so you can see what I am paying attention to next: \[ -\frac{1}{2}x^{2} + 3 x \; {\color{cyan}- \; \frac{7}{2}} = -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + 3 x \; {\color{cyan}-\;\frac{9}{2} + k} \] So, now what does \(k\) have to be? Specifically, what will \(k\) have to be to make the "constant" terms (just numbers, no \(x\)'s) match up: \(k\) will have to satisfy \[ - \frac{7}{2} = -\frac{9}{2} + k \] Solving, I get \[ k = - \frac{7}{2} + \frac{9}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1 \] In all, I get this vertex form expression by filling in the values of \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\): \begin{align*} f(x) &= a(x - h)^2 + k\\ &= -\frac{1}{2}(x - 3)^2 + 1 \\ \end{align*} From this, I can graph the function. If you need to review how to do that, you should head on over to the section on graphing from vertex form But, as a brief review, I interpret the vertex form as a transformation of \(y = x^2\): Because of the "\(-3\)" next to \(x\), I know that the graph involves a translation right 3 units. Because of the negative in front of the squared term, I know the graph involves a reflection across the \(x\)-axis—making the \(y\)-values head towards the negatives. Because the coefficient on the square term also has a magnitude of \(\frac{1}{2}\), I know those \(y\)'s will decrease at half the rate that they do for \(y = -x^2\). Because of the "\(+ 1\)" added on the end there, I know the graph will involve a translation up 1 units. So, this is my graph:

Alternate Method to Get Vertex Form I:

To "complete the square", I first need to factor out the coefficient on \(x^2\) from both the \(x^2\) term and the \(x\) term. That's a little trickier in this case, so let me talk you through it. Factoring out \(-\frac{1}{2}\) would look something like this: \[ -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - ? x + \phantom{44}\right) - \frac{7}{2} \] When "factoring out", we have to leave something behind. "Factoring" is just a word for "reversing the distributive property"; you might call it "undistributing" instead. So, to figure out what \(?\) has to be, just imagine what you could multiply by \(\frac{1}{2}\) to go back to what you started with—in other words, to get the \(3\) back. That is, "one half of what number is 3?" The answer, I hope you will agree, is \(6\). That means my first step of completing the square looks like this: \[ -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - 6 x + \phantom{44}\right) - \frac{7}{2} - \textrm{one more tweak} \] Next, I fill in the blank in such a way that "makes the thing inside the parentheses a square." What does that mean? It means we want to rewrite the part in parentheses in the form \((x - h)^2\). To realize how to do that, you have to imagine expanding the generic expression \((x-h)^2\) to realize ho to get from one form to another: \[ (x-h)^2 = x^2 - 2hx + h^2 \] The observation to make is that you have to fill in the blank with the square of one half of the middle coefficient. The middle coefficient (I mean, the coefficient on \(x\)) is \(-6\). One half of that is \(-3\), and squaring yields \(9\). So, that's it! That's the next (half-)step of completing the square. (We just "completed the square" when we filled in the blank to make the expression inside the parentheses a square.) \[ f(x) = -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - 6x + 9\right) - \frac{7}{2} - \textrm{one more tweak} \] Adding \(9\) within the paretheses—which have a \(-\frac{1}{2}\) outside of them as a coefficient—means that we have arbitrarily added \[ -\frac{1}{2}\cdot 9 = - \frac{9}{2} \] to our expression. Thus, it is not yet equivalent to the given. TO fix it, on the outside of the parentheses, we just take \(-\frac{9}{2}\) away again. (But since we will be taking away a negative, you could just register this as adding it back in after having taken it away.) \begin{align*} f(x) &= -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - 6x + 9\right) - \frac{7}{2} - \left(- \frac{9}{2}\right)\\ &= -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - 6x + 9\right) - \frac{7}{2} + \frac{9}{2}\\ &= -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - 6x + 9\right) + \frac{2}{2}\\ &= -\frac{1}{2}\left(x^2 - 6x + 9\right) + 1 \end{align*} As a final step, we recall why we inserted the \(9\) in the first place: to make the expression inside the parentheses a perfect square: \[ f(x) = -\frac{1}{2}\left(x - 3\right)^2 + 1 \] That's in vertex form! So, we are ready to graph it. (See the answer above for the graph.)

Alternate Method to Get Vertex Form II:

We view our given as example of the generic format \[ ax^2 + bx + c \] Thus, \(a = -\frac{1}{2}\), \(b = 3\), and \(c = -\frac{7}{2}\). THe vertex form \[ a(x-h)^2 + k \] can be obtained by using \(a = -\frac{1}{2}\) ("same \(a\) in both places") and \[ h = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{3}{2 \cdot -\frac{1}{2}} = -\frac{3}{-1} = 3 \] and \begin{align*} k = f(h) = f(3) &= -\frac{1}{2}\cdot 3^2 + 3\cdot 3 - \frac{7}{2}\\ &= -\frac{9}{2} + 9 - \frac{7}{2}\\ &= -\frac{16}{2} + 9\\ &= -8 + 9\\ &= 1 \end{align*} So, in all, our vertex form is \[ a(x - h)^2 + k = -\frac{1}{2}\left(x - 3\right)^2 + 1 \] We are now ready to graph it.
