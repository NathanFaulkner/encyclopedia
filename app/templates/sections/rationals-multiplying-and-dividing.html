What are we going to do with all of these weird functions? Answer: the same thing we did in the last chapter: We are going to add, subtract, multiply, divide, and graph these critters. What do you think will be easiest? If you answered "addition," you spoke too soon! Note that rational functions are foremost just fractions—albeit where the exact value of the numerator and denominator are not given (their identities are withheld under the guise of variables). So, what is easier to do with fractions—add, subract, multiply, or divide?

How do you multiply rational functions? Well, since rational functions just have fractions as outputs, the answer is "in the same way you multiply fractions." So, how you do you multiply fractions? You'd better make sure you're clear on this—because this is really the heart of the matter!

Multiply the following fractions together. Simplify (reduce to lowest terms).
  1. \(\frac{3}{4}\cdot \frac{8}{7}\)

    Answer:

    \[ \frac{3}{4}\cdot \frac{8}{7} = \frac{3\cdot 8}{4 \cdot 7} = \frac{24}{28} \] But! You could also reduce this ratio to lower terms. After all, \(24\) and \(28\) are both divisible by \(4\): \[ \frac{24}{28} = \frac{\cancel{4}\cdot 6}{\cancel{4} \cdot 7} = \frac{6}{7} \] Indeed, you actually could have reduced before multiplying by canceling common factors, like so: \[ \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{8}{7} = \frac{3}{\cancelto{1}{4}}\cdot \frac{\cancelto{2}{8}}{7} = \frac{3 \cdot 2}{7} = \frac{6}{7} \]
  2. \(\frac{5}{3}\cdot \frac{7}{15}\)

    Answer:

    \[ \frac{5}{3}\cdot \frac{7}{15} = \frac{\cancelto{1}{5}\cdot 7}{3 \cdot \cancelto{3}{15}} = \frac{7}{9} \]
  3. \(\frac{2}{9}\cdot \frac{3}{8}\)

    Answer:

    \[ \frac{2}{9}\cdot \frac{3}{8} = \frac{\cancelto{1}{2}\cdot \cancelto{1}{3}}{\cancelto{3}{9} \cdot \cancelto{4}{8}} = \frac{1}{12} \]
  4. \(\frac{4}{3}\cdot \frac{6}{5}\)

    Answer:

    \[ \frac{4}{3}\cdot \frac{6}{5} = \frac{4\cdot \cancelto{2}{6}}{\cancelto{1}{3} \cdot 5} = \frac{8}{5} \]

Next, we do "the very same thing" with rational functions! Firstly, that will mean "multiplying numerator by numerator and denominator by denominator" (don't dare think "cross multiply"!!). Next, what about the "reduce to lowest terms" concept? With numbers, you are looking for common factors to cancel, so you are mentally breaking the numerator and denominator into primes and crossing out those common factors: you are factoring. So, how does that work when the numerator and denominator aren't numbers but rather polynomials? Well, still you factor. Recall from a previous section above that, indeed, what the "primes" of the world of polynomials are can be understood by the fact that any polynomial can be (theoretically) written in the form \[ f(x) = a(x-z_1)(x-z_2)\cdots (x-z_n) \] where the \(a\) and the \(z\)'s are numbers. So, this expresses that polynomials can be factored and what the "primes are in this setting."

So, here will be our m.o.: factor numerator and denominator; cross out common factors (reducing to lowest terms); multiply numerator times numerator and denominator times denominator—but don't bother distributing out. Check out my examples to make sure you understand what I am looking for.

Simplify. That is, reduce the following to lowest terms. \[ \frac{x^2 - 3x - 18}{x^2 - 1} \cdot \frac{x^2+ 4x - 5}{x^2 -x - 30} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} \frac{x^2 - 3x - 18}{x^2 - 1} \cdot \frac{x^2+ 4x - 5}{x^2 -x - 30} &= \frac{(x-6)(x+3)}{(x-1)(x+1)} \cdot \frac{(x+5)(x-1)}{(x-6)(x+5)} \\ & = \frac{\cancel{(x-6)}(x+3)}{\bcancel{(x-1)}(x+1)} \cdot \frac{\xcancel{(x+5)}\bcancel{(x-1)}}{\cancel{(x-6)}\xcancel{(x+5)}} \\ & = \frac{x+3}{x+1} \end{align*}

Simplify. That is, reduce the following to lowest terms. \[ \frac{2x^2+2x-12}{x^2 + 6x - 7} \cdot \frac{x^2+4x-5}{4x^2 + 32x + 60} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} \frac{2x^2+2x-12}{x^2 + 6x - 7} \cdot \frac{x^2+4x-5}{4x^2 + 32x + 60} &= \frac{2(x-2)(x+3)}{(x-1)(x+7)} \cdot \frac{(x+5)(x-1)}{4(x+3)(x+5)} \\ &=\frac{\cancelto{1}{2}(x-2)\cancel{(x+3)}}{\bcancel{(x-1)}(x+7)} \cdot \frac{\xcancel{(x+5)}\bcancel{(x-1)}}{\cancelto{2}{4}\cancel{(x+3)}\xcancel{(x+5)}} \\ & = \frac{x-2}{2(x+7)} \end{align*}

Divide the fractions, and reduce to lowest terms.
  1. \(\frac{3}{4}\div\frac{7}{8}\)

    Answer:

    \[ \frac{3}{4}\div \frac{7}{8} = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{8}{7} = \frac{3}{\cancelto{1}{4}}\cdot \frac{\cancelto{2}{4}}{7} = \frac{6}{7} \]
  2. \(\frac{5}{3}\div \frac{15}{7}\)

    Answer:

    \[ \frac{5}{3}\div \frac{15}{7} = \frac{5}{3} \cdot \frac{7}{15} = \frac{\cancelto{1}{5}}{3}\cdot \frac{7}{\cancelto{3}{15}} = \frac{7}{9} \]
  3. \(\dfrac{\;\frac{6}{5} \;}{\frac{3}{4}}\)

    Answer (Style 1):
    \[ \dfrac{\;\frac{6}{5} \;}{\frac{3}{4}} = \frac{6}{5}\cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{\cancelto{2}{6}}{5} \cdot \frac{4}{\cancelto{1}{2}} = \frac{8}{5} \]

    Answer (Style 2):
    \[ \dfrac{\;\frac{6}{5} \;}{\frac{3}{4}} = \dfrac{\;\frac{6\cdot 4}{5 \cdot 4} \;}{\frac{3 \cdot 5}{4 \cdot 5}} = \frac{6 \cdot 4}{5 \cdot 3} = \frac{\cancelto{2}{6} \cdot 4}{5 \cdot \cancelto{1}{3}} = \frac{8}{5} \]
  4. \(\dfrac{\;\frac{12}{5}\;}{\frac{3}{10}}\)

    Answer (Style 1):
    \[ \dfrac{\;\frac{12}{5} \;}{\frac{3}{10}} = \frac{12}{5}\cdot \frac{10}{3} = \frac{\cancelto{4}{12}}{\cancelto{1}{5}} \cdot \frac{\cancelto{2}{10}}{\cancelto{1}{3}} = \frac{8}{1} = 8 \]

    Answer (Style 2):
    \[ \dfrac{\;\frac{12}{5} \;}{\frac{3}{10}} = \dfrac{\;\frac{12\cdot 2}{5 \cdot 2} \;}{\frac{3}{10}} = \dfrac{\;\frac{12\cdot 2}{10} \;}{\frac{3}{10}} = \frac{12 \cdot 2}{3} = \frac{\cancelto{4}{12} \cdot 2}{\cancelto{1}{3}} = \frac{8}{1} = 8 \]

So, hopefully you recall the "rule", "divide means multiply the numerator by the reciprocal of the denominator." (Say, why does that rule work? It's worth contemplating, even though I haven't yet supplied an answer—or a link to an answer—here.) Divide the rational function, and simplify (reduce to lowest terms). \[ \frac{x^2 - 3x - 18}{x^2 - 1} \div \frac{x^2 -5x - 6}{x^2+ 4x - 5} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} \frac{x^2 - 3x - 18}{x^2 - 1} \div \frac{x^2 -5x - 6}{x^2+ 4x - 5} &= \frac{x^2 - 3x - 18}{x^2 - 1} \cdot \frac{x^2+ 4x - 5}{x^2 -5x - 6}\\ & =\frac{(x-6)(x+3)}{(x-1)(x+1)} \cdot \frac{(x+5)(x-1)}{(x-6)(x+1)} \\ & = \frac{\cancel{(x-6)}(x+3)}{\bcancel{(x-1)}(x+1)} \cdot \frac{(x+5)\bcancel{(x-1)}}{\cancel{(x-6)}(x+1)} \\ & = \frac{(x+3)(x+5)}{(x+1)^2} \end{align*}

Divide the rational function, and simplify (reduce to lowest terms). \[ \frac{2x^2+2x-12}{x^2 + 6x - 7} \div \frac{4x^2 + 32x + 60}{x^2+4x-5} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} \frac{2x^2+2x-12}{x^2 + 6x - 7} \div \frac{4x^2 + 32x + 60}{x^2+4x-5} &= \frac{2x^2+2x-12}{x^2 + 6x - 7} \cdot \frac{x^2+4x-5}{4x^2 + 32x + 60}\\ &=\frac{2(x-2)(x+3)}{(x-1)(x+7)} \cdot \frac{(x+5)(x-1)}{4(x+3)(x+5)} \\ &=\frac{\cancelto{1}{2}(x-2)\cancel{(x+3)}}{\bcancel{(x-1)}(x+7)} \cdot \frac{\xcancel{(x+5)}\bcancel{(x-1)}}{\cancelto{2}{4}\cancel{(x+3)}\xcancel{(x+5)}} \\ & = \frac{x-2}{2(x+7)} \end{align*}
