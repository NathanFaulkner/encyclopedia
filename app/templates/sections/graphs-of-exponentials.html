In this section, we first contemplate how to graph exponential functions with various bases. Then we just transform those graphs (shifting left, right, up, down; reflecting; scaling vertically).

As always, when tasked with discovering what a brand-new graph looks like, we resort to the time-consuming method of "just plotting points." And, as always, let me point out that, while this method would technically work in any situation (in fact, it is the strategy used by a graphing calculator), in the exercises (and checks) I will really be asking you to graph by the method of transformations—taking these first few basic, underlying functions, which we graph in the activities below, and whose points you should recall mentally during the exercises—and shifting appropriately.

    Answer:

    If you feel somewhat at a loss as to how to answer this question, that's a good thing! What you would need to do is argue that small changes in \(x\) lead to small changes in \(y = 2^x\) and that these changes happen in a "smooth" way.
