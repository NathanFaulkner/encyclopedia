In this section, we'll take a look at the graphs of a few more power functions and then transform them (shift them left, right, up, or down; reflect them; scale them vertically.) Actually, we'll just concentrate on four of them for now:

n this section, we first contemplate how to graph exponential functions with various bases. Then we just transform those graphs (shifting left, right, up, down; reflecting; scaling vertically).

As always, when tasked with discovering what a brand-new graph looks like, we resort to the time-consuming method of "just plotting points." And, as always, let me point out that, while this method would technically work in any situation (in fact, it is the strategy used by a graphing calculator), in the exercises (and checks) I will really be asking you to graph by the method of transformations—taking these first few basic, underlying functions, which we graph in the activities below, and whose points you should recall mentally during the exercises—and shifting appropriately.

Click for video

You are going to "plot points" until you realize what the graph of \(y=2^x\) looks like. You should repeat this activity on your own whenever you forget what it looks like! However, I want you to know up front that this is not the method I suggest you use on any of the exercises in this section. Instead, this is a tedious method that you do to just get started somewhere. We will adopt a much more stream-lined method in the examples below—but only after going through the tedious, point-by-point process at least once.

All you have to do is to plug in a nice variety of \(x\) values (plug in 0, plug in some positive numbers, plug in some negative numbers), and keep going until a light bulb goes on for you—and you are utterly certain what the graph looks like.

Since this may be your first time in doing such a task, I will guide you through it. But in the future, I expect you to be able to plot points on your own.

Now, we turn to transformations of these graphs.

Graph the following function using transformations. Be sure to include enough points to leave no doubt as to the accuracy of your graph. Also, {\color{magenta} describe your transformations in words.} \[ y = 3^{x-1} \]

Answer:

This looks new and unusual, but there is a commonality with what we have studied previously. The graph of \(y=3^{x-1}\) will be much like that of \(y=3^x\) except the \(x\)-values will have to be one bigger (to the right) to achieve the "old \(y\)-values." Yep, transformation can be described as:
  • right 1

Graph the following function using transformations. Be sure to include enough points to leave no doubt as to the accuracy of your graph. Also, {\color{magenta} describe your transformations in words.} \[ y = -2^x \] \begin{minipage}{0.45\textwidth}

Answer:

You obtain the \(y\)-values from the expression \(2^x\), but then, by order of operations, take the negative of each. The transformation is:
  • reflect across \(x\)-axis.
