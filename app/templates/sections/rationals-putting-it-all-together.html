Question: What do you get when you add, subtract, multiply, or divide a rational function by another rational function? Answer: Another rational function!

In this section, we'll look at some ugly examples of rational functions added or subtracted with other rational functions and then divided by something just as bad. Why on Earth would we want to do that? Well, it so happens that you might just encounter such things in your natural mathematical ecosystem of future math classes. A second reason: because we can hopefully make them not nearly so bad as I am making them sound right now. Example time!

Simplify the following. (Your goal should be to write it plainly as a polynomial over another polynomial, as well as to look to factor these polynomials and cancel like factors, wherever possible.) \[ \frac{\frac{1}{x}-\frac{2}{x-1}}{\frac{3}{x}+5} \]

Answer, via Method 1:

We can treat the numerator and denominator as separate addition/subtraction problems. Then we'll have a fraction divided by a fraction, which we can also handle just fine. \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{1}{x}-\frac{2}{x-1}}{\frac{3}{x}+5} & = \frac{\frac{x-1}{x(x-1)}-\frac{2x}{x(x-1)}}{\frac{3}{x}+ \frac{5x}{x}}\\ & = \frac{\frac{x-1 - 2x}{x(x-1)}}{\frac{3+5x}{x}}\\ & = \frac{\frac{-x-1}{x(x-1)}}{\frac{3+5x}{x}} \\ & = \frac{-x-1}{\cancel{x}(x-1)}\cdot \frac{\cancel{x}}{3+5x} \\ & = \frac{-x-1}{(x-1)(3+5x)}\\ & = \frac{-(x+1)}{(x-1)(3+5x)} \end{align*} At the end, there, I factored the numerator just in case I could get a common factor in the numerator and denominator, but I couldn't.

Answer, via Method 2:

Another way to handle it is to multiply "top and bottom" of this "big" fraction by factors that will kill off the denominators of the "fractions within the fraction," like so:\begin{align*} \frac{\frac{1}{x}-\frac{2}{x-1}}{\frac{3}{x}+5} & = \frac{\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{2}{x-1}\right)}{\left(\frac{3}{x}+5\right)} \cdot \frac{x(x-1)}{x(x-1)}\\ &= \frac{\frac{1}{\cancel{x}}\cdot \cancel{x}(x-1) - \frac{2}{\cancel{(x-1)}}x\cancel{(x-1)}}{\frac{3}{\cancel{x}}\cdot \cancel{x}(x-1) + 5x(x-1) }\\ & = \frac{(x-1) - 2x}{3(x-1) + 5x^2 - 5x}\\ & = \frac{-x-1}{5x^2 - 5x + 3x - 3}\\ & = \frac{-x-1}{5x^2 - 2x - 3}\\ & = \frac{-x-1}{5x^2 - 5x + 3x - 3}\\ & = \frac{-x-1}{5x(x-1) + 3(x-1)}\\ & =\frac{-(x+1)}{(5x+3)(x-1)} \end{align*}

Simplify the following. \[ \frac{- \frac{3}{2} + \frac{3}{x}}{5 - \frac{1}{x}} \]

Answer:

This time, I'll just use "Method 1," but, really, you can take your pick. First, get a common denominator in the numerator and likewise in the denominator. I combine the fractions in the numerator and do likewise in the denominator. Then I have a little division problem. \begin{align*} \frac{- \frac{3}{2} + \frac{3}{x}}{5 - \frac{1}{x}} &= \frac{-\frac{3x}{2x}+\frac{6}{2x}}{\frac{5x}{x} - \frac{1}{x}}\\ &= \frac{\frac{-3x+6}{2x}}{\frac{5x-1}{x}}\\ & = \frac{-3x+6}{2\cancel{x}}\cdot \frac{\cancel{x}}{5x-1}\\ & = \frac{-3(x-2)}{2(5x-1)} \end{align*}

That's pretty much it! In a way, it isn't that complicated—though it can definitely be messy, annoying, and tedious.
