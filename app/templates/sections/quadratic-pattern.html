Quadratic Pattern

In this section, we learn that even some of the easier factoring strategies we have learned can be extended to cover seemingly more difficult cases. For example, can you imagine that you actually already have a trick for factoring the following? \[ x^{10} - 6x^5 - 16 \] Well, you have multiple tricks! Since there are multiple ways of getting this thing factored, I want you to have first crack at it---to see what ways you might tend to favor and which way might make sense to you most readily.

Activity

Goal: Completely factor \[ x^{10} - 6x^5 - 16 \] Step 1: Build a mental "bridge" between this hard new problem and the old-style problem---which I hope you have mastered--- \[ x^2 - 6x - 16 \] Reflect on how you might solve this. Using a guess-and-check approach, you set up some "empty parentheses": \[ (\quad \quad \quad)(\quad \quad \quad) \] Well, really, you probably already know that you'll need "\(x\) and \(x\)" in there, too: \[ (x \quad \quad)(x \quad \quad) \] And, as you may well know, the idea is to fill in those blanks with numbers that make it all work---which you mentally verify by checking to see how they would all multiply together and simplify out. Now, that may make perfect sense to you already, but, to clarify, one can make up symbols for "this number" and "that number". For instance, you might write \[ (x + {\color{red}A})(x + {\color{blue}B}) \] and explain that what you want is a numbers \({\color{red}A}\) and \({\color{blue}B}\) that multiply together to give you \(-16\) and add together to give you \(-6\). Go ahead and fill in the blanks here to make sure you have the right idea:

Next, I'll work some examples for you that are similar. I'll show you three techniques, and you decide whichever one you prefer. You can use either one.

Example

Completely factor the following. \[ x^6 - 10x^3 + 24 \]

Answer:

Method 1: Notice that this is like \[ w^2 - 10w +24 = (w-6)(w-4) \] After all, \[ x^6 - 10x^3 + 24 = (x^3)^2 - 10(x^3) + 24 \] See the similarity? The point is that, for any real number \(w\) \[ w^2 - 10w +24 = (w-6)(w-4) \] is true. Also, \(x^3\) is (the symbol for) a real number. So, this \(w\) equation is also true when \(x^3\) is substituted in for \(w\): \[ (x^3)^2 - 10x^3 + 24 = (x^3-6)(x^3-4) \] Get it? Since \( (x^3)^2=x^3\cdot x^3 = x^6 \), it's also true that \[ x^6 - 10x^3 + 24 = (x^3-6)(x^3-4) \]

Method 2: Use the "\( ac \)" method. That is, you first find a factorization of \( ac = 1\cdot 24 = 24 \) into two numbers that add up to \(-10\). Those numbers are \(-4\) and \(-6\). Then, rewrite the middle term according to that breakdown and then factor by grouping: \begin{align*} x^6 - 10x^3 + 24 &= x^6 - 4x^3 - 6x^3 + 24 \\ &= x^3(x^3 - 4) - 6(x^3 - 4) \\ & = (x^3-6)(x^3 - 4) \end{align*}

Method 3: Guess and Check. Guessing-and-checking might look like this: \[ x^6 - 10x^3 + 24 = (x^3 - \quad)(x^3 - \quad) \] You just sit there and think about how to fill in the blanks: You'd need two numbers that multiply together to give you \(24\) and add together to give you \(-10\)---which is the same thing you had to think about when factoring, say, \(w^2 - 10w + 24\). So, yup, \(-4\) and \(-6\) are the numbers: \[ x^6 - 10x^3 + 24 = (x^3 - 4)(x^3 - 6) \] Last step: Scrutinize the resulting factors, namely \(x^3-4\) and \(x^3 - 6\) and see if there any factoring methods that will apply. Have we learned anything that will apply to those? Nope! So, we're done.

Would you like to see one more without all the talk?

Example

Completely factor the following. \[ x^8 +7x^4 - 8 \]

Answer

\begin{align*} x^8 +7x^4 - 8 & = (x^4 - \quad)(x^4 + \quad)\\ & = (x^4 - 1)(x^4 +8) \\ & = (x^2 - 1)(x^2+1)(x^4 + 8)\\ & = (x-1)(x+1)(x^2 +1)(x^4 + 8) \end{align*}
