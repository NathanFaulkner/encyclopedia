One of the uses that functions are put to is to form "links in a chain." We have already discussed functions as machines taking an input and yielding an output. Now, there is nothing stopping us from using the output of one function as the input of another machine down the line. Think of it as like an assembly line. Indeed, you do this all the time when you think about "order of operations" questions.

There is some notation and a piece of vocabulary associated with this, but first let's look at a picture:

Illustrate the function described as follows in English as two functions "joined end-to-end", that is, with one function taking the output of the previous as its input:

The first function \(g\) adds one to the input, and the second function \(f\) multiplies its input by 5.

Answer:

When we join functions assembly-line fashion like this, it is called composition. The functions \(g\) and \(f\) above have been composed. There are different ways to denote composition notationally, but let's consider one important way. The expression \[ y = f(g(x)) \] is a way of denoting the basic picture we just saw. The way you read the expression "\(y=f(g(x))\)" is "y is f of g of x" and what it means is this: the output \(y\) is determined by first taking the input \(x\) and plugging it into the function \(g\). The resulting output, say \(u=g(x)\), is then plugged into the \(f\) function. Look again at the first illustration above. The scenario described here is exactly what is being depicted there!

Let's practice.

Let \(f(x) = x - 1\) and \(g(x) = 6x\). Compute the indicated values.

  1. \(f(g(2))\)

    Answer:

    First I compute the value of \(g(2)\): \[ g(2) = 6\cdot 2 = 12 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(g(2)\) in the expression \(f(g(2))\): \[ f(g(2)) = f(12) = 12 - 1 = 11 \] That's all there is to it!
  2. \(f(g(-1))\)

    Answer:

    First I compute the value of \(g(-1)\): \[ g(-1) = 6\cdot (-1) = -6 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(g(-1)\) in the expression \(f(g(-1))\): \[ f(g(-1)) = f(-6) = -6 - 1 = -7 \]
  3. \(g(f(-1))\)

    Answer:

    This time, I first compute the value of \(f(-1)\): \[ f(-1) = -1 - 1 = -2 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(f(-1)\) in the expression \(g(f(-1))\): \[ g(f(-1)) = g(-2) = 6(-2) = -12 \]
  4. \(g(g(2))\)

    Answer:

    You can also compose a function with itself! First, I compute the value of \(g(2)\): \[ g(2) = 6\cdot 2 = 12 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(g(2)\) in the expression \(g(g(2))\): \[ g(g(2)) = g(12) = 6 \cdot 12 = 72 \]

That's pretty much it! Except there is a just a litle more we could remark on here. Though we don't have much use for it in this class, there is a symbol for composition. You see, composition involves a combination of functions. In this way it is like addition or multiplication—which involve a combination of numbers. So, just as the addition and multiplication operations have symbols, the operation of composition also gets a symbol. Here it is: \(\circ\). So, the expression \(f \circ g\) is given meaning by the following rule: \[ (f \circ g) (x) = f(g(x)) \] The one reason I point this out is that—once you have this analogy drawn from composition to addition and multiplication—it leads you to ask the natural question: is function composition commutative like addition and multiplication are? That is, as you know, for any real numbers \(a\) and \(b\), you have \begin{align*} a + b &= b + a & \textrm{(Commutativity of Addition)} \end{align*} So, is it true that for all functions \(f\) and \(g\), \(f\circ g = g \circ f\)? The answer is "no", as you can see from the example we just did! You see, we computed both the values of \((f \circ g)(-1)\) and \((g \circ f) (-1) \), and they came out different. Therefore \(f \circ g\) and \(g \circ f\) are different functions: \[ f \circ g \not = g \circ f \]

One of the challenges you will face in these exercises is to mind your order of operations. I am assuming a familiarity with these. But let's do one more example for some practice in that direction.

Let \(f(x) = 1 - (x -2)^2 \) and \(g(x) = 6|x|\). Compute the indicated values.

  1. \(f(g(2))\)

    Answer:

    First I compute the value of \(g(2)\): \[ g(2) = 6\cdot |2| = 6 \cdot 2 = 12 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(g(2)\) in the expression \(f(g(2))\): \[ f(g(2)) = f(12) = 1 - (12 - 2)^2 = 1 - 10^2 = 1 - 100 = -99 \] Note how I carry out these operations in a particular order! You may be familiar with the mnemonic PEMDAS to help you remember this "order of operations". You carry out the operations in the following order:
    1. First, those inside parentheses.
    2. Then, apply exponents.
    3. Up next, you carry out multiplication/division operations.
    4. Lastly, you carry out addition/subtraction.
    One thing this mnemonic omits is how to treat "negation". For the purposes of applying the mnemonic, you can pretend that negation is "multiplying by \(-1\)". However, it is much more correct to say that the reverse is true: multiplication by \(-1\) really just means negation. The point concerning order of operations and negation, though, is that negation fits into the order with multiplication/division. So, you could adopt the adjusted mnemonic "PENDMAS" or "PEMNDAS" or "PEMDNAS"—whichever suits you best—in case it helps you realize how negation fits in.

    You might have some additional questions about whether multiplication and division are interchangeable or, similarly, whether addition and subtraction are interchangeable in terms of the order of operations. The answer is "yes" provided you understand division as "multiplying by a fraction" and subtraction as "adding a negative". But if you view division or subtraction as independent operations with independent symbols, then it is possible to run into trouble. Specifically, neither division nor subtraction as "associative" which means they cannot be treated as freely as multiplication and addition can be. To see what I mean, note that \[ 3 \cdot (4 \cdot 5) = 3 \cdot 20 = 60 = 12 \cdot 5 = (3 \cdot 4) \cdot 5 \] This is an illustration of the associativity of multiplication, which, together with the commutativity of multiplication, means that "you can carry out a product in any order you want". For instance, I could have also done the above product by computing: \[ (3 \cdot 5) \cdot 4 = 15 \cdot 4 = 60 \] Same thing no matter what! But this does not work out with division. Just look here to see what I mean: \[ (60 \div 3) \div 2 = 20 \div 2 = 10 \] while \[ 60 \div (3 \div 2) = 60 \div 1.5 = 40 \] So, division is not associative! Subtraction isn't either, but I'll leave that to you to prove to yourself. Moral: PEMDAS works, but you may have more thinking to do about it. Never shut off yoru brain in math! (Don't just naively "follow the rules.")

  2. \(f(g(-1))\)

    Answer:

    First I compute the value of \(g(-1)\): \[ g(-1) = 6\cdot |-1| = 6 \cdot 1 = 6 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(g(-1)\) in the expression \(f(g(-1))\): \[ f(g(-1)) = f(6) = 1 - (6 - 2)^2 = 1 - 4^2 = 1 - 16 = -15 \]
  3. \(g(f(-1))\)

    Answer:

    This time, I first compute the value of \(f(-1)\): \[ f(-1) = 1 - (-1 - 2)^2 = 1 - (-3)^2 = 1 - 9 = -8 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(f(-1)\) in the expression \(g(f(-1))\): \[ g(f(-1)) = g(-8) = 6 \cdot |-8| = 6 \cdot 8 = 48 \]
  4. \(g(g(2))\)

    Answer:

    First, I compute the value of \(g(2)\): \[ g(2) = 6\cdot |2| = 6 \cdot 2 = 12 \] Then I substitute this value in for the symbol \(g(2)\) in the expression \(g(g(2))\): \[ g(g(2)) = g(12) = 6 \cdot |12| = 6 \cdot 12 = 72 \]
