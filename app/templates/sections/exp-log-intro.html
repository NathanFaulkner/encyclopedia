In this chapter, as well as the next, we study various things we can do with powers, AKA, exponents. Even though the words "power" and "exponent" refer to same thing, there is an important distinction made in the vocabulary by using these two different but synonymous words: "power functions" and "exponential functions" refer to different classes of objects.

A new chapter, a new topic—but one with connections to the one we just studied: Previously, we discussed power functions. Again:

A power function \(f\) is any whose outputs can be obtained by a formula of the form \[ f(x) = Ax^p \] where \(p\) and \(A\) can be any real number. For example, \[ f(x) = x^2 \] and \[ g(x) = x^\frac{1}{3} = \sqrt[3]{x} \] are both power functions.

Of course, what we ended up studying, as well, was transformations of these. In this chapter, we study, first of all, exponential functions. Clearly, these will have something to do with exponents—but so did power functions! In fact, let's agree right from the start that "power" and "exponent" are just two words for the same thing. But, as you will see, "power function" and "exponential function" have two very distinct meanings—even while they both have something to do with exponents. So, pay attention to the following distinction:

An exponential function \(f\) is any whose outputs can be obtained by a formula of the form \[ f(x) = Ab^{x} \] where \(A\) can be any real number but \(b\) we will restrict to positive numbers (actually, 0 and 1 aren't very much worth considering either—but allowing negative numbers for \(b\) opens up a can of worms). For example, \[ f(x) = 2^x \] and \[ g(x) =\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^x \] are both exponential functions.

In words, the difference between exponential functions and power functions has to do with where the input appears: the input is being raised to a power/exponent in a power function but in an exponential function, the input is the power/exponent that a given, fixed number is being raised to. Okay—that last sentence was a mouthful: Wouldn't it be nice to have a shorter way of saying "the number that is being raised to some exponent?"

Given an expression \[ b^a \] you call \(a\) the exponent (or, power, if you like), whereas "that number \(b\) that is being raised to the power" is called the base. In particular, if you have an exponential function \[ f(x) = A b^x \] then you call \(b\) the base of the exponential. For example, \(2\) is the base of \[ f(x) = 2^x \]

A very nice way to draw a contrast between power functions and exponential functions is gotten by comparing their graphs. So, that is how we begin.
