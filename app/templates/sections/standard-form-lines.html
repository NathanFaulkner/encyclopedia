Here is a quick recap of the important forms we have learned: \begin{equation*}\tag{Slope-intercept Form} y = mx+b \end{equation*} \begin{equation*}\tag{Point-slope Form} y = m(x-x_0)+y_0 \end{equation*} In this section, we learn yet another format for equations of lines—called standard form—and how best to use this new format to produce a graph. We also learn what is nice about standard form. (We will also use this form in another context later.)

An equation for a line given in the form \[ ax+by = c \] is said to be in standard form. Again, \(x\) and \(y\) will remain "variables", but, in the context of a specific problem, \(a\) and \(b\) will be filled in as known, specific quantities.

How do you graph the equation when it is given in standard form?

Graph the equation \[ 3x - 2y = 6 \]

Answer:

Here is your method: Note that any two points determines the line. So, you just have to pick two points that are very easy to plot. Like what? Well, it's easy to plug in \(x=0\) and then see what \(y\) will have to be—namely, \(-3\)—and then plug in \(y=0\) and see what \(x\) will have to be: 2. That is, mentally, you can note that the pairs \((0, -3)\) and \((2,0)\) will lie on the graph (since they satisfy the equation).

Let's see one more example.

Graph the equation \[ -35x - 25y = 175 \]

Answer:

Here is your method: Note that any two points determines the line. So, you just have to pick two points that are very easy to plot. Like what? Well, it's easy to plug in \(x=0\) and then see what \(y\) will have to be: \begin{align*} &-35 \cdot 0 - 25y = 175\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -25y = -175\\ \Rightarrow \quad & y = \frac{-175}{-25}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & y = 7 \end{align*} So, the graph must pass through the point \((0, 7)\). Next, plug in \(y=0\) and see what \(x\) will have to be: \begin{align*} &-35 x - 25 \cdot 0 = 175\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -35x = -175\\ \Rightarrow \quad & y = \frac{-175}{-35}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 5 \end{align*} Thus, the graph will have to pass through the point \((5, 0)\), as well. Finally, just plot those points, and "connect the dots"!
