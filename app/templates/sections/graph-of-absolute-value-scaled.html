This is a continuation of the work begun in the section of graphs of absolute value functions (basic transformations). The difference in this section is that we will be adding in one more type of transformation—as well as combining them with the other three seen in the previous section. In particular, while we already considered transformations of \(y = |x|\) involving horizontal shifts, vertical shifts, and reflections across the \(x\)-axis, we will now consider "scaling" of the \(y\)-values. This won't necessarily be harder than what we did in the previous section—it's just that it can be confusing to consider everything at once when you are starting out with the concept of transformations, so I have saved part of the story until now. (Actually, I will also be saving yet more until a later time, too.)

Graph the function \[ y = 2|x| \]

Plot both the graph of \(y=|x|\) and \(y=2|x|\) on the same set of axes. Use different colors for each. Plot points if you have to, but think about the pattern that emerges and why it makes sense.

Answer:

The first thing to realize is what the meaning of this expression even is. You see, you have a '2' written write next to \(|x|\). (To use a fancy English-class word, you could say that the '2' and the \(|x|\) are "juxtaposed", meaning they are written right next to each other with nothing in between.) What does that mean? Well, in algebra, it is important to realize that when there is a "missing" symbol like this, the operation that is being communicated is multiplication. For instance, you can just write \(4x\) and what you mean is "4 times \(x\)". You could have also included a dot for the multiplication sign, as in \(4 \cdot x\), but that's optional, because your reader is expected to know what it means either way (provided it is explained to them at some point!) Similarly, I could have written \(2\cdot |x|\) above, but the expression \(2|x|\) has the same meaning.

Now that that's out of the way, think about what happens when you plug in some different values of \(x\). If you plug in 0, you get \[ y = 2|0| = 2 \cdot 0 = 0 \] If you plug in, say, 3, you get \[ y = 2|3| = 2 \cdot 3 = 6 \] You probably get the idea! So, all that we're doing is taking the \(y\)-value that comes out of the expression \(|x|\) and doubling it. Easy enough! Now, the thing to contemplate is what that means for the graph of this new, transformed function. Since \(y = 0\) when \(x = 0\) but then is positive for any other value of \(x\), the vertex (the lowest point) of the graph will still be at \((0,0)\)—the same as with \(y = |x|\). But, then, for each unit you travel away from there in the \(x\)-direction, the \(y\)-value will grow at double the rate of that distance—which makes it "rise" at double the rate of the graph of \(y = |x|\). That means it will look a lot like \(y = |x|\), but will just rise more steeply:

Note that I used gray for the graph of \(x = |x|\) (for comparison purposes) and blue for the final answer, that is, the graph of \(y = 2|x|\). Because of the visual effect created, this transformation thus exhibited is often said to be "vertically stretching by a factor of 2". In the next example, we will see "vertical shrinking". A word that can encompass both "stretching" and "shrinking" is "scaling".

The concept of "slope" should come to mind here! It is kind of like the slope of \(y = 2|x|\) is \(2,\) whereas the slope of \(y = |x|\) is \(1\). The only problem with that is that neither of those graphs should be said to have just one slope in the strictest sense. After all, "slope" should be understood as the proportional change in \(y\) as \(x\) runs to the right. So, in that strict sense, \(y = |x|\) has a negative slope for \(x \lt 0\) and a positive slope for \(x \gt 0\). So, it doesn't have a single slope, but two different slopes on the different parts of the graph. But, otherwise, thinking in terms of "slope" is pretty helpful!

As a final recap, it can be said that for the function \( y = 2|x| \) the \(y\)'s start at 0 for \(x = 0\) and then \(y\) increases in a 2 to 1 ratio no matter what direction you go (positive is the same as negative) from there. That's just a restatement of the formula in English, but, in my opinion, also serves to express exactly what the graph looks like.

Graph the function \[ y = - \frac{3}{5} \left|{x - 4}\right| + 5 \]

Answer:

You can either try to analyze separately what each part of what you are seeing in this formula means in terms of its graph—to note each transformation separately and then also to execute them in the proper order—or one can try to narrate what the function does in a natural way. Or you can try to both, and one will reinforce the other! (Of course, that's what I recommend.)

If you note the transformations one at a time working in an "inside to out" fashion, you will be doing pretty good. This would go as follows:
\(y = |x - 4|\)Shift right 4 units
\(y = -\frac{3}{5}|x-4|\)\(y\)'s will fall by 3 for every 5 you travel away from \(x = 4\)
\(y = -\frac{3}{5}|x - 4| + 5\)Shift up 5
Because of the shift right 4 and up 5, I know the vertex will be at \((4, 5)\) (right 4 and up 5 from the origin). And because of the coefficient of \(- \frac{3}{5}\), I know that the rate of change of \(y\) will be "down 3 \(y\)'s for every 5 over in \(x\)". Because of the absolute value, that means that sort of change will happen in the same way whether we go to the left of 4 or to the right of it. So, the graph looks like so:

Maybe all of that made sense, but let's try another way of narrating what is going on. This other way of narrating really involves rewriting the formula slightly, like so: \[ y = 5 - \frac{3}{5}|x - 4| \] So, here is what I can say: The \(y\)'s start at 5, and then proceed downwards from there—which takes me this far into the formula: \[ y = 5 - \phantom{x} \] For what follows next—the part that involves \(x\)—I see that \(y\) will change depending on what \(x\) is. I see that \(y\) will go down by 3 for every 5 that \(x\) gets away from \(x = 4.\) That means that, whether you go left or right of \(x = 4\), the \(y\)'s will change in a "down 3, over 5" sort of fashion. Note that \(|x-4|\) gives me "the absolute value of the difference between number \(x\) and 4", which is literally the distance (which can't be negative) of whatever \(x\) anyone wants to consider to 4. And the \(-\frac{3}{5}\) is functioning kind of like slope, albeit in a new or generalized sense of the word, as opposed to how it is strictly defined in the context of a single line.

Answer:

I note that the vertex is at \((-1, -5)\). I use that as a starting point for both a narrative of what happens in the graph as well as in developing the equation. In particular, I can start by saying "the \(y\)'s start at \(-5\) and then go up from there". So, it will work to start by writing: \[ y = -5 + \phantom{x} \] Next, I see that for every 4 you trace away from \(x = -1\)—whether to the left or to the right—the \(y\)'s by 1. So, that's like a "rise over run" of \(\frac{1}{4}\)— but, again, in such a way that I'll get \(y = -5\) when \(x = 1\). So, I continue: \[ y = - 5 + \frac{1}{4} \left|{x - (-1)}\right| = -5 + \frac{1}{4} \left|{x + 1}\right| \] Of course, you could also write this as \[ y = \frac{1}{4} \left|{x + 1}\right| - 5 \] (The answer checker will take anything mathematically equivalent to the right answer!—meaning, it doesn't have to be in one format or another.)
