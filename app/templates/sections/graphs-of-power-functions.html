In this section, we'll take a look at the graphs of a few more power functions and then transform them (shift them left, right, up, or down; reflect them; scale them vertically.) Actually, we'll just concentrate on four of them for now:

  • \(y=x^2\)
  • \(y = \sqrt{x}\)
  • \(y = x^3\)
  • \(y = \sqrt[3]{x}\)
One of these, you may have already graphed elsewhere —that being \(y=x^2\). But, in the present section, it will be interesting to note how the graph of \(y=\sqrt{x}\) is related to that of \(y=x^2\) (on account of the connection between the definitions of these functions.) In the same way, we will see that the graphs of \(y=x^3\) and \(y=\sqrt[3]{x}\) are related.

As always when beginning the study of a new graph, we will get our initial ideas of what the graphs look like by "just plotting points." We'll do these in a series of four examples. Actually, this first one is a repeat of an example given in the section on vertex form, for quadratics.

The interesting thing to note here is that we have—in a way—plotted "the same" thing in these two activities: All the pairs appearing on the graph of \(y=x^2\) also appear on the graph of \(y=\sqrt{x}\)—except, they are reversed! I mean, for example, that while \((2,4)\) gets plotted on the graph of \(y=x^2\), \((4,2)\) gets plotted on \(y=\sqrt{x}\), and, while \((3,9)\) gets plotted on \(y=x^2\), \((9,3)\) gets plotted on \(y=\sqrt{x}\). There is a special word for this act of "reversing" the pairs. Actually, the word used by mathematicians is inverse.

Let \[ \mathcal S = \left\{(x_1, y_1),(x_2, y_2), \ldots \right\} \] be a set of ordered pairs. Then its inverse, denoted \(\mathcal S^{-1}\), is just what you get when you reverse all of the pairs—simple as that! To wit, \[ \mathcal S^{-1} = \left\{(y_1, x_1),(y_2, x_2), \ldots \right\} \] For example, we know that \[ \mathcal S = \left\{(1,1), (2, 4),(3, 9)\right\} \] are points that appear on the graph of \(y=x^2\). Well, the inverse of this set of pairs appears on \(y=\sqrt{x}\). That is, \[ \mathcal S^{-1} = \left\{(1,1), (4, 2),(9, 3)\right\} \] is a subset of the pairs on the graph \(y=\sqrt{x}\).

By the way, as it becomes annoying to say again and again the phrase "set of ordered pairs," there is a word for this as well.

A set of ordered pairs can be called a relation for short.
