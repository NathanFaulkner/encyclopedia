In this section, we'll take a look at the graphs of a few more power functions and then transform them (shift them left, right, up, or down; reflect them; scale them vertically.) Actually, we'll just concentrate on four of them for now:

  • \(y=x^2\)
  • \(y = \sqrt{x}\)
  • \(y = x^3\)
  • \(y = \sqrt[3]{x}\)
One of these, you may have already graphed elsewhere —that being \(y=x^2\). But, in the present section, it will be interesting to note how the graph of \(y=\sqrt{x}\) is related to that of \(y=x^2\) (on account of the connection between the definitions of these functions.) In the same way, we will see that the graphs of \(y=x^3\) and \(y=\sqrt[3]{x}\) are related.

As always when beginning the study of a new graph, we will get our initial ideas of what the graphs look like by "just plotting points." We'll do these in a series of four examples. Actually, this first one is a repeat of an example given in the section on vertex form, for quadratics.

You are going to "plot points" until you realize what the graph of \(y=x^2\) looks like. You should repeat this activity on your own whenever you forget what it looks like! However, I want you to know up front that this is not the method I suggest you use on any of the exercises in this section. Instead, this is a tedious method that you do to just get started somewhere. We will adopt a much more stream-lined method in the examples below—but only after going through the tedious, point-by-point process at least once.

All you have to do is to plug in a nice variety of \(x\) values (plug in 0, plug in some positive numbers, plug in some negative numbers), and keep going until a light bulb goes on for you—and you are utterly certain what the graph looks like.

Since this may be your first time in doing such a task, I will guide you through it. But in the future, I expect you to be able to plot points on your own.

Explain how you can know how to connect the dots.

    Answer:

    If you feel somewhat at a loss as to how to answer this question, that's a good thing! In the context of first learning about quadratics, we don't have a lot at our disposal to answer it. However, one thing you might remark on is what \(x^2\) might represent: it would be the area of a square whose sides are length \(x\). So, what we are doing when we graph this is visualizing how the area of such a square would grow as the side lengths grow (imagining some sort of expanding square.) Intuitively, you can imagine how the area would grow in a kind of steady fashion as \(x\) grows, so, without any reason to the contrary, it seems natural to connect the dots in a smooth fashion. If that seems vague and a little suspiciously subjective for mathematics, than that is to your credit as a math student! A more thorough explanation of how and why you can "connect the dots" is better left until to your calculus course. But, for now, you'll be heading in the direction of calculus to imagine the "growing square". (Calculus can be described as the study of how things change and, furthermore, as a set of techniques for considering how very small changes in one variable affect changes in a second one. The interesting thing to note here is that we have—in a way—plotted "the same" thing in these two activities: All the pairs appearing on the graph of \(y=x^2\) also appear on the graph of \(y=\sqrt{x}\)—except, they are reversed! I mean, for example, that while \((2,4)\) gets plotted on the graph of \(y=x^2\), \((4,2)\) gets plotted on \(y=\sqrt{x}\), and, while \((3,9)\) gets plotted on \(y=x^2\), \((9,3)\) gets plotted on \(y=\sqrt{x}\). There is a special word for this act of "reversing" the pairs. Actually, the word used by mathematicians is inverse.

\label{def: inverse} Let \[ \mathcal S = \left\{(x_1, y_1),(x_2, y_2), \ldots \right\} \] be a set of ordered pairs. Then its inverse, denoted \(\mathcal S^{-1}\), is just what you get when you reverse all of the pairs—simple as that! To wit, \[ \mathcal S^{-1} = \left\{(y_1, x_1),(y_2, x_2), \ldots \right\} \] For example, we know that \[ \mathcal S = \left\{(1,1), (2, 4),(3, 9)\right\} \] are points that appear on the graph of \(y=x^2\). Well, the inverse of this set of pairs appears on \(y=\sqrt{x}\). That is, \[ \mathcal S^{-1} = \left\{(1,1), (4, 2),(9, 3)\right\} \] is a subset of the pairs on the graph \(y=\sqrt{x}\).

By the way, as it becomes annoying to say again and again the phrase "set of ordered pairs," there is a word for this as well.

A set of ordered pairs can be called a relation for short.

How does this work? If you're perplexed, start thinking about plugging in actual values for \(x\): what sorts of values would you want to plug in for \(x\) now? Well, note that you don't want to plug in 1, 4, or 9, because that negative under the radical would be forcing you to contend with the questions like "What number times itself is \(-1\)?" We know the answer to that (it's the imaginary number \(i\)) and, since we are graphing, here—that is, plotting coordinates that line up with points on the real \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes, we don't care to mess with imaginary numbers: There is no place for them on the graph, right? So, instead, what you want to think about is plugging in negative numbers: \(-1\), \(-4\), \(-9\)—to make it come out positive underneath the square root sign. \begin{minipage}{0.45\textwidth} Indeed, because of the negative on the \(x\), the "old \(y\)-values" will now instead be associated with the \(x\)-values on the other side of the \(y\)-axis—the negative counterparts of "the old \(x\)-values." In a phrase, we have
  • reflected across the \(y\)-axis.
Be sure to include the dots, as I have, so that I can see you have an accurate knowledge of the graph. \end{minipage}\hfill \begin{minipage}{0.5\textwidth} \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{power-of-x-transformations-example-3} \end{minipage}
