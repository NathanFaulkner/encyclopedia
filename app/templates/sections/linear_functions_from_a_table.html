Here is a quick bit of vocab:

A function \(f\) is said to be a linear function provided its outputs can be obtained via a rule of the form \[ f(x) = mx+ b \]
We have remarked elsewhere that number \(m\) is often called "slope" and the number \(b\) is called the \(y\)-intercept.

What we are going to do in this section is find formulas for some real-world linear functions based on some data presented in a narrative or in a table. Actually, this is something you may have already done in the section on basic functions from a table and words It's just that, now—after having practiced the various skills for developing the equation for a line based on some information about its graph—we can tackle problems that we might have previously found to be difficult.

What we're looking at in this table is the pressure experienced by an object found at different depths under the ocean. And, by the way, these numbers are basically accurate! That is, this is actually how the numbers would go if you were to take pressure measurements at different depths.

  1. Give an English description of how pressure is related to depth. Be detailed enough so that someone could reconstruct all the data in this table without having seen it using just your description.

    Answer:

    The pressure at the surface is already pounds per square inch (air pressure, apparently). And for every additional 10 feet you dive down, you will experience an increase of pressure of 4.5 pounds per square inch.
  2. To start getting a handle on a complicated relationship, it might help to sketch a graph of it. Do so now. Hint: since we are viewing pressure as determined by depth (pressure is a function of depth), we are thinking of pressure as the "output" variable and depth as the input variable. When discussing functions as ordered pairs, the first coordinate in each pair is always the input and the second is the output. Also, on graphs the first coordinate gives the position left to right relative to the origin and the second coordinate gives the position up and down relative to the origin. So, use the vertical axis for pressure and the horizontal axis for depth.

    Answer:

  3. Develop an equation that relates pressure to depth. Use \(p\) for pressure and \(d\) for depth.

    Answer:

    Look at your sketch! Do you see that straight-line relationship coming together? The graph helps the concept of "slope" kind of click into place in our minds. Recall that slope is "rise over run"—or, the amount that \(y\) increases by when \(x\) increases by 1 unit. Recall, too, that, in such a situation, you get a change in \(y\) that is proportional to how much \(x\) changes. That means that, even if you don't know how much \(y\) changes when when \(x\)\increases by 1 unit, as long as you know how \(y\) changes across some corresponding change in \(x\) you can use that instead. What I am talking about here is this: Note that pressure increases by 4.5 pounds per square inch when depth increases by 10 feet. Thus, the slope of our linear function will be \[ m = \frac{4.5}{10} = 0.45 \] We also know the \(y\)-intercept is \(b = 14.7\) pounds per square inch. Thus, using the fact that an equation of the form \[ y = mx + b \] will have a straight line graph, we get our formula: \[ p = 0.45 d + 14.7 \]
  4. As a follow-up to the previous question, compute the pressure at the deepest recorded point in the ocean: the Mariana Trench which sits at 36,070 ft below the surface.

    Answer:

    The nice thing about a formula like the one we just developed is that you can just "plug in" to find whatever value you want. So, for instance, when \(d = 36,070\), we find \[ p = 14.7 + 0.45 d = 14.7 + 0.45 \cdot 36,070 = {{ 14.7 + 0.45*36070 }} \] So, the pressure at the bottom of the Mariana Trench will be around 16,242.2 pounds per square inch. (You can look up online what actual recorded pressures have been at that depth. You see that our estimate is close, but a little off. Why is that? One reason is that I have used rounded figures in constructing the table and hence our model. If one used more decimal places in the recorded data, one would get better accuracy.) By the way, how do you suppose they obtain the depth of the water at such a deep place, anyway?

Here is somewhat accurate but partially contrived average low temperatures for December as you pass in altitude from Winston-Salem up to Boone in North Carolina. Temperatures are measured in Fahrenheit and altitude is measured in feet.

{% for i in range(0,9) %} {% endfor %} {% for i in range(0,9) %} {% endfor %}
Altitude (in ft){{ 960 + 300*i }}
Temperature (in F){{ 34 - 1.25*i }}
  1. Write an English sentence that perfectly captures the relationship between the average December low temperatures and the altitude. Someone else should be able to reconstruct the table using just your English sentence.

    Answer:

    The average December low is 34 F at 960 ft (which is an approximate altitude for places in Lewisville), and it decreases by 1.25 F for every 300 feet in altitude you climb on the way to Boone.

  2. Develop an equation that captures the relationship between average monthly lows and temperature. Use \(y\) for the temperature and \(x\) for the altitude.

    Answer:

    My strategy will be to use "point-slope form", which is discussed in this section of the same name: \[ y = m(x-x_0) + y_0 \] I have plenty of pairs to choose from in my table to use as \( (x_0, y_0) \), so all I need to figure out is the slope, \(m\). As we have discussed, slope is "the change in \(y\) when \(x\) increases by 1 unit". We have also remarked on the fact that, with these straight line (constant rate of change) relationships, whenever \(x\) changes by some given amount other than 1, \(y\) will change by a proportional amount. So, we can figure out that constant rate of change by doing "rise over run" for any run (it doesn't have to be 1). We can see that, as altitude increases by 300 ft, we get a constant drop of 1.25 F for that average December low. So, the slope is \[ m = \frac{-1.25}{300} \] You could simplifiy this (like we did in the depth and pressure example just above), but it really isn't necessary to. In all, our equation is \[ y = -\frac{1.25}{300}(x - 960) + 34 \]
  3. Mt. Mitchell is the highest point in North Carolina, sitting proudly at 6,684. Assuming this model of average December low temperature related to altitude also holds as we walk from Lewisville, N.C., (a big "if") to Mt. Mitchell, compute the average December low there.

    Answer:

    We plug in \(x = 6684\): \begin{align*} y &= -\frac{1.25}{300}(x - 960) + 34\\ &= -\frac{1.25}{300}(6684 - 960) + 34\\ &= 10.15 \end{align*} So, we predict the average December low at Mt. Mitchell to be about 10.15 F. (According to my favorite website, Wikipedia, the average December low is 20.6 F, so there is a problem with the accuracy of the model—even if our "math" was without blemish!)

We'll do one more example: a "generic" one, with no context attached. Note that for a "generic" situation, you are not limited to what numbers would make sense for a physical scenario. So, expect to (sometimes) encounter negative numbers here.

Consider the data below in answering the questions that follow.

{% for i in range(0,4) %} {% set disp = -4.6 + 9.8*i %} {% endfor %} {% for i in range(0,4) %} {% set disp = -16.8 + i*7.3 %} {% endfor %}
\(x\){{ '%0.1f' % disp|float }}
\(y\){{ '%0.1f' % disp|float }}
  1. Write an English sentence that perfectly captures the relationship between \(x\) and \(y\). Someone else should be able to reconstruct your table using just your English sentence.

    Answer:

    When \(x = -4.6\), you have \(y = -16.8\), and for every increase of 9.8 in \(x\), \(y\) increases by 7.3.

  2. Develop a "math sentence"—that is, an equation—that captures the relationship between \(y\) and \(x\).

    Answer:

    Paralleling my sentence above exactly, I start off with what is really the subject of my sentence, \(y\). \[ y = \] Next up, I know \(y\) starts out at \(-16.8\) and grows from there, so I write: \[ y = -16.8 + \phantom{x} \] How does it grow? (Note here that I might also ask you a question where \(y\) decreases with increasing \(x\) instead of increases!) Each time \(x\) increases by of 9.8, \(y\) increases by 7.3. To get how far (and in what direction) from the reference point of \(x_0 = -4.6\) any arbitrary \(x\) is, you subtract: \[ y = -16.8 + \frac{?}{?} (x - (-4.6)) \] I have also left a blank (fraction) to prepare for the next step: Because I know \(y\) will have to increase by 7.3 for every increase of 9.8 in \(x\), I need to see how many units of length 9.8 the value of \(x - (-4.6) = x + 4.6\) represents. That is, I need to see how many times 9.8 goes into that value. That's division! So, I divide by 9.8: \[ y = -16.8 + \frac{?}{9.8} (x + 4.6) \] Now, I need \(y\) to increase by 7.3 for every 9.8 that increases by, so, my plus sign is correct, and I just need to work in that concept of repeated addition—by multiplying: \[ y = -16.8 + \frac{7.3}{9.8} (x + 4.6) \]

    The foregoing is how I like to narrate such problems to myself—to make sure it all makes sense and that I have processed the information faithfully—but you can also just use "point-slope form": \[ y = m(x - x_0) + y_0 \] For \(x_0, y_0\), I can just choose the initial pair, \((-4.6, -16.8)\). For the slope, \(m\), I can just do "rise over run" as I go from the point \((-4.6, -16.8)\) to the next one of \((5.2, -9.5)\). \begin{align*} m &= \frac{-9.5 - (-16.8)}{5.2 - (-4.6)}\\ &= \frac{7.3}{9.8} \end{align*} On the other hand, it may be easier to compute the "rise" and "run" using some later pair of points. The same values should come from any comparison of adjacent points—or else the data cannot be modeled by a linear function at all!

  3. Compute the value of \(y\) for where \(x = -20.3\)

    Answer:

    We plug in \(x = -20.3\): \begin{align*} y &= -16.8 + \frac{7.3}{9.8} (x + 4.6) \\ & = -16.8 + \frac{7.3}{9.8} (-20.3 + 4.6)\\ & \approx {{ -16.8 + 7.3/9.8*(-20.3 + 4.6) }} \end{align*} On that last step, you could use a calculator—or, you will find—the "answer checker" on the exercises page doesn't mind if you enter things unsimplified. It has a calculator (and more!) backing up the answer-checking algorithm, so just treat it like your calculator command line—where you are having to learn a few keyboard buttons that replace those on your hand-held calculator.
