In this section, we solve power equations again but this time where the powers are fractions. In fact, the powers will often be presented in radical form. Let's get right to it.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^\frac{1}{3} = 2 \]

Answer:

In words, this equation poses the question, "What number has 2 as its one-third factor?" Put another way, "What number can you break into three equal factors and the result will be 2?" Hopefully, when that sinks in correctly, you'll realize the answer is "8." After all, \[ 8^\frac{1}{3} = 2 \] But perhaps you would like some sort of "process" to follow? One way to narrate how to solve such an equation is to realize the old truth that "When you are solving an equation, you can do whatever you like as long as you do it to both sides," thus deducing a simpler equation that is a consequence of the first. In this case, "the thing you can do to both sides" that will be helpful is to cube both sides—that is, to raise both to the third power: \begin{align*} &x^\frac{1}{3} = 2\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x^\frac{1}{3}\right)^3 = 2^3\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 8 \end{align*}

Here's another equally simple example. After this we'll start to make them a little more complicated:

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ \sqrt{x}= 5 \]

Answer:

This time the equation says "the square root of what number is 5?" Perhaps you can intuit the answer: 25. But, again, what process can you follow here? Well, to get rid of the square root, you can "square both sides": \begin{align*} & \sqrt{x}= 5\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(\sqrt{x}\right)^2 = 5^2\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 25 \end{align*} Note that \(\left(\sqrt{x}\right)^2 = \sqrt{x} \sqrt{x} = x\)! That's really the whole point of square roots: they serve to break apart a number into two equal factors.

The next couple of examples only spices things up slightly.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ \sqrt{x-1} + 5 = 17 \]

Answer:

Don't forget about your usual techniques! You can do whatever you want as long as you do it to both sides. I choose to subtract 5 from both sides, and then go from there. \begin{align*} & \sqrt{x-1} + 5 = 17 & \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \sqrt{x-1} = 12 & \textrm{Subtract 5 from both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(\sqrt{x-1}\right)^2 = 12^2 & \textrm{Square both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 1 = 144 & \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 145 & \textrm{Add 1 to both sides}\\ \end{align*}

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ 2x^{\frac{1}{4}} - 4 = 10 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 2x^{\frac{1}{4}} - 4 = 10 & \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x^{\frac{1}{4}} = 14 & \textrm{Add 4 to both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^{\frac{1}{4}} = 7 & \textrm{Divide both sides by 2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x^\frac{1}{4}\right)^4 = 7^4 & \textrm{Raise both sides to the power 4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 7^4 = 2401& \textrm{Simplify}\\ \end{align*} Regarding the very last step of writing \(7^4 = 2401\), this is just something you ask your calculator. Enter exponents like so: '7^4'.

Here is one more; then we have to make them a little weirder still.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ 3\left(x - 5\right)^\frac{1}{3} + 7 = 28 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 3\left(x - 5\right)^\frac{1}{3} + 7 = 28 & \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3\left(x - 5\right)^\frac{1}{3} = 21 & \textrm{Subtract 7 from both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x - 5\right)^\frac{1}{3} = 7 & \textrm{Divide both sides by 3}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(\left(x - 5\right)^\frac{1}{3}\right)^3 = 7^3 & \textrm{Raise both sides to the power 3}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 5 = 2401& \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 2406& \textrm{Add 5 to both sides}\\ \end{align*}

Next, we take alook at what to do when the fraction involved isn't quite so simple: What happens when it isn't just 1 in the numerator of the power? For starters, recall that we made the following definition in the section on rational exponents \begin{equation}\label{multiple-fractional-power}\tag{1} a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \left(a^\frac{1}{n}\right)^m, \end{equation} where \(a\) is an appropriate real number and \(n\) and \(m\) are positive integers. For example, \[ 8^\frac{2}{3} = \left(8^\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = 2^2 = 4 \]

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^\frac{2}{3} = 16 \]

Answer:

In words, this equation poses the question, "16 is two third factors of what number?" In that's confusing, you may want to use the definition I just sited. You can proceed as follows: \begin{align*} & x^\frac{2}{3} = 16 & \\ & \left(x^\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = 16 & \textrm{Using the definition in Equation (\ref{multiple-fractional-power}) above} \end{align*} Now, we see that the square of \(x^\frac{1}{3}\) is 16—where \(x^\frac{1}{3}\) is some number whose identity we don't know yet. So, ask yourself, what number has a square of 16? Of course, 4 does—except that \(-4\) does, too. Therefore \begin{align*} & \left(x^\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = 16 & \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x^\frac{1}{3} = 4 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x^\frac{1}{3} = -4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = 4^3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = (-4)^3 & \textrm{Cube both sides} \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = 64 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -64 & \textrm{Simplify} \\ \end{align*}

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^\frac{3}{4} = 27 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & x^\frac{3}{4} = 27 & \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x^\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 = 27 & \textrm{Using the definition in Equation (\ref{multiple-fractional-power}) above} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^\frac{1}{4}= 3 & \textrm{because \(3^3\) is \(27\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 3^4 & \textrm{Raise both sides by the power 4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 81 & \textrm{Simplify} \\ \end{align*}

Now, it doesn't always have to come out so nicely:

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^\frac{4}{3} = 10 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & x^\frac{4}{3} = 10 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x^\frac{1}{3}\right)^4 = 10 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^\frac{1}{3}= 10^\frac{1}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x^\frac{1}{3}= -10^\frac{1}{4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x= \left(10^\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \left(-10^\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 \end{align*} Note that if you cube a negative, you get a negative. In particular, \[ \left(-10^\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 = \left(-10^\frac{1}{4}\right) \left(-10^\frac{1}{4}\right) \left(-10^\frac{1}{4}\right) = -\left(10^\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 \] Also, you can again use the definition in Equation (\ref{multiple-fractional-power}) to make the following change of notation: \[ \left(10^\frac{1}{4}\right)^3 = 10^{\frac{3}{4}} \] Therefore, we can rewrite our answer to the previous problem as follows: \[ x = 10^\frac{3}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -10^\frac{3}{4} \] I hope that makes you a little pleased, because it means that we could have simplified that whole process—provided we are a little careful: \begin{align*} & x^\frac{4}{3} = 10 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x^\frac{4}{3}\right)^\frac{3}{4} = \pm 10^\frac{3}{4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^{\frac{4}{3}\cdot\frac{3}{4}} = \pm 10^\frac{3}{4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \pm 10^\frac{3}{4}\\ \end{align*}

The only thing to be careful about in the shortcut illustrated at the end there is the "\(\pm\)". Ask yourself—how in the world were you supposed to know that either answer (positive or negative) would work? You'd only know it was there if you knew the meaning of \(x^\frac{4}{3} = \left(x^\frac{1}{3}\right)^4\): the \(x\)-value that you ultimately find will be getting raised to the fourth power—an even power—in the original equation. That means that it doesn't matter if \(x\) is negative or positive; you get two answers. The moral is that if there is an even integer in the numerator of the power on a variable then it is really being raised to an even power—so there will be two possible answers, a positive one and a negative one. Now, we just combine this new concept with various other minor complications involving adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing—the usual stuff.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ - 10 \left(x + 6\right)^{\frac{4}{3}} - 3 = -163 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & - 10 \left(x + 6\right)^{\frac{4}{3}} - 3 = -163 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 10 \left(x + 6\right)^{\frac{4}{3}}= -160 & \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x + 6\right)^{\frac{4}{3}}= 16 & \\ \color{blue}\Rightarrow \quad & x + 6 = 16^\frac{3}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 6 = -16^\frac{3}{4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 6 = 8 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 6 = -8\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 2 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -14\\ \end{align*} In that blue step, I raised both sides to power \(\frac{3}{4}\). I had to take care, though, because this really involves contemplating the quarter-factor of a number—and then multiplying that by itself three times. I know that there are two ways to break apart 16 into 4 equal factors (using real numbers)—using 2 and using \(-2\): \[ 16 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = (-2) \cdot (-2) \cdot (-2) \cdot (-2) \] Therefore, I know \(x+6\) could be \( 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2\) or \((-2) \cdot (-2) \cdot (-2)\). Again, be careful if your original power has an even number in its numerator: There will be two solutions!

Lastly, we need to cover the case where there is no solution—no real number that makes the equation true when substituted in for \(x\). Here is a pretty simple example.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ 4 \sqrt{x} - 9 = -37 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &4 \sqrt{x} - 9 = -37\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 4 \sqrt{x} = -28\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \sqrt{x} = -7\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \textrm{No Solution}\\ \end{align*} Why is there no solution? Note that the square root of a number \(x\) is defined to be the positive real number \(a\) so that \(a\cdot a = x\). Put another way, a square root of a number must be positive.

The basic concern in the previous example is already well-illustrated: A square root—in fact an even root of a number is defined to be positive. Here is a more complicated instance of this issue:

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ \left(x + 10\right)^{\frac{3}{4}} = -6 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &\left(x + 10\right)^{\frac{3}{4}} = -6 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \textrm{No solution} \end{align*} Here, I am recognizing the fact that the \(\frac{1}{4}\) factor (or \(4^\textrm{th}\) root) of a number—such as a\(x+10\)—is positive. The \(\frac{3}{4}\) factor of a number is just the \(\frac{1}{4}\)-factor multiplied by itself 3 times—which would still come out positive. So, there is no way for \((x+10)^\frac{3}{4}\) to come out to a negative number. If this seems hard to realize, you could also discover there is no solution by checking the one answer you might have thought was right.
