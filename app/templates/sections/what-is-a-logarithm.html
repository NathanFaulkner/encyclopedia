What is a logarithm? We shall soon find out—by first considering questions that naturally arise out of our study of exponential functions.

The point of logarithms can be seen when you consider questions of this sort: How long will it take you to reach a particular investment goal? For instance, consider the following activity.

Click for video

Suppose you have $1000, but you wish you had $2000. Of course, you can invest the money, and, in time, you will have that much money. The question is, how long will it take? Say that a bank is offering to pay 3% APR in interest, compounded monthly. How long will it take you to earn that much money? To figure it out, you write down everything you know about this situation; you write down an equation. Recall the formula: \[ A(t) = P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \] In this case, we know \[ A(t) = 1000\left(1+\frac{0.03}{12}\right)^{12t} \] This time, I chose 3% just so you could rewrite \(1+\frac{r}{n}\) in a slightly more convenient way: \[ 1+\frac{r}{n}= 1 + \frac{0.03}{12} =1 + .0025 = 1.0025 \] We want to find out when (for what \(t\)) \(A(t) = 2000\). So, really, what we need is to solve the following equation: \[ 2000 = 1000 \cdot 1.0025^{12t} \] We want to know what real number \(t\) will make this true. This is a different sort of equation than we have previously encountered, but it's still just an equation; we can still simplify it in the usual ways. For instance, we could divide both sides by 1000. Here is what we get: \[ 2 = 1.0025^{12t} \] Now, at this point, we could observe that we need to know the answer to this question: "What exponent can you raise 1.0025 to to hit the target 2." If we can figure that out, then we can reason that \(t\) will be that exponent divided by 12. Do you see why I say that? So, as a preliminary task for this section, I want you to repeatedly guess-and-check with your calculator until you find an exponent that will work (or, if you like, a \(t\) that will work). What exponent can you raise 1.0025 to to hit the target 2? Guess and check to find out.

So, guess-and-checking can be kind of fun, but it can also take a while. Lucky for us, the calculator has an algorithm for answering just the sort of question that we considered in this previous activity; all we need to know is how to ask it.

Now, think about it as a general notion: How does one interact with a computer (which your calculator certainly is)? The way a human interacts with a computer—and, vice versa, a computer interacts with a human—is always through the interchange of symbols. The symbols mean something to you (if they didn't, the computer would be unintelligible and useless to you, as a computer in another language would.) To the computer, the symbols don't mean anything. Instead, they meant something only to the programmer who programmed the computer to offer those symbols under the right circumstances, while the computer can be regarded as an (unsentient) automaton. This is the point: Your job, as the human in this relationship, is to know the meaning of symbols: they are both your way of asking a computer to do something and your way of interpreting the result of the computer's work. It is the computer's job, on the other hand, to do slavish mechanical calculations. The slavish work we want the computer to do in this case is to "Find the exponent that you can raise 1.0025 to to hit the target 2." The symbol, in this case, will involve the word (which is just a symbol itelf) "log." The meaning of this symbol, in other words, is to provide a marker for the act of raising questions of the form "what exponent can I raise a particular given base to to hit a particular given target?" We will see exactly how to use the symbol log to symbolize the answer to such a question in a moment. But first we turn to some simpler questions of this form as a further illustration of the concept.

Consider each of the following questions. Solve each using guess-and-check (enlisting the help of a calculator is fine, but isn't really necessary.)

  1. Solve the equation, \(2^x = 8\).

    Answer:

    \(x = 3\)
  2. Solve the equation \(9^x = 81\)

    Answer:

    \(x = 2\)
  3. The way you might phrase the first question is this: "What exponent can I raise 2 to to hit the target 8?" Answer this question, instead: "What exponent can I raise 2 to to hit the target 32?" Guess and check if you need to.

    Answer:

    You should raise 2 to the 5th power to get 32.
  4. Another way you might phrase the first question is by asking, "8 is worth how many factors of 2?" So, answer this: "27 is worth how many factors of 3?"

    Answer:

    27 is worth 3 factors of 3.
  5. How many factors of 6 is 36 worth?

    Answer:

    36 is worth 2 factors of 6.
  6. Solve the equation \(2^x = \frac{1}{2}\). To put it another way, we regard \(\frac{1}{2}\) as a power of \(2\); what power is it?

    Answer:

    \(x = \frac{1}{2}\). That is, \(\frac{1}{2}\) is the \(-1\) power of \(2\). After all, it would "kill off" a single factor of \(2\) if multiplied onto a product of a bunch of 2's.
  7. Solve the equation \(2^x = 1\). What power of 2 do we regard 1 to be—or, how many factors of 2 is 1 worth?

    Answer:

    \(x = 0\). We regard \(1\) as the \(0\)-power of \(2\) (as well as the \(0\)-power of any other number except \(0\) itself). After all, if you were to multiply a product of a bunch of 2's by 1, then this would neither increase nor take away from your count of repeated factors of \(2\). Put another way, \(1\) is the number that is worth 'nothing' in terms of multiplication.
  8. Solve the equation \(2^x = \sqrt{2}\). What power of 2 do we regard \(\sqrt{2}\) to be—or, how many factors of 2 is \(\sqrt{2}\) worth?

    Answer:

    \(x = \frac{1}{2}\). The point of a square root is to break apart a target number into two equal factors. So, it makes perfect sense to call each of them a "one half factor" of the target number. In particular, \(\sqrt{2}\) is a one half factor of \(2\).

    2. Answer:

  2. \(2^x = 100\)

So, what are the actual solutions to the previous three problems? Time for the big reveal. We learn the following symbol—which is the manner in which we ask the calculator how to find the answers; we learn the definition of logarithms.

For appropriate real numbers, we define the symbol \[ \log_b (A) \] to be the solution to the equation \[ b^x = A \] In words, \(\log_b{(A)}\) is "the number of factors of \(b\) that \(A\) is worth" or "the exponent that we raise \(b\) to to hit the target \(A\)." The number \(b\) in the symbol is called the base of the logarithm—because it is the base of the corresponding exponential. I'm not aware of a special name for the \(A\), but here is a reasonable name for it.

For example, while the most I can tell you off the top of my head about the solution to the equation \[ 2^x = 17 \] is that \(x\) is between 4 and 5, I also know the symbol for the answer is given in \[ x = \log_2{(17)} \]

You also often see the symbol displayed without parentheses as \(\log_b{A}\). However, that is inadvisable; the parentheses are used to communicate that \(\log_b\) is actually the symbol for a function—namely, the one that takes the output of the exponential function \(b^x\) as its input and returns the input that led to that output. That is, when you see "\(\log_b(x)\)" this is reall like seeing "\(f(x)\)"—except that there are 4 letters that make up the symbol for the function \(\log_b\) but only one letter that makes up the symbol for the (hypothetical) function \(f\).

Actually, in the case of two special bases, you don't have quite so many buttons to push.

In most high school and college texts, when you see \(\log\) without a subscript, it represents a base-10 logarithm: \[ \log = \log_{10} \] This is similar to how you drop the decoration on the radical when denoting square-root—as opposed to cube root or \(4^\textrm{th}\) root, which requires special identification. However, many texts and programming languages use the symbol log to represent the base-\(e\) logarithm.

So, when you are wanting the TI to compute the value of a base-10 logarithm, just reach for the "log" button:

Remember base \(e\) from the previous section? It is so important, its associated logarithm also gets a special symbol: ln. Put another way: \[ \ln = \log_e \] Also, you will need to know that this \(\ln\) function is called the natural logarithm. Why is it called that? It is because the base \(e\) ends up "naturally arising" in applications. I doubt that I have fully convinced you of that, but it's so. So, the 'l' in "\(\ln\)" stands for "logarithm" and the 'n' stands for "natural." Why is it backwards? I have always supposed that it is because the symbol was invented by or for speakers of French, which reverses noun and adjective, as in "log naturel". But you should probably check up on that yourself in case I am wrong.

Say that you have \$1000, invested at 4% interest, compounded monthly. How long until you have \$2000, assuming you make no withdrawals? Write your answer in terms of a logarithm before also using your calculator to obtain a numerical value rounded to two decimal places. Include the units of your answer.

Answer:

Using the formula \[ A(t) = P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \] we need to find the \(t\) that satisfies \[ 2000 = 1000 \left(1+\frac{0.04}{12}\right)^{12t} \] For starters, you can divide both sides by 1000: \[ 2 = \left(1+\frac{0.04}{12}\right)^{12t} \] Now, we don't know what exponent we can raise \(\left(1+\frac{0.04}{12}\right)\) to to hit the target \(2\), but we do know a symbol for it. That is, it must be that \[ 12t = \log_{\left(1+\frac{0.04}{12}\right)}\left(2\right) \] Note: that monster on the right is just the symbol for a number. Your calculator can help you out with that, but let's throw one more task on top of the calculator (it won't mind); you can also divide both sides by 12 to get a symbolic solution for \(t\): \[ t = \frac{\log_{\left(1+\frac{0.04}{12}\right)}\left(2\right)}{12} \] Here is what it looks like when I plug it all into my calculator:
As you can see, the numerical answer is \[ t \approx 17.36 \textrm{ years} \]

Say that you have \$4000, invested at 2.39% interest, compounded continuously. How long until you have \$5000, assuming you make no withdrawals? Write your answer in terms of a logarithm before also using your calculator to obtain a numerical value rounded to two decimal places. Include the units of your answer.

Answer:

Note the buzzword, "continuously compounded." This signals the fact that we must use the formula \[ A(t) = Pe^{rt} \] We solve as follows: \begin{align*} & 5000 = 4000 e^{0.0239t} &\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{5}{4} = e^{0.0239t} & \textrm{(Divide both sides by 4000)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad& 0.0239t = \log_e\left(\frac{5}{4}\right) = \ln\left(\frac{5}{4}\right) & \textrm{(Recall: }\ln=\log_e \textrm{.)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & t = \frac{\ln\left(\frac{5}{4}\right)}{0.0239} \approx 9.34 \textrm{ years} & \end{align*}

Francium-221 (\(^{221}\)Fr) is a radioisotope with a half life of 4.8 minutes. Find out how many minutes it will take 61 grams of \(^{221}\)Fr to decay leaving only 2 grams left. Express your answer in terms of a logarithm before also using your calculator to find its numerical value to two decimal places.

Answer:

We use the formula \[ A(t) = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\frac{t}{h}} \] Plug in the givens in the appropriate way and solve, like so: \begin{align*} & 2 = 61 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\frac{t}{4.8}} &\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{2}{61} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\frac{t}{4.8}} & \textrm{(Divide both sides by 6)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{t}{4.8} = \log_{\frac{1}{2}}^{\phantom{1}}\left(\frac{2}{61}\right) & \textrm{(Definition of logarithms)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & t = 4.8\log_{\frac{1}{2}}^{\phantom{1}}\left(\frac{2}{61}\right) & \textrm{(Multiply by 4.8.)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & t \approx 23.67 \textrm{ minutes}& \textrm{(Ask the calculator.)}\\ \end{align*}
