In the section on simple graphing , we practiced developing an equation for a line based on its \(y\)-intercept and slope. But you can develop an equation for a line based on other information, too. The next scenario we consider is where you know the slope and one point that the line passes through.

Note that if you know a point a line passes through, and you know its slope, then you can draw it! Put another way, there is only one line with that property (which is a more clear way of saying "a point and the slope determine the line"). The question is how to find an equation for it. Still, let's first practice just graphing a line based on information about a point it passes through and its slope.

Graph the line that passes through the point \( (2, -4) \) and has slope \( m = \frac{1}{3} \).

Answer:

Well, so I plot a point at \((2, -4)\) and then proceed out with a slope of \(\frac{1}{3}\). As discussed in the section in which we learned "point-slope form" , slope can be expressed as "rise over run". That is, you can view it as telling you how much \(y\) increases by (or decreases by, if slope is negative) when \(x\) increases a given amount. In particular, if you write slope as a fraction, you can observe that \(y\) will "rise" (or "fall", if negative) by the amount in the numerator of the fraction when \(x\) increases by the amount in the denominator. When I "run" 3 units to the right from \((2, -4)\) and then "rise" 1 unit, I end up at the point \((5, -3)\). Two points are all you need to graph a line, so I'm done:

Now, let's talk about how to obtain an equation for this graph. One method is try to figure out what the \(y\)-intercept is. While that method is okay, there is another method. Once you learn this other method, it will be easier than the method of finding the \(y\)-intercept.

Let me demonstrate the method, then I'll explain it, then you can practice it.

Find an equation for the line that passes through the point \((1, -3)\) and has slope \(m = \frac{1}{3}\).

Answer:

You can readily verify that \((x,y) = (1,-3)\) will satisfy the equation \[ y = \frac{1}{3}\left(x-1\right)-3 \] (Just plug in those values!! Don't they make the equation true? Do you see why I have placed the '-1' and the '-3' just so?) Also, you can easily imagine that if you distributed the \(\frac{1}{3}\) in and combined like terms you would soon have an equation of the form \(y = mx + b\)—and the slope would be \(m=\frac{1}{3}\). So, it is evident without doing any real work that the equation above will have a graph with the correct slope and will pass through the given point. Therefore it is an equation of the graph described.

Here's another example without all of the talk.

Find an equation for the line that passes through the point \((2, 5)\) and has slope \(m = -\frac{3}{4}\).

Answer:

\[ y = -\frac{3}{4}\left(x-2\right)+5 \]

I am using what is called point-slope form here.

An equation in the form \[ y = m(x-x_0) + y_0 \] is said to be in point-slope form. In this generic equation, \(x\) and \(y\) are the variables, whereas \(m\), \(x_0\), and \(y_0\) will be numbers specified in each problem.

As I demonstrated in my example above, you can just "read off" the fact that the substitution of \(x=x_0\) and \(y=y_0\) will make the equation true. If \(x = x_0\) and \(y=y_0\), then \[ \textrm{RHS} = m(x-x_0) + y_0 = m(x_0-x_0)+y_0 = m\cdot 0 + y_0 = 0 + y_0 = y_0 = y = \textrm{LHS} \] Therefore the graph of this equation passes through \(( x_0, y_0) \). You can also tell that it will have the correct slope, since it can be put into \( y=mx+b \) form and retain the given slope: \[ y = m(x-x_0) + y_0 = mx - mx_0 + y_0 \] (This means that your \(y\)-intercept would be \(-mx_0+y_0\).)

Now, to contrast, there is another bit of important vocabulary to know:

An equation in the form \[ y = mx + b \] is said to be in slope-intercept form.
