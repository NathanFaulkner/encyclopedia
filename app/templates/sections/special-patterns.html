When it comes to factoring trinomials, there are some "special patterns" that aren't really exceptions to the rule, but which are just worth looking at specifically. The "special patterns" also provide a bridge to harder techniques of factoring.

Factor the following. \[ x^2 - 9 \]

Answer:

This is really just a special case of examples treated elsewhere in the section on factoring trinomials, part 1. The only thing that might confuse you about this problem is that the middle term is missing. Really you could rewrite the given as \[ x^2 - 9 = x^2 + 0x - 9 \] So, using our technique from the section just linked to above, you want to find two numbers that multiply together to give you \(-9\) but add together to give you \(0\). Of course, the numbers are \(-3\) and \(3\): \[ x^2 - 9 = (x-3)(x+3) \]

So, the above example wasn't necessarily something new to you. But it still might be worth considering in a special way; that is, it can make your life easier to watch for the pattern exhibited in this example. The special pattern involved here can be expressed by the fact that for any real numbers \(x\) and \(p\) \[ (x-p)(x+p) = x^2 +px - px - p^2 = x^2 - p^2 \] So, if someone hands you something of the form "square minus a square" then you know how to factor it. Here's one more example.

Factor the following. \[ x^2 - 64 \]

Answer:

It's a "square minus a square"! \[ x^2 - 64 = (x-8)(x+8) \]

Now, actually, this "pattern" can be extended to cover more complicated expressions. After all, in the expression \((x-p)(x+p) = x^2 - p^2\), there's no reason why \(x\) or \(p\) couldn't be something more complicated. To see what I mean, it might help to look at the following "pattern" instead. For any real numbers \(A\) and \(B\) \[ (A-B)(A+B) = A^2 - B^2 \] So, if someone hands you something that follows the "pattern" of the right side (or you are able to recognize it as an instance of this pattern) then you can factor it, by writing it according to the pattern of the left hand side. In words, what you are looking for is "a square minus a square" (which is the same as we already practiced above.)

Factor the following. \[ 16x^2 - 9 \]

Answer:

I recognize this as an instance of the formula \(A^2 - B^2\) since \[ 16x^2 - 9 = (4x)^2 - 3^2 \] That is, I can see this as a "square minus a square." Therefore, it factors into the form \((A-B)(A+B)\) where \(A=4x\) and \(B=3\): \[ 16x^2 - 9 = (4x-3)(4x+3) \]

Here is an even weirder example:

Factor the following. \[ 9r^2 - 100p^2 \]

Answer:

You just have to recnognize that each of the terms are squares of less complicated expressions, which means this follows the "square minus a square" pattern: \begin{align*} 9r^2 - 100p^2 &= (3r)^2-(10p)^2\\ & = (3r-10p)(3r+10p) \end{align*}

Next up, we examine another special pattern before you do some mixed practice on the various special patterns. The special pattern or patterns to watch for this time is \[ (A+B)^2 = A^2 + 2AB + B^2 \] and \[ (A-B)^2 = A^2 - 2AB + B^2 \]

Factor the following. \[ x^2 + 14x + 49 \]

Answer:

This does indeed follow the pattern mentioned just above, as follows: \begin{align*} x^2 + 14x + 49 &= x^2 + 2\cdot 7x+ 7^2 \\ & = (x+7)^2 \end{align*}

A harder example will better illustrate the usefulness of being able to recognize this "special pattern."

Factor the following. \[ 9x^2 - 48x + 64 \]

Answer:

Because \(9x^2\) and \(64\) are both squares, I suspect that this might follow the \[ (A-B)^2 = A^2 - 2ABx + B^2 \] pattern. To verify that it does, I just note that \(48 = 2\cdot 3 \cdot 8\) and so \begin{align*} 9x^2 - 48x + 64 & = (3x)^2 - 2\cdot 3x \cdot 8 + 8^2 \\ & = (3x-8)^2 \end{align*}
