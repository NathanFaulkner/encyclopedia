In this section, we learn how to tackle more general cases—those that were the result of multiplying out something of the form \[ (mx+p)(nx+q) \] This is a bit more complicated, but can be tackled in a similar way as the easier cases you already learned—by first considering the general "forward" process and then imagining what it would take to "run it in reverse.""

When you multiply the above expression out, showing all work, you get \begin{align*}\tag{Forward Process}\label{forward process} (mx+p)(nx+q) & = (mx+p)nx + (mx+p)q \\ & = mnx^2 +pnx + mqx + pq \\ & = mnx^2 +(pn+mq)x +pq \end{align*} Seems complicated, right? And we have to imagine how to run this process in reverse! That is, we will need to take some thing of the form \[ ax^2 +bx +c \] and view it as being of the form \[ mnx^2 + (pn+mq)x +pq \] That is, we need to find \(m\), \(n\), \(p\), and \(q\) so that \[ ax^2 + bx + c = mnx^2 + (pn+mq)x +pq \] What the heck? Well, after much contemplation, you may get the following insight: To run that \ref{forward process} in reverse, you should start by taking the product \(ac\). Note that if \[ ax^2 + bx + c = mnx^2 + (pn+mq)x +pq \] then \(ac = mnpq\)—meaning, all the numbers we need "are in there." Next, we need two factors of \(ac=mnpq\) that add up to \(b\)—namely \(pn\) and \(mq\). Actually, at that point we won't know what \(m\), \(n\), \(p\), or \(q\) will be. But, if we can find two factors of \(ac\) that add up to \(b\) we can then run the \ref{forward process} in reverse by "factoring by grouping."

That's the explanation, but no doubt you'll need to see it done a couple times to make sense of it.

Factor the following. \[ 6x^2 + 11x - 10 \]

Answer:

  1. First find the product of the outer two numbers, "\(a\) times \(c\)." \[ ac = 6\cdot (-10) = -60 \]
  2. Next, break apart \(-60\) into two factors that add up to 11. You might accomplish that by just listing factorizations of \(60\) until you discover which two have a difference of \(11\): \begin{align*} 1 & \quad \quad 60 \\ 2 & \quad \quad 30 \\ 3 & \quad \quad 20 \\ 4 & \quad \quad 15 \end{align*} That's it! Since \(15 - 4= 11\), the factorization of \(-60\) we are looking for is \(15\cdot (-4)\).
  3. Next, you rewrite \(bx = 11x\) according to that break down: \[ 6x^2 + 11x - 10 = 6x^2 + 15x - 4x - 10 \]
  4. Then you factor by grouping. \begin{align*} 6x^2 + 11x - 10 &= 6x^2 + 15x - 4x - 10 \\ & = 3x(2x+5) - 2(2x+5) \\ & = (3x-2)(2x+5) \end{align*}

Factor the following. \[ 2x^2 - 9x + 9 \]

Answer:

Here are the steps:
  1. \(ac = 2\cdot 9 = 18\)
  2. \((-3)\cdot(-6)=18\) and \(-3+(-6) = -9\).
  3. Rewrite the quadratic: \[2x^2-9x+9 = 2x^2 - 6x - 3x +9\]
  4. Factor by grouping: \begin{align*} 2x^2 - 6x - 3x +9 &= 2x(x-3) - 3(x-3)\\ &=(2x-3)(x-3) \end{align*}
