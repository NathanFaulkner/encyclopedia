In this section, we take our study of power functions one step further by solving equations that involve them. Keep in mind, a power function is one whose outputs can be determined using a formula of the form \[ f(x) = Ax^p \] where \(A\) and \(p\) are real numbers. But, when \(p\) is a rational number, these functions are often conveyed instead using radical notation. We will look at both presentations. There are basically two types of problems you will have to contend with: one, where the power is an integer, and, two, where the power is a fraction (or is instead presented in radical form). We'll do the second type in a compananion section (Part 2!).

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^3 = 64 \]

Answer:

This one is pretty easy, I hope you will agree. It just poses the question, "What times itself 3 times is 64?" Now, the answer might not just jump out at you, but after a little guessing-and-checking, I hope you will realize the answer is 4.

Another way to explain what is going on here is that the question, "What times itself 3 times is 64?" is the same as that posed by this equation: \[ x = 64^\frac{1}{3} = 4 \] The power \(\frac{1}{3}\) means we are trying to break 64 apart into 3 equal factors. Of course, you might have also conveyed your answer using radical notation: \[ x = \sqrt[3]{64} = 4 \] The expressions \(x^\frac{1}{3}\) and \(\sqrt[3]{x}\) have the same meaning.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^3 = 60 \]

Answer:

This time, there is no nice answer; there is no convenient way to break 60 apart into 3 equal factors. Instead we just use a symbol for the answer. Of course, you could ask the calculator what the answer would be in decimal form. You might also imagine trying to guess-and-check your way to this decimal answer. (It would take awhile.) \begin{align*} &x^3 = 60 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \sqrt[3]{60} \end{align*}

Now, something a little different happens when an even number appears as the power. You should already be familiar with this little twist, but take a look at the following example as a reminder.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^2 = 64 \]

Answer:

What number times itself is 64? Well, 8. But \(-8\) works as well! So, we have two solutions: \begin{align*} &x^2 = 64 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 8 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -8 \end{align*}

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^2 = 60 \]

Answer:

There is no nice number that you can multiply by itself to arrive at 60. But, there is a yucky number—some decimal that the calculator will be glad to find for you. But, as you are not a calculator, but rather a human who works in symbols (which is also the way you interact with calculators and computers—through the interchange of symbols that mean very different things to you, on the one hand, and the computer on the other), this is what I want from you: \begin{align*} &x^2 = 60 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \sqrt{60} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = - \sqrt{60} \end{align*} Two answers!

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ x^4 = 81 \]

Answer:

Hmmm. What number times itself is 81? Well, again, this is just the sort of question we have already been entertaining with posers like \(81^\frac{1}{4}\). Noting that \[ 81 = 9 \cdot 9 = 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \] we realize the answer is 3\ldots except \(-3\) times itself 4 times is also 81! You might write this: \begin{align*} &x^4 = 81 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \sqrt[4]{81} = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\sqrt[4]{81} = - 3 \end{align*}

The key take-away from the previous example is to realize that a negative times itself an even number of times comes out positive; therefore, any power equation with an even power will have two answers, a negative and a positive. Now, we just "spice things up" in a few more ways.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ (x-4)^3 + 7 = 34 \]

Answer:

Don't forget your standard kit of tools to apply when solving an equation! You can do whatever you want as long as you do it to both sides. For instance, I could subtract 7 from both sides: \begin{align*} & (x-4)^3 + 7 = 34 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-4)^7 = 27 \end{align*} Next, I reason like so: While I don't know about \(x\) quite yet, I do know that \(x\) is supposedly a number. Therefore, \(x-4\) is also a number. And I know that that number—\(x-4\)—times itself comes out to 27. So, \(x-4\) must be 3: \begin{align*} & (x-4)^3 = 27 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x-4 = 3 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 7 \end{align*} Of course, to find what \(x\) could be, I just added 4 to both sides in my last step.

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ 2(x-4)^4 - 11 = 151 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 2(x-4)^4 - 11 = 151 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2(x-4)^4 = 162\\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-4)^4 = 81\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 4 = \sqrt[4]{81} = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 4 = -\sqrt[4]{81} = - 3\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 7 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 1\\ \end{align*}

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ 3x^3-6=24 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 3x^3-6=24 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x^3 = 30\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^3 = 10\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \sqrt[3]{10} \end{align*}

Find all reals that solve the equation. (Find the solution set—the set of all real numbers that would make the equation true when substituted in.) \[ 3(x-2)^2-6=24 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 3(x-2)^2-6=24\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3(x-2)^2 = 30\\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-2)^2 = 10\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 2 = \sqrt{10} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 2 = -\sqrt{10}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 2 + \sqrt{10} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 2 - \sqrt{10}\\ \end{align*}
