In this section, we encounter a new concept—actually a new number. This number (an actual number—e.g., one that you could plot on the real line!) shows up naturally in exponential applications. In some ways, this number will seem strange, but you'll want to lump into other interesting numbers that have already come up in your study of math, like \(\pi\approx 3.14159\) and \(\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414213\). This new number, which is symbolized by \(e\), is approximately as follows: \[ e \approx 2.718281828\ldots \] Like \(\pi\), \(e\) cannot be expressed as a ratio of whole numbers and therefore also cannot be represented as a "repeating decimal" expansion. Also, as in the case of \(\pi\), staring at its decimal expansion tells you a bsolutely nothing about it—except that it does impress upon you, I hope, that we are talking about a real number here, and one which is in the neighborhood of \(2.7\).

Now, for the more interesting part of the story. I mentioned above that \(e\) (which is designated by this letter at least in part to honor the 18\(^\textrm{th}\)-Century mathematician, Leonhard Euler who played a large role in our understanding of this number) comes up naturally in the study of natural phenomena related to exponential growth (or decay). But we start with an application which certainly isn't natural but altogether "arbitrary," which nevertheless presents a traditional and easy road to the definition of this number. We consider its application to finance.

In the previous section, we discussed the concept of compound interest—where the bank adds interest to your account periodically and then even adds interest on the interest. We also studied the question of how often the bank chooses to compound your interest: does it compound it once a year? every month? every day? We observed that one does gain a little bit by the end when the bank is willing to compound monthly as opposed to once a year—but the gain is modest. For instance, if you invest \$1,000 at \(4%\), as we saw, then, using the equation \[ A(t) = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \] we find that, if the bank awards interest only at the end of the year, then you would have \[ 1000\left(1+0.04\right) = \$1040 \] but if the bank compounds your interest each month, then you would have \[ 1000\left(1+\frac{0.04}{12}\right)^{12} \approx \$1040.74 \] So, that isn't a very impressive gain. But what if the bank was willing to compound interest more often? How much could you earn? Let's try "every day": \[ 1000\left(1+\frac{0.04}{360}\right)^{360} \approx \$1040.81 \] Rather disappointing!

We have already seen that you do indeed earn more money when the bank compounds your interest more often, but that you don't earn much more. Well, what if, say, the bank compounds interest at some incredible frequency—millions or billions of times per year, etc. See how much you can earn that way by using the equation \[ A(t) = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \] and using some really large number for \(n\). How large of a number will your calculator even allow you to use in place of \(n\)?

In the previous activity, I hope you were impressed by how little difference using larger and larger numbers for \(n\) made. It's almost as if the amounts that you can make were "zeroing in" on some mysterious, secret value. Is this right, or is this an illusion? Maybe your TI-84 is just too meager to get to the bottom of this. What if we used a supercomputer, like Summit in Oak Ridge, Tennessee? Maybe the value of our one-year investment would really be quite large if we could use a very much bigger value for \(n\). In fact, this turns out not to be the case. One can prove using some slick algebra that there is a ceiling on how large the expression \[ 1000\left(1+\frac{0.04}{n}\right)^n \] can be, regardless of how large \(n\) is. And if it has a ceiling, t hen it has a lowest such ceiling. (Actually, this can be taken to be the mathematical definition of the real numbers: It's the set of numbers that includes all the lowest ceilings on any (bounded) set of rational numbers.) In case you are following along closely, you may anticipate what I have to say next: this ceiling has something to do with the number \(e\). To see exactly how, we are going to strip this question down to its most basic illustration—in the following activity.

You might even be curious what happens when you plug in still larger numbers for \(n\). But let me caution you, if you use one of the Texas Instruments (TI) series of calculators, because of limitations (or even choices) in the programming of the TI, it will spit out erroneous values when you go above \(10^{12}\)—say, to \(10^{13}\) (but some of your calculators may in fact perform better than mine). I hope you are impressed, in passing, by the fact that calculators can in fact err, but that's not the key point I want to make here. Rather, if you are successful in your calculation of \(\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n\) for \(n=10^{12}\), then I hope you found that \[ \left(1+\frac{1}{10^{12}}\right)^{10^{12}} \approx 2.718281828 \] And, of course, I hope you will recognize that as the number introduced in the first paragraph above: it is \(e\).

In words, \(e\) is the (lowest) ceiling on the amount of money you can make in 1 year when you invest \$1 at 100% interest—even if the bank is willing to compound your interest not every month, not every day, but even trillions of times per year.

In math symbols, we can say that \[ \textrm{As } n \rightarrow \infty, \left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n \rightarrow e \approx 2.71828182 \] An even more convenient use of \(e\) is given in the following statement: \[ \textrm{For large \(n\), } \left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n \approx e = 2.71828\ldots \]

Making use of that last statement, we can actually rewrite the "ceiling on the amount you can make on \$1000 in one year at 4% interest if the bank is willing to compound your interest any number of times," which we had contemplated above. In other words, we can really use \(e\) to rewrite the ceiling on the amount you can earn in any set time on a given principle and interest rate, where the bank let's you play with how often they will compound your interest. Here is how to do that.

  1. Say that you are investing \$\(P\) at APR \(r\cdot 100%\) for \(t\) years. Say, too, that the number \(n\) of times the bank compounds your interest is very large. Note that \[ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}} = \frac{\frac{1}{1}}{\frac{n}{r}} = \frac{1}{1} \cdot \frac{r}{n} = \frac{r}{n} \] Then, \[ A(t) = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} = P\left(1+ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)^{nt} \]
  2. Next, rewrite the exponent: \[ nt = \frac{n}{r} rt \] That is, make the alteration: \[ A(t) = P\left(1+ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)^{\frac{n}{r}rt} \]
  3. Next, use the exponent rule, \({\color{red}a}^{\color{blue}b\color{orange}c} = \left({\color{red}a}^{\color{blue}b}\right)^{\color{orange}c}\). In particular, I am looking at \(\frac{n}{r}\) as my target "\({\color{blue}b}\)" and \(rt\) as my target "\({\color{orange}c}\)" with \(\left(1+\frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)\) as my "\({\color{red}a}\)"; rewrite as follows: \[ A(t) = P{\color{red}\left(1+ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)}^{ \color{blue}\frac{n}{r} {\color{orange}rt} } = P\left( {\color{red}\left(1+ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)}^{\color{blue}\frac{n}{r}} \right)^{\color{orange}rt} \]
  4. Now, I note that if \(n\) is very large, then \(\frac{n}{r}\) will be, too. Therefore, I can write \(\color{blue}\left(1+ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)^\frac{n}{r} \color{black}\approx \color{blue}e\). Substituting this in, I get \[ A(t) = P\left({\color{blue}\left(1+ \frac{1}{\frac{n}{r}}\right)^{\frac{n}{r}}}\right)^{rt} \approx P \bigg({\color{blue}e}\bigg)^{rt} = Pe^{rt} \] which is a much simpler equation, I hope you will agree.
Moral: When you go to the bank and tell them to "compound as many times as you are willing," they happily oblige by computing the amount \(A\) in your account after \(t\) years using \[ A(t) = Pe^{rt} \] Actually, because bankers like nothing more than impressing you with their technical know-how, they have invented a very high-falutin way of talking about all of this. An investment growing via this formula is said to be compounded not just every second (which may amount to the same thing) but is instead said to be compounded continuously.

When interest is compounded not monthly or daily but is instead compounded continuously, then the amount \(A\) in your account after \(t\) years can be found by implementing the formula \[ A(t) = Pe^{rt} \] in which \(r\) is the annual percentage rate (APR) of interest expressed as a decimal.

Say that you invest \$1000 at 4% interest with interest compounded continuously.
  1. Write down a formula for the amount \(A\) in your account after \(t\) years.

    Answer:

    \(A(t) = Pe^{rt} = 1000 e^{0.04t}\)
  2. Compute the value of your account after 5 years (assuming you don't make any withdrawals.)

    Answer:

    \(A(5) = 1000 e^{0.04 \cdot 5} \approx \$1221.40\)

Say that you invest \$54,000 at 2.5% interest with interest compounded continuously.
  1. Write down a formula for the amount \(A\) in your account after \(t\) years.

    Answer:

    Note that 2.5% expressed as a decimal is \(r = 0.0205\). \(A(t) = Pe^{rt} = 54000 e^{0.0205t}\)
  2. Compute the value of your account after 5 years (assuming you don't make any withdrawals.)

    Answer:

    \(A(5) = 54000 e^{0.0205\cdot 5} \approx \$59,828.61\)
