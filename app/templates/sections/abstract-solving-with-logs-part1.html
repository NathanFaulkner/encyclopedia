In this section, we continue to solve equations that involve exponentials, but we "spice them up" in a few other ways. Ultimately, a logarithm is needed to provide a symbolic solution to a "what exponent" question. In the next section, we solve equations that initially involve logs themselves.

These first couple are hardly worse than anything we did in the section What is a Logarithm? —Actually they are a little easier!

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 3 \cdot 7^{x-1} + 5 = 17 \]

Answer:

This isn't so bad! We just apply our normal strategies for deducing simpler equations. Lastly, we apply the definition of a logarithm: \begin{align*} & 3 \cdot 7^{x-1} + 5 = 17\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3 \cdot 7^{x-1} = 12 & \textrm{(Subtract 5)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 7^{x-1} = 4 & \textrm{(Divide by 3)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 1 = \log_7{(4)} & \textrm{(Definition of log)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \log_7{(4)} + 1 & \textrm{(Add 1)}\\ \end{align*}

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 12 - 7 \cdot 2^{3x+5} = -17 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 12 - 7 \cdot 2^{3x+5} = -17\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 7 \cdot 2^{3x+5} = -29 & \textrm{(Subtract 12)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2^{3x+5} = \frac{29}{7} & \textrm{(Divide both sides by \(-7\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x + 5 = \log_2{\left(\frac{29}{7}\right)} & \textrm{(Definition of log)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x = \log_2{\left(\frac{29}{7}\right)} - 5 & \textrm{(Subtract 5)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{\log_2{\left(\frac{29}{7}\right)} - 5}{3} & \textrm{(Divide by 3)}\\ \end{align*}

Now, we turn to something just a little harder—or, easier, depending on your perspective. We look at equations in which the unknown appears in more than one exponent.

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of an exact fractional answer. \[ 8^{3x-1} = 8^{2 - 6x} \]

Answer:

What \(x\) will make this true? Well, what if you can find an \(x\) that will make \(3x-1 = 2 - 6x\)? Clearly, that \(x\) would satisfy the equation. But, what's more, if you think about the graph of \(y=8^x\), you can realize that there is only one exponent (\(x\)-value) that gives rise to a particular value of \(8^x\) (\(y\)-value)—because the graph increases from left to right, and therefore no \(y\)-value is arrived at twice. What that means is that the \(x\) that makes \(3x-1 = 2 - 6x\) is the only \(x\) that could possibly work, the one and only solution. Solving is easy, I hope you will agree: \begin{align*} & 3x-1 = 2 - 6x\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x-1 = 2 & \textrm{(Add \(6x\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x= 3 & \textrm{(Add \(1\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x= \frac{3}{9} = \frac{1}{3} & \textrm{(Divide by \(9\))}\\ \end{align*}

Now, I bet you found that to be easy. But I want to show you one more way you could have gone about that—an actually much clumsier way—because it will provide an insight into how you might solve a slightly tweaked but harder equation. Previous Example, Method 2: You use "properties of exponents." Namely, we'll be using the rule \[ \frac{a^r}{a^s} = a^{r-s} \] Starting with \[ 8^{3x-1} = 8^{2 - 6x} \] you can divide both sides by, say \(8^{2-6x}\), apply the rule I just reminded you of, and then apply the definition of logarithms: \begin{align*} &8^{3x-1} = 8^{2 - 6x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad &\frac{8^{3x-1}}{8^{2-6x}} = 1 & \textrm{(Divide both sides by \(8^{2-6x}\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 8^{(3x-1)-(2-6x)}& \textrm{(Apply the rule \(\frac{a^r}{a^s} = a^{r-s}\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 8^{9x - 3} = 1 & \textrm{(Simplify)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 3 = \log_8{(1)} & \textrm{(Definition of log)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 3 = 0 & \textrm{(Simplify: \(\log_8(1) = 0\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x = 3 & \textrm{(Add 3)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{1}{3} & \textrm{(Divide by 9)}\\ \end{align*} So, you get the same answer. But, yes, this way was harder. Why did I just show you that? Again, it's because this is the method we'll need to adopt when we alter that equation just a tad, as in the following:

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 8^{3x-1} = 7\cdot 8^{2 - 6x} \]

Answer:

Now, I have messed things up with that \(7\). There are different ways to go about this, but one of the easiest would actually be to mimic what I just did in the "Method 2" demonstration just above. \begin{align*} & 8^{3x-1} =7\cdot 8^{2 - 6x} \\ \Rightarrow \quad &\frac{8^{3x-1}}{8^{2-6x}} = 7 & \textrm{(Divide both sides by \(8^{2-6x}\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 8^{(3x-1)-(2-6x)} = 7& \textrm{(Apply the rule \(\frac{a^r}{a^s} = a^{r-s}\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 8^{9x - 3} = 7 & \textrm{(Simplify)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x - 3 = \log_8{(7)} & \textrm{(Definition of log)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9x = \log_8{(7)} + 3 & \textrm{(Add 3)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{\log_8{(7)}+3}{9} & \textrm{(Divide by 9)}\\ \end{align*}

Better do another:

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 5^{4x+5} = 12\cdot 5^{2x + 7} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 5^{4x+5} = 12\cdot 5^{2x + 7} \\ \Rightarrow \quad &\frac{5^{4x+5} }{5^{2x + 7}} = 12 & \textrm{(Divide both sides by \(5^{2x + 7}\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5^{(4x+5)-(2x+7)} = 12& \textrm{(Apply the rule \(\frac{a^r}{a^s} = a^{r-s}\))}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5^{2x - 2} = 12 & \textrm{(Simplify)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x - 2 = \log_5{(12)} & \textrm{(Definition of log)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x = \log_5{(12)} + 2 & \textrm{(Add 2)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{\log_5{(12)} + 2}{2} & \textrm{(Divide by 2)}\\ \end{align*}

Perhaps you have gotten the hang of that. Now, there is one final complication that we will consider: when the unknown appears in two different exponents, but the bases aren't the same. Actually, we will only consider special cases of this scenario; we will hold off until a full treatment of this scenario until the next section, in which we learn some handy facts about logarithms.

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 5^{4x+5} = 25^{2x + 7} \]

Answer:

What can we do now? Well, here is a strategy: Try to rewrite one or the other bases in terms of the other (or, conceivably, rewrite both in terms of a third, new base). In this case, I note that 25 is a power of 5, and make the substitution, \(25 = 5^2\). Then, apply the following rule of exponents \[ \left(a^r\right)^s = a^{rs} \] \begin{align*} & 5^{6x+5} = 25^{x + 7} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5^{6x+5} = \left(5^2\right)^{2x + 7} & \textrm{Substitute \(5^2\) for 25}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5^{6x+5} = 5^{2(2x + 7)}& \textrm{Apply the rule \(\left(a^r\right)^s = a^{rs}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 5^{6x+5} = 5^{4x+14} & \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 6x+5= 4x+14 & \textrm{Equate exponents}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x + 5= 14 & \textrm{Subtract 4x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x = 9 & \textrm{Subtract 5}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{9}{2} & \textrm{Divide by 2}\\ \end{align*}

I'll show you a couple more of those, and then it's time for you to practice.

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 3^{4 - 5x} = \left(\frac{1}{27}\right)^{3 - 7x} \]

Answer:

Note that \(\frac{1}{27} = 3^{-3}\). Making that substitution is the key: \begin{align*} & 3^{4 - 5x} = \left(\frac{1}{27}\right)^{3 - 7x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{4 - 5x} = \left(3^{-3}\right)^{3 - 7x} & \textrm{Substitute \(3^{-3}\) for \(\frac{1}{27}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{4 - 5x} = 3^{-3(3 - 7x)}& \textrm{Apply the rule \(\left(a^r\right)^s = a^{rs}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{4 - 5x}= 3^{21x - 9} & \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 4 - 5x = 21x - 9 & \textrm{Equate exponents}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 13 = 26x & \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{13}{26} = \frac{1}{2} & \textrm{Divide by 26}\\ \end{align*}

Solve the following. That is, give a concise description of the set of real numbers that satisfy the equation. Write your answer in terms of a logarithm. \[ 3^{4 - 5x} = 4\cdot \left(\frac{1}{27}\right)^{3 - 7x} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & 3^{4 - 5x} = 4 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{27}\right)^{3 - 7x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{4 - 5x} = 4 \cdot \left(3^{-3}\right)^{3 - 7x} & \textrm{Substitute \(3^{-3}\) for \(\frac{1}{27}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{4 - 5x} = 4 \cdot 3^{-3(3 - 7x)}& \textrm{Apply the rule \(\left(a^r\right)^s = a^{rs}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{4 - 5x}= 4 \cdot 3^{21x - 9} & \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{3^{4 - 5x}}{3^{21x - 9}} = 4& \textrm{Divide both sides by \(3^{21x-9}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{(4 - 5x)-(21x - 9)} = 4& \textrm{Apply the rule \(\frac{a^r}{a^s} = a^{r-s}\)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3^{13 - 26x} = 4 & \textrm{Simplify}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 13 - 26x = \log_3{(4)} & \textrm{Definition of logs}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & - 26x = \log_3{(4)} - 13 & \textrm{Subtract 13}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{\log_3{(4)} - 13}{-26} & \textrm{Divide by \(-26\)}\\ \end{align*}
