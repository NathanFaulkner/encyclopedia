We now turn to another pretty basic concept of algebra: absolute value. You probably already know what absolute value is. But let's practice the very basics:

Click for video.

Compute the value of each of the following:
  1. \(|-6|\)

    Answer:

    As you may know, \[ |-6| = 6 \] Simple as that!
  2. \(|6|\)

    Answer:

    \[ |6| = 6 \]
  3. Now, describe how absolute value works. As I have said elsewhere, the real line is our go-to mental model for explaining the meaning of many algebraic concepts. Explain what absolute value means on the real line.

    Answer:

    All that the absolute value of the number is is its distance from 0. You could also say that absolute value gives the magnitude or "bigness" of the number. This comment must be understood carefully, though---in a sense to a point when I made in the discussion of inequalities. A fact that is well known to you is that, by golly, numbers get bigger as you go deeper into the negatives. That is, in a sense, even though it's true that \(-100\) is "less than" \(-50\)—if we understand that phrase in the sense of a less than inequality, like so: \[ -100 \lt -50 \] But, in still another sense, \(-100\) is bigger than -50—in the sense that 100 is a bigger number than 50. This latter sense of bigness—looking at the bigness of the number while ignoring the negative sign attached—is what absolute value is about.

    Let me put it in more concrete terms by first reminding you why negative numbers even exist. Think of the real line as the ability to travel infinitely far away in "one dimension". That means that you can't go "East/West" and "North/South" but only one or the other—but you do still have the two directions to choose from. On the (horizontally-oriented) real line, you can go as far as you like to the East—which would be the positive direction—but what's the good in that if you couldn't also go to the West! Sometimes you need to change directions or backtrack, no? So, negative numbers are really just a way of saying "go to the west". Of course, if you want to go "negative West", then what you mean is East; if going "reverse East" or backtracking after going East is really "West", then, clearly, going "reverse West" is East. Hence, "the negative of a negative is positive." Here, I invite you to imagine a world in which the negative sign doesn't exist and where we write something like "W5" for \(-5\) and "E5" for \(5\). You might not preferto live in a world like that, but I assure you that it would be a perfectly valid world with the same mathematics as us—only written differently. (Same in what sense, then?)

    Now, going back to absolute value, you can say that absolute value doesn't care whether you went East or West—it only cares about how far you went. There is some more nice vocab that you can inject into this conversation. Have you ever learned the concept of a vector as information about "distance and direction"? Well, that's what the word means! So, in fact, while people have the habit of referring to negative numbers as numbers, this is just a choice that people made in how to use language. In a way, they are not numbers at all but because they tell you both about distance from 0 (the number part of absolute value) and direction (whether it is negative or positive). Actually, it could prevent a lot of pain and confusion on the part of young students if no one ever insisted that negative numbers are numbers at all—and instead learned that helpful word vector that helps clarify the new distinction being created at a younger age.

The above problems were really much to easy. Maybe you'd like to be tested on something like that, but that's not the wort of thing I'll be asking in the exercises. For a lot of people, absolute value gets harder pretty quick. But why should it? Why should we start with such super-easy beginnings and then suddenly find ourselves in something hard? What if we took it slow, step-by-step—could we figure out the point at which it becomes difficult? (Or maybe, if we take it slow enough, making each step carefully, putting one foot in front of the other—but only after making sure the previous one was planted firmly—it will never become hard at all!)

So, the next step after contemplating very easy things like the absolute value of a number is to contemplate the meaning of the absolute value of a number in disguise—a variable.

What can we say about the absolute value of an unknown number? Take any unknown real number \(x\). That is, you take one number, but I might take another; a third person may choose yet another. What can you say about the value of \(|x|\)?

Answer:

Has it gotten hard yet? Nah—not too hard, anyway! Here's what you can say about \(x\): if \(x\) was a negative, then \(|x|\) will be what you get when you throw the negative sign away. And if \(x\) is positive, then \(|x|\) will just be itself. One could say more about this—but not much!

Not hard yet, so let's take one more baby step:

What we are warming up to is to solve equations involving absolute value. Here's one that isn't too hard to start with.
  1. Solve. (Identify the solution set.): \[ |x| = 3 \]

    Answer:

    Let's talk it out. What the meager prompt "solve" means (in this context) is this:
    Suppose that \(x\) is a real number—so, it's sitting somewhere on the real line, and that all we know about it is this: \[ |x| = 3 \] It's absolute value is 3! So, what is the number? Can you guess it? Well,maybe—but you'd have a 50/50 chance! After all, there are two numbers on the real line that have an absolute value of 3—both \(3\) and \(-3\). That's it! Simple as that. Absolute value is just "how far is the number from 0?" So, literally, when you say \(|x| = 3\), you are saying that \(x\) is a number that is at a distance of 3 from 0. 'Nuff said!
  2. Solve. (Identify the solution set.): \[ |x| = -3 \]

    Answer:

    Hmm... so, \(x\) is a real number—something we could plot on the real line—and we know that its aboslute value is \(-3\). Looks like math, right? Well, be sure and think about the meaning of the math. I'm taking for granted that above, in Example 1, for example, you agreed that absolute value is just about the easiest thing in the world. You wish all math could be that easy. So, what about this problem? Just think about the meaning! You also agreed above that absolute value is just "the distance from 0" of the number: no funny business, just the honest-to-goodness distance. So, let me ask you, can distance be negative? Don't answer me like a poor young person who has been cowed by the mathematical powers-that-be into thinking that you don't know nothing and the teacher has to tell it to you. You know very well what distance is---"how far?" Negatives don't enter in there. (Where do they enter in? Answer: only when you care about direction, rather than just "how far?") So, distance can't be negative. Absolute value is just distance from 0. So, this problem is one of those trick problems—or, rather, its a statement which can never be true no matter what you plug in for \(x\). That is, it the equation has no solution.
  3. Previously, you have probably adopted the following mental "scheme" when solving equations: "In order to solve equations, you do something to both sides..."" Is that what is happening here? What is a new mental scheme that will help here? By the way, if you have no idea what I am talking about with the phrase "mental scheme," take a look at the following Wikipedia article.

    Answer:

    No, we're not "doing something to both sides". So, that point of view or mindset is not universally applicable to when it comes to solving equations. So, what is a universally applicable scheme or strategy to solving any and all equations? Well, you may be both pleased and disappointed by my truly honest and definitive answer: what you do is you think about the meaning of the symbols. That's all we did to solve the equation! And, I promise, this is what you'll have to continue to do to have continued success with solving absolute value equations—and in math in general. You have to know and think about the meanings of the symbols (and what they mean when strung together in particular ways.)

    Thnking is hard! That's why a lot of people tend to avoid it much of the time! As you may know, too, math is hard. Why?—because it requires thinking. That's the bad news. The good news is that that is all it requires. So, yes, you have to think about the meaning of the absolute value equations below (remember—we are making them a little harder as we proceed)—but that is all you have to do to succeed. Nothing more and nothing less. Do you have to memorize a bunch of rules? No, you don't have to do that. (Could you? Maybe, but I don't recommend it; I have seen how students fair who pursue that strategy.) But maybe, first, you have to commit yourself to the strategy of thinking as a strategy and to give up on other strategies like memorization. The sooner you do, the better and more happier you will be—even though (or maybe even because) it will take hard work—but hopefully less anxiety.

    As a bit of encouragement, were these problems hard to think about? Was it hard to think about the question of \[ |x| = 3 \] or \[ |x| = -3 \] I hope you will agree that it wasn't very hard to think about those. And, I promise, it really isn't that much harder to think about the "harder" problems below. But it is hard or at least painful to sit there and try to cram in a bunch of rules into your memory bank (because you have chosen to just follow rules and stop thinking).

Solve. (Identify the solution set.) \[ |x - 5| = 3 \]

Answer:

Note, of course, that this is not so different from an example above. This is awfully similar to \[ |x| = 3 \] For that one, we started—in the way that we always should—by reminding ourselves that "Solve such-and-such-an-equation-with-\(x\)" means first of all that we are entertaining this narrative:
Suppose that \(x\) is a real number with the given property.
Given that the absolute value of \(x\) is 3, it was pretty simple to deduce what \(x\) might be—one of two possibilities. This time, the only difference is that we aren't told about \(x\) directly, in a way. We are told about \(x - 5\) instead. But, look, \(x\) is a real number—meaning, very concretely, that \(x\) exists somewhere along the real line. Therefore, \(x-5\) is also a number—or, you could say, \(x-5\) is also the symbol for a number. Indeed, \(x-5\) is the number that lies precisely 5 units to the left of wherever \(x\) is on the real line.

So, we are told this time about the number \(x-5\): we are told that its absolute value is 3. And that is not a mysterious statement. That means that this number represented by "\(x-5\)" is either \(3\) or \(-3\). Writing this in math symbols provides a further insight (in case the solution is not already clear): \[ x - 5 = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5 = -3 \] From there, it is easy to deduce the answer—or, if you prefer—the two solutions in the solution set: \[ x = 8 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 2 \]

Solve. (Identify the solution set.) \[ |x - 5| = -3 \]

Answer:

Note, of course, that this is not so different from an example above. This is awfully similar to \[ |x| = -3 \] In that prior problem, we noted that absolute value cannot come out negative, so we get no solution. That's what happens here as well. The narrative goes like this:
  1. Assume the \(x\) is a real number with the property that \[ |x - 5| = -3 \]
  2. Then \(x - 5\) is also a real number.
  3. We are told that the absolute value of \(x-5\) is -3. But absolute value cannot come out negative, so this is impossible.
  4. We are forced to reject our starting premise (that \(x\) is a real number with the given property). That is, there is no solution.

That's pretty much it!! You might be getting the hang of it if you understand the next problem. But you should probably check out the next three examples just to be safe.

Solve. (Identify the solution set.) \[ |2x - 5| = 3 \]

Answer:

The logic goes like this:
  1. Assume that \(x\) is a real number (so could be plotted on the real line).
  2. Therefore, \(2x - 5\) is also a number (you could literally take the number that is twice as far from 0 as \(x\)—but in the same direction, whether negative or positive—and then scoot that number to the left 5).
  3. We are given that the absolute value of \(2x - 5\) is \(3\). Therefore, \[ 2x - 5 = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 2x - 5 = -3 \]
  4. From there, we deduce that \[ 2x = 8 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 2x = 2 \] and then that \[ x = 4 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 1 \]
  5. As a final step, it is quite prudent to check your answers. Observe that if we take \(x\) to equal 4 we find that \[ \textrm{LHS} = |2x - 5| = |2\cdot 4 - 5| = |8 - 5| = |3| = 3 = \textrm{RHS} \] (\(\textrm{LHS}\) means "left-hand side of our original equation" and \(\textrm{RHS}\) means "right-hand side of our original equation"). Likewise, if we substitute in \(1\) for \(x\), we get \[ \textrm{LHS} = |2x - 5| = |2\cdot 1 - 5| = |2 - 5| = |-3| = 3 = \textrm{RHS} \] So, both answers work. Further, by the logic of the previous steps, we know that they are the only possible solutions.

So, we are told this time about the number \(x-5\): we are told that its absolute value is 3. And that is not a mysterious statement. That means that this number represented by "\(x-5\)" is either \(3\) or \(-3\). Writing this in math symbols provides a further insight (in case the solution is not already clear): \[ x - 5 = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5 = -3 \] From there, it is easy to deduce the answer—or, if you prefer—the two solutions in the solution set: \[ x = 8 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 2 \]

Solve. (Identify the solution set.) \[ |2x - 5| + 7 = 10 \]

Answer:

You can probably do with less narrative on this one. As a matter of fact, it is hardly different from the previous problem—except that you ought to subtract \(7\) from both sides to take it to the situation we have been studying in this section, namely, where you have "the absolute value of some unknown is a given number". So, solving might look like this: \begin{align*} & |2x - 5| + 7 = 10 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & |2x - 5| = 3 & \textrm{Subtracted 7 from both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x - 5 = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 2x - 5 = -3 & \textrm{Thought about the meaning}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x = 8 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 2x = 2 & \textrm{Added 5 to both sides of each}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 4 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 1 & \textrm{Divided both sides of each by 2}\\ \end{align*}

Solve. (Identify the solution set.) \[ |2x - 5| + 10 = 7 \]

Answer:

Don't forget. The key thing to do—the only true strategy to pursue—is to think about the meaning of what you are looking at. Here is my process and thoughts: \begin{align*} & |2x - 5| + 10 = 7 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & |2x - 5| = -3 & \textrm{Subtracted 10 from both sides}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \textrm{No solution} & \textrm{Thought about the meaning}\\ \end{align*} What happened there? I deduce that, if \(x\) is a real number with the given property, then \(2x-5\) is a number whose absolute value is \(-3\). Absolute value is about distance, which cannot be 0, so this is nonsense. There is no such \(x\).
