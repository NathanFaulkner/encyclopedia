We start (in a manner very much in the current traditions of mathematics!) with a bunch of definitions. You can regard these definitions as just providing a vocabulary, a convenient shorthand used in subsequent discussions. However, in this section, I also aim to build your skills in carrying out some processes that will be a sort of intermediate step toward harder tasks to come.

A function is called quadratic if its outputs can be obtained by executing a formula of the form \[ f(x) = ax^2 + bx +c \] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are numbers that will be specified, with the added rule that \(a\neq 0\) (because if \(a=0\) then the function would actually be linear!)

A quadratic written in the form \begin{equation*}\tag{Standard Form} ax^2+bx+c \end{equation*} is said to be in standard form.

But, at first, we will mostly be working with quadratics written in other forms. ("Standard form" is not always the most convenient!)

A quadratic written in the form \begin{equation*}\tag{Vertex Form} a(x-h)^2+k \end{equation*} (where \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will be numbers) is said to be in vertex form.

A quadratic written in the form \begin{equation*}\tag{Intercept Form} a(x-x_0)(x-x_1) \end{equation*} (where \(a\), \(x_0\), and \(x_1\) will be numbers) is said to be in intercept form.

Let's practice a little bit of algebra that may come in handy: switching from intercept form or vertex form into standard form.

Rewrite the following quadratic so that it is in standard form (by multiplying it all out.) \[ f(x) = (x-5)(x+3) \]

Answer:

You have to multiply this all out. Let me note that, in general, \begin{align*} (a+b)(c+d) & = (a+b)c + (a+b)d & \textrm{(Distributive Property)}\\ & = ac + bc + ad + bd & \textrm{(Distributive Property again!)} \end{align*} Long story short "all the terms in the first pair get multiplied by each of the terms in the second pair." (You can also say "FOIL" if you like, but that is perhaps a limited point-of-view in the long-run!) So, we get that \[ (x-5)(x+3) = x^2 + 3x - 5x - 15 = x^2 - 2x - 15 \]

Rewrite the following quadratic so that it is in standard form (by multiplying it all out.) \[ f(x) = -2(x-5)(x+3) \]

Answer:

Recall the associative property of multiplication: \[ a(b\cdot c) = (a\cdot b)\cdot c \] What does that mean? It just means that you can multiply a product of three things in either of those orders. (Together with the commutativity of multiplication—i.e., the fact that \(a\cdot b = b \cdot a\)—it means you can multiply in any order you like.) So, for this problem, I choose to multiply \((x-5)(x+3)\) and then just multiply everything by \(-2\) at the end: \begin{align*} -2(x-5)(x+3) &= -2\left(x^2 + 3x - 5x - 15\right)\\ & = -2\left(x^2 - 2x - 15\right) \\ & = -2x^2 +4x + 30 \end{align*}

Rewrite the following quadratic so that it is in standard form (by multiplying it all out.) \[ f(x) = (x-2)^2+5 \]

Answer:

Multiply out the \((x-2)^2\) and then add 5 at the end: \begin{align*} (x-2)^2+5 &= (x-2)(x-2) + 5\\ & = x^2 - 2x -2x + 4 + 5 \\ & = x^2 -4x +9 \end{align*}

Rewrite the following quadratic so that it is in standard form (by multiplying it all out.) \[ f(x) = -2(x-2)^2+5 \]

Answer:

Carry it all out in the order of operations: Multiply out the \((x-2)^2\), then multiply the result by \(-2\), and then add 5 at the end: \begin{align*} -2(x-2)^2+5 &= -2(x-2)(x-2) + 5\\ & = -2\left(x^2 - 2x -2x + 4\right) + 5 \\ & = -2\left(x^2 -4x +4\right) +5 \\ & = -2x^2 +8x - 8 +5 \\ &= -2x^2 +8x -3 \end{align*}
