This section has a lot in common with that on vertex form of parabolas, in which we graphed transformations of \( y=x^2. \) Just as we did then, when we are starting from scratch, we begin by "plotting points"—by which I mean we plug in \(x\)-values and generate the corresponding \(y\)-value; then you plot 'em! Then, after that, we developed the theory of transformations to argue that any function of the form \[ f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k \] will have a graph similar to that of \(y = x^2\) albeit shifted left, right, up, or down, as well as possibly stretched or shrunk vertically and possibly reflected across the \(x\)-axis. Instead, in this section, having convinced ourselves what the basic function \(y = \frac{1}{x}\) looks like, we then argue that any function of the form \[ f(x) = \frac{a}{x-h} + k \] will have a graph that can be produced from that of \(y=\frac{1}{x}\) via transformations: left, right, up, down, reflected, and possibly stretched or rescaled in some way.

Since all graphs of equations of the form \(y = a(x-h)^2+k\) have a similar shape, we made up a word for this shape: We call them parabolas. What, then, will we call transformations of \(y=\frac{1}{x}\)? They are called "hyperbolas."

The graph of \(y=\frac{1}{x}\) or any "transformed" version of it is called a hyperbola. That is to say, the graph of any equation of the form \[ y = \frac{a}{x-h} + k \] is a hyperbola. For example, the graph of \[ y = \frac{2}{x-4} + 5 \] is a hyperbola. (Actually, you can also consider more exotic transformations, such as rotations, which will produce another class of graphs more traditionally called hyperbolas in the high school curriculum, which are those of the form \[ \left(\frac{x-h}{a}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{y-k}{b}\right)^2 = 1 \] But the connection between these two is probably best treated after the study of vectors, so I am not going to pursue that here.)

(By the way, the Greek suffix "bola" has something to do with a meaning of "throwing or casting" as in a rock or other missile—with a related Latin root forming the word "ballistics." Source: etymonline.com.

  4. Is this a legitimate thing we have done to connect the dots like that? How can we be sure that the graph passes through the points we have suggested even though we haven't checked them all?

    Answer:

    A weak argument would be to ask, "Well, why would the graph deviate from the pattern in between the points that we plotted?" Or, fancier, but amounting to the same thing: one might point out that the way we have connected the dots is "the most parsimonious way". But there are more helpful things to say here than that.

    For one, note that the trend for larger (positive or negative) \(x\)-values will continue: when you divide \(1\) by larger and larger numbers, you do indeed get a smaller and smaller fraction. So, the \(y\)-values will trend toward or "approach" \(0\) as \(x\) gets larger.

    Concerning the graph in between the points that we graphed, it is helpful to realize that even though it is easier to conceptualize division of \(1\) into, say, a whole number of pieces, as we have done, you can also conceptualize division of \(1\) into various fractional values. For instance, if you wanted to divide \(1\) into \(5.1\) pieces, well, you'd have to imagine some length (to make it concrete) that would divide up \(1\) meter into five equal pieces that leave just a little bit left over, and that this little bit of the \(1\) meter left over should constitute one-tenth part of the other five equal pieces. (In case you never thought of this before, the length represented by each of the 5 equal pieces is \(\frac{1}{5.1}\) meters. I recommend thinking this through as many ways as you can if you aren't quite sure what I mean or are at all skeptical.) This dividing of \(1\) into \(5.1\) pieces (five and a little, specific amount leftover) instead of \(5\) pieces will result in pieces that are just a little smaller than they would have been if you had divided that \(1\) meter into just \(5\) equal pieces. The point I am trying to make here is that making small adjustments in that value of \(x\) in the denominator of \(\frac{1}{x}\) correspond to small changes in the sizes of the resulting pieces when you carry out the division of \(1\). Having in mind this concrete model of what division means in our minds can convince us that the graph should consist of smooth transitions in the way that we have drawn it. (For the sophisticated reader, I am saying that having this physical model for division is a sure sign that you are dealing with a continuous and even a differentiable function—but you should ignore those words I just mentioned if you have never heard of them—that is, unless you want to try to research them further on your own.)

One interesting thing about this function (and its graph) is that, for very large (positive or negative) \(x\)-values, the \(y\)-values get very close to 0. The result is that the graph gets "squashed" against the line \(y=0\), AKA, the \(x\)-axis. There is a special word for this: The line with equation \(y=0\) is called a horizontal asymptote of \(y=\frac{1}{x}\). At the same time, the graph has a vertical asymptote. After all, for \(x\)-values close to \(0\), the \(y\)-values get very large (positive or negative, depending). The visual result is that the graph gets smashed up against the vertical line \(x=0\), AKA, the \(y\)-axis.

A function (and its graph) have a horizontal asymptote at level \(y=b\) if for very large values of \(x\), the \(y\)-values get closer and closer to \(b\). (It is even possible to have different horizontal asymptotes, one for what happens for large negative \(x\)-values and one for large positive \(x\)-values.) You can also connect this concept to that of end behavior, which we studied in the section on end behavior. So, to use precise mathematical notation like we did in that section, a function \(f\) has horizontal asymptote \(y=b\) if and only if
  • As \(x \rightarrow -\infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow b\),
  • Or, as \(x \rightarrow \infty\), \(f(x) \rightarrow b\)

A function (and its graph) have a vertical asymptote of \(x=a\) provided the \(y\)-values of the function get very large (positive or negative) for \(x\)-values that are getting closer and closer to \(a\). (In fact, there can be different behavior of the \(y\)-values for \(x\)-values close to \(a\) but to the left versus close to \(a\) but to the right.) A function can have multiple vertical asymptotes. To use precise mathematical notation, function \(f\) has vertical asymptote at \(x=a\) if and only if \(f(x) \rightarrow -\infty\) or \(f(x) \rightarrow \infty\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\) from either the left or the right side of \(a\).

You may recall that when we graphed functions of the form \[ y = a(x-h)^2 + k \] part of my recommended strategy was to locate the vertex, \((h,k)\), by thinking about transformations (left, right, up, or down). Then, I would decide whether the parabola "opened up" or "opened down" as well as check how fast the \(y\)-values would change as you take \(x\)-values working outward from the vertex. I will do something similar here—with functions of the form \[ y = \frac{a}{x-h}+ k \] But the difference is partly just a matter of perspective. The point \((h,k)\) here will not even actually be part of the graph. So, it isn't quite right to still call it the vertex. Instead, it will be where the horizontal and vertical asymptotes cross. Put another way: I will basically track where my "axes" are shifting—though, what I mean by that is that I am shifting my frame of reference, so to speak, from the real \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes to the asymptotes of the graph, which will be \(x=h\) and \(y=k\). Then, after that, I will decide which way the \(y\)-values will be placed (above or below the \(x\)-axis) and how fast they will change as you take \(x\)-values moving outward from that center of your frame of reference, \(x=h\). That explanation is probably a little to much to take in, but that's what the following examples are for: to illustrate step-by-step what I am talking about here.

One thing that you mat have to learn "by doing" is that these transformations (e.g.: reflect across the \(x\)-axis, shift up 2) may not work for you if you carry them out in reverse order. Given the graph, give an equation for it of the form \( y = \frac{a}{x-h}+k \)

Answer:

Note that the graph is similar to that of \(y = \frac{1}{x}\) except shifted to the left 3 units and shifted down 1 unit. Therefore, \[ y = \frac{1}{x+3}-1 \] is an equation for it.

Given the graph, give an equation for it of the form \( y = \frac{a}{x-h}+k \)

Answer:

Note that the graph is similar to that of \(y = \frac{1}{x}\) except shifted to the right 4 units, reflected across the \(x\)-axis, and shifted up 1 unit. Therefore, \[ y = \frac{-1}{x-4}+1 \] is an equation for it.
