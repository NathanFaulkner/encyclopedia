In this section, we turn to a concept that we will use again and again. We can represent a set of ordered pairs—what a mathematician would call a relation as a graph on the \((x,y)\)-plane. It's a pretty simple concept—but one that provides a remarkably helpful perspective on equations and functions. (Recall that a function is a special kind of a set of ordered pairs—a special type of relation.)

This may be something you have studied before, but, as it is pretty simple to explain, I'll give a full treatment here. The context is the concept of an ordered pair of real numbers, such as \[ (2, -1) \] But the further context is that these ordered pairs might either represent the input/output pairs associated with a function (which, as discussed in the section on Function Notation is a set of ordered pairs). Or they might be members of the set of all pairs that satisfy (make true) and equation that involve \(x\) and \(y\). For instance, consider the equation \[ x^2 + y^2 = 1 \] Imagine the set of all \((x,y)\)-pairs that make this equation true. For instance, the pair \((x,y) = (1,0)\) is in this set, since, when you let \(x = 1\) and \(y = 0\), you get \[ \textrm{LHS} = x^2 + y^2 = 1^2 + 0^2 = 1 + 0 = 1 = \textrm{RHS} \] They make the equation true; they "satisfy" the equation! So, imagine the set of all pairs that makes this equation true. What we consider here is what the set "looks" like. Do you have any idea what I mean by that? If you do, imagine what it would be like to be asked that question and to have never before learned the concept of a graph. And if you have no idea, just read to the next paragraph.

Having gotten slightly ahead of the discussion, we now need to pause and say just exactly how we might visualize a set of ordered pairs—or, for starters, even just a single ordered pair. The method is basically just a form of "charting." It is something that will be utterly unsurprising to you if you have ever done anything related to maps with longitude and latitude or even used a table with rows and columns or have every played a game of Battleship. Graphing of ordered pairs happens on a chart or playing field that looks like this, but is imagined to extend out infinitely:

First of all, to understand what you are looking at, what you have here is two real lines, one oriented horizontally and the other vertically. Where they cross forms the point 0 on both lines. The grid lines are just sort of decorative—they are there to help you visuallize—but they really don't need to be drawn at all or could be drawn differently. Now, when we plot an ordered pair, like \[ (2,-1) \] we are just going to view it as "instructions" about how far to move from 0 on both lines. The first coordinate tells us how far from 0 and in what direction we are to move on the horizontal line. The second coordinate tells us how far from 0 and in what direction we are to move on the vertical line. We plot the unique "point" that coincides with both of these instructions. So, since the first coordinate is \(2\), we imagine moving from the 0-point (by the way, it is called the origin because that is where you imagine you start) 2 units to the right, and because the second coordinate is \(-1\), we imagine travelling (additionally) one unit down. (If the first coordinate is negative, you move left, whereas if it is positive, you move right. If the second coordinate is negative, you move down, whereas if it is positive you move up.) Now, as mentioned above, we will be mainly concerned with the graph of a (given) equation. What does that mean? For instance, taking again the same example as above, you might consider the equation \[ x^2 + y^2 = 1 \] Its graph is the set of all \((x,y)\) pairs that make the equation true. Of, you might say, it is a visualization of that set. So, in other words, it is just the solution set of that equation—or, again, really a visualization of it. So, in that sense, it is "the same" as what we did in the section on real line graphs : you shade or draw the points that make the relation true. One difference here is that it takes two values (one for \(x\) and one for \(y\)) to make the equation true, while when you are graphing on the real line, you are only concerned with a single variable, say \(x\). To elaborate a little further, you could also graph the solution to inequalities that involve two variables, and you could graph the solution to equations that involve only a single one. In the next example, we are just going to explore the basic, fundamental concept of a "graph of an equation". Then, soon enough, we'll start trying to figure out to draw the whole graph of certain simple equations.

Without graphing (by doing an algebraic check), tell me whether the following points lie on the graph of the given equation.

  1. \((-1, 2)\)

    Answer:

    When you make the substitutions \)x= -1\) and \(y=2\) you get that \[ \textrm{LHS} = (x+2)^2 + 1 = (-1+2)^2 +1 = 1^2 + 1 = 1+1 = 2 = y = \textrm{RHS} \] So, yes the pair \((-1, 2)\) lies on the graph of the equation.
  2. \((1, 0)\)

    Answer:

    When you make the substitutions $x= 1$ and $y=0$ you get that \[ \textrm{LHS} = (x+2)^2 +1 = (1+2)^2 +1 = 3^2 + 1 = 9 + 1 = 10 \neq 0 = y = \textrm{RHS} \] So, no, the pair \((-1, 2)\) does not lie on the graph of the equation.
  3. \((1, 0)\)

    Answer:

    When you make the substitutions $x= 1$ and $y=0$ you get that \[ \textrm{LHS} = (x+2)^2 +1 = (1+2)^2 +1 = 3^2 + 1 = 9 + 1 = 10 \neq 0 = y = \textrm{RHS} \] So, no, the pair \((-1, 2)\) does not lie on the graph of the equation.

We now turn to question that will address again and again as we encounter new and different equations: How do you know that the graph of an equation is? As a warning up front, I want you to know that there is no uniform way to figure out what the graph of an equation looks like. Instead, I will advocate different methods depending on the nature of the equation. However, what I am saying here isn't totally true in the sense that there is a method that you can always apply and will always help out, even if it doesn't efficiently lead you to knowledge of what the graph looks like. I am speaking here of "trial and error". One thing you can always try is to plug in different values for, say, \(x\) and then figure out what \(y\) would have to be. You can then plot the resulting value. I will tend to refer this approach as "just plotting points". The only problem with it is that there (typically) an infinite number of points to try—so you really need some other appraoch to know for sure what a given graph looks like. Time to get going on it!

Now, we just alter this equation bit by bit, seeing each change in the formula changes the resulting graph.

We will graph this equation: \[ y = x + 1 \]

  1. Think about the English descriptions for a relationship between variables like those we gave in the section on developing formulas from tables.     Answer:

    When \(x=0\), \(y = 0 + 1 = 1\). Also, for each increase in \(x\), \(y\) will increase by the same amount.
  2. Graph the equation.

    Answer:

    In terms of the visual, our description means that the graph will be a straight line. The fact that "for each increase in \(x\), \(y\) will increase by the same amount" means that proportional changes in \(x\) lead to proportional changes in \(y\), which makes it a straight line. Since any two points serve to determine a line, we can plot the pair \((0,1)\) and then just one more to be certain of getting the correct line. Plugging in \(x=1\) yields \(y = 1 + 1 = 2\). So, you could plot \((1, 2)\), and then trace the straight line that passes through those points:
    A good observation to make here is that the graph looks the same as that of \(y = x\) only "shifted up one unit."

Note that there was nothing special about the adding 1 in the previous example. We could have added 3 or 5 or any number whatsoever. We also could have subtracted any number, for that matter. So, the general take-away there is that an equation of the form \[ y = x + b \] will run through the point \((0, b)\) and will then "go up 1 for every 1 you run to the right in the \(x\)-direction", following a straight-line course. Since the point \((0,b)\) lies on the \(y\)-axis, it is called the \(y\)-intercept. That is, it represents where the line "intercepts" or intersects the \(y\)-axis.

There is some vocabulary commonly associated with lines that is starting to be of use. It has to do describing how \(y\) changes as \(x\) increases. In the last example, we noted that "for every increase of 1 in \(x\), there is an increase of 2 in \(y\)". On the graph, this resulted in a "steeper" graph, did it not? Thinking of the "steepness"—imagining that one is climbing the graph like a hiker—these degree of steepness is often called "slope". Specifically, we describe the slope with a numerical—2 in the last case! In short, whatever you say the \(y\) increases by when \(x\) increases by 1 is the slope. When you are looking at the graph, the slope is therefore how far the graph "rises" when you "run" (to the right) one unit in the \(x\) direction. Let's come back to that thought after the next example.

Now, back to the concept of "slope". We discussed above the fact that the "slope of a line" can be understood as the amount that \(y\) increases by for each increase of 1 in \(x\). In the past example, we had that the slope was \(\frac{3}{2}\). We also realized that we could instead express this (constant) slope in a different fashion: instead of saying that \(y\) increases by \(\frac{3}{2} = 1.5\) for every increase of 1 in \(x\), it is equivalent to say that \(y\) increases by 3 for every increase of 2 in \(x\). Therefore, we can also remark that the numerator of the slope tells you what \(y\) "rises" by while \(x\) "runs" (specifically, to the right) by 2, which appears in the denominator. For that reason, slope is often referred to (by math teachers) as "rise over run". I will warn you, though, that you must independently remember that that "run" is "to the right".

Now, the astute reader might have a follow-up question in regard to my previous paragraph. What about the example of \[ y = 2x \] We discussed above that slope of the line with this equation is 2. That is, for every increase of 1 in \(x\), \(y\) increases by 2. But in what sense is the number 2 like "rise over run"? Where is the numerator and denominator there? To see that the "rise over run" slogan still works, you would just need to rewrite 2 as a fraction: \[ 2 = \frac{2}{1} \] So, yes, it still is "rise over run".

We have now seen a pretty full variety of examples. We can now generalize on what we are seeing, and make a final remark on the vocabulary. In the exercises, you will be asked to graph equations—like those you have just encountered—that are of the form \[ y = mx + b \] You could also write these as \[ y = b + mx \] if you so chose. Since when \(x = 0\) you have \[ y = b + m\cdot 0 = b + 0 = b \] you have that the graph passes through the point \((0, b)\). Since the \(y\)-axis is placed where \(x = 0\), you have that this point represents the unique point at which the graph crosses the \(y\)-axis. Thus, \(b\) is called the \(y\)-intercept. Further, if you increase \(x\) by 1, you will get that \[ y = b + m \cdot 1 = b + m \] Thus, we see that, after starting at \(y = b\), \(y\) increases by \(m\) as \(x\) increases by 1. With just a little more thought, one can argue that the change in \(y\) is always proportional to the corresponding change in \(x\) and thus we are looking at the equation for a straight line. The value \(m\) is thus called its "slope". As mentioned above it is called slope because it measures the steepness of the increase. Spefically, it tells you how much \(y\) increases (or, decreases, if \(m\) is negative) for each increase of 1 in the \(x\).

Find an equation for the given graph:

Answer:

We can describe this line as follows:
When \(x = 0\), \(y = 3\), and for every 4 that \(x\) increases, we see that \(y\) drops by 5.
We can get capture the first part of this statement by writing \[ y = 3 - \phantom{x} \] and then we can work in the fact that "\(y\) decreases by 5 as \(x\) increases by 4" by continuing: \[ y = 3 - \frac{5}{4}x \] Have I gone too fast? To verify what I have written down, note that if you plug in \(x = 0\), you get \(y = 3\), which agrees with the graph. And if you plug in \(x = 4\), you get \[ y = 3 - \frac{5}{4} \cdot 4 = 3 - 5 = -2 \] which means the other point on the graph also satisfies our equation. We can reason that the graph our equation must be straight line, so our answer must be correct.
