This section is really a direct continuation of the section on point-slope form. Take a look at the following examples.

Develop an equation for the line graphed below.

{% set svg_id = 'svg1' %} {% set points = [[1,5], [3, 2]] %} {% set poly_points = '0.0,-201.250000000000 350.0,323.750000000000 ' %} {% set parameters = { 'svg_x_length': 350, 'svg_y_length': 350, 'cart_x_min': -10, 'cart_x_max': 10, 'cart_x_length': 20, 'cart_y_min': -10, 'cart_y_max': 10, 'cart_y_length': 20 } %} {% include "_static_graph.html" %}

Answer:

One thing we'll need here is the slope. You can determine that by looking at the "rise over run" from one point to the next. In going from \( (1, 5) \) to \( (3, 2) \) the graph "runs" (to the right!—always ask what happens to \(y\) as \(x\) moves to the right) 2 units while \(y\) falls 3 units. You can look at a fall of 3 units as a rise of \(-3\) units. Thus, the "rise over run" for this case is \( \frac{-3}{2} \). Next, we can use "point-slope form" to get an equation—making use of either of the indicated points. For instance, since the graph passes through \((1, 5)\), I know that an equation of the form \[ y = m(x - 1) + 5 \] will work. After all, no matter what \(m\) is, I know that plugging in 1 for \(x\) will yield \(y=5\), which means the pair \((1, 5)\) satisfies the equation. We already found the slope, so we can conclude that a correct equation is \[ y = -\frac{3}{2}(x - 1) + 5 \] As I already mentioned, the pair \((1, 5)\) clearly satisfy this equation; so the graph of this equation has to pass through \( (1, 5) \). (Recall that a graph of an equation is just the set of pairs that make the equation true.) But, we can also verify that the graph of our answer also passes through the other given point by plugging in \(x=3\) and verifying that \(y\) comes out to equal 2. Indeed, when \(x = 3\), \begin{align*} y &= -\frac{3}{2}(x - 1) + 5 \\ &= -\frac{3}{2}(3 - 1) + 5 \\ &= -\frac{3}{2} \cdot 2 + 5 \\ &= -3 + 5 \\ &= 2 \end{align*} As long as you are convinced (rightly) that the graph of our equation is a line, you see that it must also be the correct answer: because its graph passes through the points indicated in red, and there is only one line that passes through any pair of points.

Now, we just consider the same type of question delivered in a slightly different way.

Develop an equation for the line that passes through the given points: \[ (-3, 4), (6, -5) \]

Answer:

A good strategy to pursue is to go for "point-slope form", i.e., an equation of the form \[ y = m(x - x_0) + y_0 \] We have not one but two points to choose from to use as \((x_0, y_0)\). The question is, how to find slope? My personal favorite way is to just draw a sketch of where these points are on the \((x, y)\)-plane and think about "rise over run."
{% set svg_id = 'svg2' %} {% set points = [[-3,4], [6, -5]]%} {% set poly_points = '0.0,-17.5000000000000 350.0,332.500000000000 ' %} {% set parameters = { 'svg_x_length': 350, 'svg_y_length': 350, 'cart_x_min': -10, 'cart_x_max': 10, 'cart_x_length': 20, 'cart_y_min': -10, 'cart_y_max': 10, 'cart_y_length': 20 } %} {% include "_static_graph.html" %}
I can now see pretty easily that while \(x\) "runs" 9 units from \(-3\) to \(6\), \(y\) falls from \(4\) to \(-5\). That's a fall of 9 units, of course. So, my "rise over run" is \[ \frac{-9}{9} = -1 \] That is, my slope is \(m = -1\).

Now, if you are someone that likes to memorize formulas, you could also make note of the fact that, if you are given two points (with different \(x\) coordinates), \[ (x_0, y_0), (x_1, y_1) \] then the slope between them will always be \[ \frac{y_1 - y_0}{x_1 - x_0} \] For example, taking \((-3, 4)\)to be \((x0, y0)\) and \((6, -5)\)to be \((x1, y1)\), you get \[ \frac{y_1 - y_0}{x_1 - x_0} = \frac{-5 - 4}{6 - (-3)} = \frac{-9}{9} = -1 \] But, take my advice and draw a quick sketch, even if you decide to try to memorize that formula. (Or at least visualize in your mind where the points are in relation to each other!)

Now that we have obtained the slope, getting the equation is simple; you can just use the point-slope form. In this case, \[ y = m\left(x - x_0\right) + y_0 \] becomes, in this case, \[ y = -\left(x - (-3)\right) + 4 \] I have chosen the "first" point, \((-3, 4)\) as my \((x_0, y_0)\), but I could have used the other! You get a different equation that way, but the two equations are equivalent. ("The two equations are equivalent": isn't that a strange phrase? And yet it has a meaning! Do you know what it is? If you don't you should drop what you are doing and contemplate it right now! I'll tell you what I have in mind in the next example if you are impatient, though.) Note that it is also common to apply the "double negative", making it positive, rewriting like so: \[ y = -\left(x + 3\right) + 4 \]

Let's check out one more, just in case.

Develop an equation for the line that passes through the given points: \[ (-8, -7), (-2, 6) \]

Answer:

A good strategy to pursue is to go for "point-slope form", i.e., an equation of the form \[ y = m(x - x_0) + y_0 \] We have not one but two points to choose from to use as \((x_0, y_0)\). The question is, how to find slope? My personal favorite way is to just draw a sketch of where these points are on the \((x, y)\)-plane and think about "rise over run."
{% set svg_id = 'svg3' %} {% set points = [[-8,-7], [-2, 6]]%} {% set poly_points = '0.0,373.333333333333 350.0,-385.000000000000 ' %} {% set parameters = { 'svg_x_length': 350, 'svg_y_length': 350, 'cart_x_min': -10, 'cart_x_max': 10, 'cart_x_length': 20, 'cart_y_min': -10, 'cart_y_max': 10, 'cart_y_length': 20 } %} {% include "_static_graph.html" %}
I can now see pretty easily that while \(x\) "runs" \(6\) units from \(-8\) to \(-2\), \(y\) rises from \(-7\) to \(6\). That's a rise of \(13\) units, of course. So, my "rise over run" is \[ \frac{13}{6} \] That is, my slope is \(m = \frac{13}{6}\).

If you want to see the formula introduced in the answer to the previous example in action once again, just take a look: \[ \frac{y_1 - y_0}{x_1 - x_0} = \frac{6 - (-7)}{-2 - (-8)} = \frac{13}{6} \] Or just draw a picture!!

Now that we have obtained the slope, getting the equation is simple; you can just use the point-slope form. In this case, \[ y = m\left(x - x_0\right) + y_0 \] becomes, in this case, \[ y = \frac{13}{6}\left(x - (-8)\right) - 7 \] I have chosen the "first" point, \((-8, -7)\) as my \((x_0, y_0)\), but I could have used the other! You get a different equation that way, but the two equations are equivalent. ("The two equations are equivalent": isn't that a strange phrase? And yet it has a meaning! Do you know what it is? Two equations are equivalent if they are "satisfied" (made true) by the exact same values assigned to variables. From another perspective, it also means that you can deduce one from the other—and back again—using very simple steps of logic, such as adding a number to both sides or doing some other process to both sides.) Note that it is also common to apply the "double negative", making it positive, rewriting like so: \[ y = \frac{13}{6}\left(x + 8\right) - 7 \]
