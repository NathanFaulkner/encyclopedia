In the section entitled Solving by Elimination we learned an algebraic process for solving a system of two linear equations in two unknowns—elimination. The good news is that elimination always works! In fact, elimination can be applied to solving a system of as many linear equations as you want in as many variables as you want. Of course, when the systems get large, it is much preferable to let a computer solve it for you. (Oh, yes—there are indeed computer programs for performing elimination!) The bad news is that elimination doesn't always work—if what you are talking about is solving systems of more arbitrary equations, not necessarily linear ones. So, in preparation for such things, we are going to practice another method of solving which also always works for linear systems but which works on more general systems as well. More good news: Substitution can be easier on some systems, though not all. Just because I am nice, I will be giving you systems to solve via substitution which are actually (in a sense) easier to solve that way compared to using elimination.

Solve the following system using the method of substitution. \[ \begin{cases} x - 3y = 9 \\ 4x - 7y = 26 \end{cases} \]

Answer:

The substitution method works via some of the same observations we made to justify elimination: \(x\) and \(y\) are honest, real numbers, and we are assuming that they represent the very same numbers in both equations. The method is to use one of the equations to solve for one of the variables (whichever one is convenient). I find it convenient to solve the first equation for \(x\)—do you see why? Of course, I get \[ x = 3y +9 \] So, we have a formula for \(x\) in terms of \(y\). To put this into words, what this means that, even though we don't yet know what \(x\) and \(y\) are, we know that, whatever \(y\) turns out to be, \(x\) has to be 9 more thatn 3 times that as-yet-unknown \(y\). Now, we just substitute this formula for \(x\) into the other equation and then solve: \begin{align*} & 4\left(3y + 9\right) - 7y = 26 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 12y + 36 - 7y = 26 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& 5y = -10 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& y = -2 \\ \end{align*} Finally, sub-in this value for \(y\) into your formula for \(x\) to get the value of \(x\): \[ x = 3\cdot (-2) + 9 = -6 + 9 = 3 \] So, our final answer is \((x,y) = (3, -2)\). Checking is always a good idea: \[ 3 - 3(-2) = 3 + 6 = 9 \] and \[ 4\cdot 3 - 7(-2) = 12 + 14 = 26 \] So, yes, the answer is correct.

Next, we look at two examples that illustrate what happens in the substitution process when the system has no solution or infinite solutions lying along a single line.

Solve the following system using the method of substitution. \[ \begin{cases} -6x + 3y = 9\\ 2x - y = -3 \end{cases} \]

Answer:

This time, I find it convenient to solve the second equation for \(y\). I get \[ y = 2x +3 \] Now, I sub that in for \(y\) in the first equation and solve for \(x\): \begin{align*} & -6x + 3(2x+3) =9\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -6x + 6x + 9 = 9 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9 = 9 \end{align*} What does that mean? Well (making a similar comment to an example in the section on solving by elimination), strictly speaking, that 9 equals 9 isn't any kind of news: you already knew that was true before doing any of that work. But we can still take it as a reliable indicator that the solution set of this system consists in an infinite set of pairs lying along the line with equation \(2x - y = 3\). After all, in hindsight, we can observe that the first equation is just the second with both sides multiplied by \(-3\). So, they have the same graph. The solution set is all \((x, y)\)-pairs lying along the graph of either of the equations (same graph either way).

Next, we look at an example that results in an answer of "no solution."

Solve the following system using the method of substitution. \[ \begin{cases} -6x + 3y = 9\\ 2x - y = -12 \end{cases} \]

Answer:

Again, solve the second equation for \(y\): \[ y = 2x +12 \] Sub that in for \(y\) in the first equation and solve for \(x\): \begin{align*} & -6x + 3(2x+12) =9\\ \Rightarrow \quad & -6x + 6x + 36 = 9 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 36 = 9 \end{align*} That's a false statement, so there is no \((x,y)\)-pair that could make both equations true at the same time: "No solution."
