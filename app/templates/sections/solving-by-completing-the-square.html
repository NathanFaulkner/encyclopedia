In the section entitled, Solving Quadratics: Only One x, we solved quadratic equations in which there was only one occurrence of the variable \(x\). This extended the range of possible quadratics we are able to solve beyond the "factorable" cases covered in sections Solving by Factoring, Part 1 and Part 2. Actually, the techniques that we developed in the section Solving Quadratics: Only One x, enable us to "solve" any quadratic equation of the form \[ a(x-h)^2 +k = 0 \] However, we also learned in the section on putting standard form into vertex form that any quadratic can be put into the form \[ a(x-h)^2 + k \] Thus, the skills from these previous sections can be leveraged to solve any quadratic equation whatsoever! On the other hand, there is a little bit of "fine print" here. We did observe that some quadratics will not have a "real" solution. But the process we develop here (which, again, is just a combination of skills developed in the other sections linked to here) will also uncover exactly when that will happen.

In yet other cases, you will find that there is no (real) solution to the quadratic:

Solve the following. \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = 0 \]

Answer, via Method 1:

As discussed in the section entitled, Quadratics in Standard Form we can take any quadratic given initially in standard form, \[ ax^2 + bx + c \] and rewrite it in vertex form \[ a(x-h)^2 + k \] There are different ways to accomplish this that you might review by clicking the link just referenced, but I will review one way here. Start by writing out what your goal is, namely to figure out what \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will make this equation true: \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = a(x-h)^2 + k \] One approach is to multiply out the right side and then make a comparison of the coefficients on each side: \begin{align*} x^2 - 10x + 18 &= a(x-h)^2 + k \\ &= a\left((x-h)(x-h)\right) + k \\ &= a\left(x^2 - 2hx + h^2\right) + k \\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \end{align*} It also helps to rewrite the left side right next to the end result from rewriting the right side: \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] The first easy observation to make is that setting \(a = 1\) will at least make the \(x^2\) terms match up. I proceed by subbing in \(a=1\): \begin{align*} & x^2 - 10x + 18 = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 - 10x + 18 = x^2 -2hx + h^2 + k \end{align*} Next, I compare the coefficients of \(x\) on the left and the right sides. On the left, we have \(-10x\) (so, the coefficient is \(-10\). On the right we have \(-2hx\). So, what can we set \(h\) to to make this work out? Evidently, we just need to figure out what real number \(h\) makes \(-2h = -10\). Of course, the answer is \(h=5\). So, we make that substitution: \begin{align*} & x^2 - 10x + 18 = x^2 -2hx + h^2 + k\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 -10x + 18 = x^2 -10x + 25 + k\\ \end{align*} Finally, we see that on the left we have \(18\) sitting there and on the right, we have \(25 + k\). Observe that if we just set \(k = -7\) all of this will work out! Now we just refer back to our original vertex form, and sub-in the \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) that we have figured out: \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = a(x-h)^2 + k \] becomes, after subbing in \(a = 1\), \(h = 5\), and \(k = -7\), \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = (x-5)^2 - 7 \] Now, we recall that our intent was to solve the equation \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = 0 \] We have just learned that the left side of this equation is equivalent to \((x-5)^2-7\), so we can make that change. That is, it is equivalent to solve this: \[ (x-5)^2-7 = 0 \] We do this by following the approach modeled in the section, Solving Quadratics: Only One x: \begin{align*} &(x-5)^2-7 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & (x-5)^2 = 7 \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x-5 = -\sqrt{7} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5 = \sqrt{7}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = 5-\sqrt{7} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 5+\sqrt{7}\\ \end{align*}

Answer, via Method 2:

Another approach, called "completing the square", involves adding or subtracting numbers from both sides until one side follows either the "special pattern" \[ (x+B)^2 = x^2 + 2Bx + B^2 \] or \[ (x-B)^2 = x^2 - 2Bx + B^2 \] It may be helpful to review the section on factoring special patterns. Here is how this method works in this case. Beginning with \[ x^2 - 10x + 18 = 0 \] I can simply subtract the \(18\) from both sides, leaving myself a generous blank space where the \(18\) used to be: \[ x^2 - 10x \phantom{+\, 18} = -18 \] Next, I imagine how to "fill in the blank" in such a way as to make the left side an instance of the pattern \[ x^2 - 2Bx + B^2 \] The point is, I really want it to be equivalent to something of the form \[ (x-B)^2 \] They key is to realize that whatever coefficient you have on \(x\), you just need to take half of it (to get rid of the 2, thus extracting the unknown \(B\)), which you then square. That is, I am viewing the term \(10x\) as being of the form \(2Bx\). My goal is to supply the missing part, the \(B^2\). So, I just take half of \(10\)—my "\(2B\)" in this case—to get the \(B\). (That means \(B\) is \(5\) in this case!) Then I just square that to get \(B^2\). Thus, I choose to add \(5^2 = 25\) to both sides to complete this pattern. Since it makes the left side follow one of the "square" special patterns, this trick is called "completing the square." Here is what it looks like after I add \(25\) to both sides: \begin{align*} & x^2 - 10x \phantom{+\, 25\,} = -18\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 - 10x + 25 = 7 \end{align*} Now, I observe that the left side is equivalent to \((x-5)^2\)—to be sure, that was the reason I chose to add 25 to both sides!—and I rewrite it as such: \[ (x-5)^2 = 7 \] Now, I can proceed using the methods of the section, Solving Quadratics: Only One x: \begin{align*} & (x-5)^2 = 7 \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x-5 = -\sqrt{7} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5 = \sqrt{7}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = 5-\sqrt{7} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 5+\sqrt{7}\\ \end{align*}

That's pretty much it! The only way to complicate these a bit is to introduce more terms. But that is easy to handle: you just combine like terms as a first step. Still, let's look a couple examples like that to reassure you on those as well.

Solve the following. \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 43 = -4 \]

Answer, via Method 1:

This method involves combining like terms by pulling everything over to one side and then rewriting what results in vertex form. In this case, to combine like terms, all I can do is to add \(4\) to both sides. (It turns out that this step isn't all that important in this case since it involves only combining numbers whereas combining terms with \(x\) or \(x^2\) would be useful.) Here is what I get: \begin{align*} & 2x^2 + 20x + 43 = -4\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 0 \end{align*}

Next, as discussed in the section entitled, Quadratics in Standard Form we can take any quadratic given initially in standard form, \[ ax^2 + bx + c \] and rewrite it in vertex form, \[ a(x-h)^2 + k \] There are different ways to accomplish this that you might review by clicking the link just referenced, but I will review one way here. Start by writing out what your goal is, namely to figure out what \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will make this equation true: \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = a(x-h)^2 + k \] One approach is to multiply out the right side and then make a comparison of the coefficients on each side: \begin{align*} 2x^2 + 20x + 47 &= a(x-h)^2 + k \\ &= a\left((x-h)(x-h)\right) + k \\ &= a\left(x^2 - 2hx + h^2\right) + k \\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \end{align*} It also helps to rewrite the left side right next to the end result from rewriting the right side: \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] The first easy observation to make is that setting \(a = 2\) will at least make the \(x^2\) terms match up. I proceed by subbing in \(a=2\): \begin{align*} & 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 2x^2 -4hx + 2h^2 + k \end{align*} Next, I compare the coefficients of \(x\) on the left and the right sides. On the left, we have \(20x\) (so, the coefficient is \(20\)). On the right we have \(-4hx\). So, what can we set \(h\) to to make this work out? Evidently, we just need to figure out what real number \(h\) makes \(-4h = 20\). Of course, the answer is \(h=-5\). So, we make that substitution: \begin{align*} & 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 2x^2 - 4hx + 2h^2 + k\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 2x^2 + 20x + 50 + k \end{align*} Finally, we see that on the left we have \(47\) sitting there and on the right, we have \(50 + k\). Observe that if we just set \(k = -3\) all of this will work out! Now we just refer back to our original vertex form, and sub-in the \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) that we have figured out: \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = a(x-h)^2 + k \] becomes, after subbing in \(a = 2\), \(h = -5\), and \(k = -3\), \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 2(x+5)^2 - 3 \] Now, we recall that our intent was to solve the equation \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 0 \] We have just learned that the left side of this equation is equivalent to \(2(x+5)^2 - 3\), so we can make that change. That is, it is equivalent to solve this: \[ 2(x+5)^2 - 3 = 0 \] We do so following the approach modeled in the section, Solving Quadratics: Only One x: \begin{align*} &2(x+5)^2-3 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & 2(x+5)^2 = 3 \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & (x+5)^2 = \frac{3}{2} \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x+5 = -\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 5 = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -5-\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -5+\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}\\ \end{align*}

Answer, via Method 2:

Another approach, called "completing the square", involves adding or subtracting numbers from both sides until one side follows either the "special pattern" \[ (x+B)^2 = x^2 + 2Bx + B^2 \] or \[ (x-B)^2 = x^2 - 2Bx + B^2 \] It may be helpful to pause here and review the section on factoring special patterns. Here is how this method works in this case. Beginning with \[ 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 0 \] We want to rewrite the left side in the form \[ x^2 + 2Bx + B^2 \] So, for starters, we need to divide both sides by the coefficient on \(x^2\), namely \(2\): \begin{align*} & 2x^2 + 20x + 47 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 +10x + \frac{47}{2} = 0 \end{align*} As you can see, this results in a fraction in this case, which isn't so nice, but we can live with it. Next, as in the example above, I can simply subtract the "constant term" from both sides—that is, the part that is just a number and does not involve a variable—which, in this case, is \(\frac{47}{2}\). Again, I leave myself a nice blank space where the constant was in preparation for replacing it with a number that makes the left side follow one of those special patterns involving a square—that is, I am getting ready to "complete the square". \[ x^2 + 10x \phantom{+\, 18} = -\frac{47}{2} \] Next, I imagine how to "fill in the blank" in such a way as to make the left side an instance of the pattern \[ x^2 + 2Bx + B^2 \] The point is, I really want it to be equivalent to something of the form \[ (x+B)^2 \] They key is to realize that whatever coefficient you have on \(x\), you just need to take half of it (to get rid of the 2, thus extracting the unknown \(B\)), which you then square. That is, I am viewing the term \(10x\) as being of the form \(2Bx\). My goal is to supply the missing part, the \(B^2\). So, I just take half of \(10\)—my "\(2B\)" in this case—to get the \(B\). (That means \(B\) is \(5\) in this case!) Then I just square that to get \(B^2\). Thus, I choose to add \(5^2 = 25\) to both sides to complete this pattern. Since it makes the left side follow one of the "square" special patterns, this trick is called "completing the square." Here is what it looks like after I add \(25\) to both sides: \begin{align*} & x^2 + 10x \phantom{+\, 25\,} = -\frac{47}{2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 + 10x + 25 = -\frac{47}{2} + 25\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 + 10x + 25 = -\frac{47}{2} + \frac{50}{2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 + 10x + 25 = \frac{3}{2}\\ \end{align*} Now, I observe that the left side is equivalent to \((x+5)^2\)—to be sure, that was the reason I chose to add 25 to both sides!—and I rewrite it as such: \[ (x+5)^2 = \frac{3}{2} \] Now, I can proceed using the methods of the section, Solving Quadratics: Only One x: \begin{align*} & (x+5)^2 = \frac{3}{2} \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x+5 = -\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 5 = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -5-\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -5+\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}\\ \end{align*}

On this last example, I'm only going to demonstrate the method of "completing the square" rather than showing both methods as I did above.

Solve the following. \[ - 5 x^{2} - 39 x + 130 = - 7 x^{2} - 7 x - 12 \]

Answer, via Method 2:

"Completing the square" involves adding or subtracting numbers from both sides until one side follows either the "special pattern" \[ (x+B)^2 = x^2 + 2Bx + B^2 \] or \[ (x-B)^2 = x^2 - 2Bx + B^2 \] It may be helpful to pause here and review the section on factoring special patterns. Here is how this method works in this case. To begin, we combine like terms, bringing all terms to, say, the left side, and leaving \(0\) on the other: \begin{align*} & - 5 x^{2} - 39 x + 130 = - 7 x^{2} - 7 x - 12\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & 2x^2 - 32x + 142 = 0 \\ \end{align*} Next, stay alert to the possibility that you might be able to simplify a little bit. In this case, I notice that each of the coefficients is even, so I can divide both sides by \(2\): \begin{align*} & 2x^2 - 32x + 142 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 - 16x + 71 = 0 \\ \end{align*} Next, as in the example above, I can simply subtract the "constant term" from both sides—that is, the part that is just a number and does not involve a variable—which, in this case, is \(71\). Again, I leave myself a nice blank space where the constant was in preparation for replacing it with a number that makes the left side follow one of those special patterns involving a square—that is, I am getting ready to "complete the square". \[ x^2 - 16x \phantom{+\, 18} = -71 \] Next, I imagine how to "fill in the blank" in such a way as to make the left side an instance of the pattern \[ x^2 - 2Bx + B^2 \] The point is, I really want it to be equivalent to something of the form \[ (x-B)^2 \] They key is to realize that whatever coefficient you have on \(x\), you just need to take half of it (to get rid of the 2, thus extracting the unknown \(B\)), which you then square. That is, I am viewing the term \(16x\) as being of the form \(2Bx\). My goal is to supply the missing part, the \(B^2\). So, I just take half of \(16\)—my "\(2B\)" in this case—to get the \(B\). (That means \(B\) is \(8\) in this case!) Then I just square that to get \(B^2\). Thus, I choose to add \(8^2 = 64\) to both sides to complete this pattern. Since it makes the left side follow one of the "square" special patterns, this trick is called "completing the square." Here is what it looks like after I add \(64\) to both sides: \begin{align*} & x^2 - 16x \phantom{+\, 64\,} = -71\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x^2 - 16x + 64 = -7\\ \end{align*} Now, I observe that the left side is equivalent to \((x-8)^2\)—to be sure, that was the reason I chose to add 64 to both sides!—and I rewrite it as such: \[ (x-8)^2 = -7 \] Now, I observe that there is actually no (real) solution, since on the left, I have a square and, on the right, I have a negative number. You can't square any real number and have it come out negative, so that's the end of that.
