Now, let me give you one explanation for why we are bothering with such a spurious notion. I am going to argue that adding in this suspicious "number" \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) has much in common with our previously having argued that negative numbers should also be regarded as "a thing." In fact, while complex/imaginary numbers are regarded by students with suspicion—as they also were by early Renaissance mathematicians who were struggling with algorithms like the quadratic formula and encountered the notion as you have—negative numbers are also regarded by students (initially) with suspicion and also were historically. Why should we say that \(-1\) is a number? Why should we start inventing notions like \((-1)\cdot(-1) = 1\)? Well, it's because we want full freedom of movement on the real line: we want to be able to move left as well as to the right by certain distances. That is, the sign of a real number helps you tell about direction, whereas the "number" part of the number tells you the distance traveled. The reason we invent the further rules like \((-1)(-1)=1\) is because that is the sort of thing that enables us to treat at one and the same time changes in direction and also regular multiplication and division (meaning just as with adding in \(i\), "all the rules apply", except we add a few more in, namely that \((-1)a = -a\), \(-(-1) = 1\), and \(1 + (-1) = 0\) and then we just deal with the consequences of that choice, among them that \((-1)(-1) = 1\).

In brief, the idea of negative numbers is, first of all, the desire to express direction in a convenient manner and, secondly, streamlining the system to work with "all the old rules." The full story of imaginary numbers has to do with the desire to do "something similar" for 2-d space. Here is what I mean: You are familiar with the idea of using coordinates like \((2,3)\) for points in the \((x,y)\) plane. So, since it takes two coordinates to specify a point on the plane, it is said to be two-dimensional. Now, in this space, you can move forward, back, and side-to-side. You can strike off in any direction you like! For instance, negative numbers do come into play here, too; you can plot \((-2,-3)\) and see for yourself that it will lie in exactly at the same distance away from the origin \((0,0)\) as \((2,3)\) but in the opposite direction. So, how can we represent the other movements? Imagine that you are a programmer working on problems of computerized animation, and you want to have a figure spin around with that figure specified as a bunch of \((x,y)\) pairs (which are lit up as colored pixels on the screen, of course). Well, then, you want a way to conveniently capture that rotation mathematically. You may be rather surprised to learn the complex numbers—the villain \(i\)—is exactly the thing! Don't believe me? Well, as rotations are really about angles, which is really what trig is all about—we won't do a full treatment of the concept here, but I can get you started in the following activity:

  4. Now, what we are going to do is, first of all, to think about a complex number as a set of coordinates—that is, as specifiying directions in the plane. (A mathematician would call a complex number a vector and he or she would use the same work to describe a pair of \((x,y)\)-coordinates). That is, we are going set up an obvious "visual" comparsion between the complex number \(a+bi\) and \((a,b)\). Let \(\sim\) be a symbol suggesting this obvious comparison, like so: \[ a + bi\sim (a,b) \] For example, \[ 2 + 3i \sim (2,3) \] Now, what do you get when you multiply by \(i\)? Simplify the left side and the fill-in-the-blank on the right side of the \(\sim\) symbol: \[ i(a+ib) \sim [?] \]

    Answer:

    \[ i(a+ib) = ia + i^2 b = -b + ia \sim (-b, a) \]
  5. Conclude with me that, viewed as coordinates in the \((x,y)\)-plane via this relation \(\sim\) just discussed, \(i(a+bi)\) is just \(a+bi\) rotated \(90^\circ\) counter-clockwise about the origin. The point is that multiplication by \(i\) is a way of representing rotation in the plane.

    Answer:

    The key observation is that given any point \((a,b)\) in the \((x,y)\)-plane and imagining the ray, \(\mathcal R\), pointing from the origin to this point, the point \((-b, a)\) will lie on a ray emanating from the origin that is perpendicular (specificall, rotated \(90^\circ\) counter-clockwise) to \(\mathcal R\). Since multiplication by \(i\) converts the point \[ (a, b) \sim a + ib \] to \[ -b + ia \sim (-b, a) \] it follows that multiplication by \(i\) "represents" rotation of a vector by \(90^\circ\) counter-clockwise about the origin.

You can also demonstate that multiplying complex number \(z\) by another complex number \(w\), the latter representing a point lying at distance 1 from the origin, will result in the rotation of \(z\) about the origin an angle congruent to that between the \(x\)-axis and \(w\). Treating these other, arbitrary angles, though, involves further discussion of right triangles and angles, so we'll skip that for now.

Find all solutions. Instead of merely saying there is "no solution" when you encounter a negative under a square root, write those solutions in terms of \(i\).
  1. \(x^2 = -1\)

    2. Answer:

    \(x = \pm\sqrt{-1} = \pm i\). That is, both \(x=i\) and \(x=-i\) are solutions. In particular, \[ (-i)^2 = (-i)(-i) = +i^2 = -1 \]
  2. \(x^2 = -4\)

    3. Answer:

    \(x = \pm 2i\). To check this, note that \[ (2i)^2 = (2i)(2i)= 4i^2 = -4 \]
  3. \(x^2 = -3\)

    4. Answer:

    \(x = \pm\sqrt{-3} = \pm i \sqrt{3}\). To check this, note that \begin{align*} (i\sqrt{3})^2 &= (i\sqrt{3})(i\sqrt{3})\\ & = i^2 \cdot 3 \\ & = -1 \cdot 3 \\ & = -3 \end{align*}

Find all solutions. Instead of merely saying there is "no solution" when you encounter a negative under a square root, write those solutions in terms of \(i\). \[ 3x^2 - 4x +2 = 0 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} 3x^2 - 4x +2 &= 0 \\ x &= \frac{4\pm\sqrt{16-4 \cdot 3\cdot 2}}{2\cdot 3}\\ x &= \frac{4\pm\sqrt{16-24}}{6}\\ x &= \frac{4\pm\sqrt{-8}}{6}\\ x &= \frac{4\pm i\sqrt{8}}{6}\\ \end{align*}

Find all solutions. Instead of merely saying there is "no solution" when you encounter a negative under a square root, write those solutions in terms of \(i\). \[ 4x^2 - 7x +2 = 0 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} 4x^2 - 5x +2 &= 0 \\ x &= \frac{5\pm\sqrt{25-4 \cdot 4\cdot 2}}{2\cdot 4}\\ x &= \frac{5\pm\sqrt{25-32}}{8}\\ x &= \frac{5\pm\sqrt{-7}}{8}\\ x &= \frac{5\pm i\sqrt{7}}{8}\\ \end{align*}
