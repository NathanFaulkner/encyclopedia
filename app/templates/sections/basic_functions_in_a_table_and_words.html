In this section, we study functions. As always, my goal will be try to make the concept meaningful to you—to ask what the meaning of the symbols and concepts are. In other sections, I tried to make equations and inequalities meaningful to you by talking about what they mean in terms of numbers and relationships on the real line. The concept of function—you could say—is more abstract in that it isn't just about numbers any more or even sets of numbers but about relationships between sets of numbers. But, in another sense, the concept of function the most concrete—the most real world of the concepts that we can discuss. To see what I mean by that, in this section—which is really mean to be an introduction to the concept of functions—we will look at functions represented in two ways: in tables and in words.

What is a function? All it is is a special type of pairing between sets of numbers. So, a function is, literally, a relationship. (Actually, mathematicians use the technical, jargon word "relation".) But functions are special types of relationships. Briefly, they are input/output relationships. What do I mean by that? Good question! Time to start looking at some concrete examples to get an idea.

Consider the very basic relationship between the price of a pizza and the number of toppings you order. Actually, this could be a fairly complicated thing!—depending if the pizza place prices different toppings differently (e.g.: standard toppings versus premium, etc.) or if they start offering deals when you order more toppings ("buy two toppings at regular price, get the third one free!"). But our purpose here is just to get a basic mental scheme of "what a function is", so we can just start with your basic scenario of pricing. Therefore, below, I give you a hypothetical pricing structure for a kind of a gourmet-ish, sit-down pizza place (brand name withheld!)

  1. To start off, I often like to ask "What does this mean?" In this case, the meaning is obvious. Instead, our task is to attach a new word to a familiar concept. Here is the concept: The price of your pizza is determined by the number of toppings you order. The new word we attach here—which is thus given at least tentative meaning through this example—is "function". A function relationship is what you have whenever you have the generic situation
    [One variable] is determined by [some other variable].
    In this case, we say that
    The [one variable] is a function of [the other variable].
    In other words, the word function works (so far) on only in the phrase "is a function of"; and all that it does is to provide another kind of fancy-sounding way of saying "is determined by".

    So, as an initial test of your understanding here, practice the phrase "is a function of" by correctly applying it to the situation of pricing pizza. Express the variables in your answers in the same way thay they are expressed in the chart.

    Answer:

    The price of the pizza is a function of the number of toppings you buy.
  2. Construct an English sentence (or two) that fully captures the relationship between the price of a pizza and the number of toppings you buy. What I'm looking for is a sentence (or two) that is so detailed, someone could use just your information and reconstrut that table—that is, out simply, they would completely know how much they are going to pay for a pizza no matter how many toppings they are going to buy. For instance, just imagine that you work at this nameless downtown pizza place and you are taking an order over the phone from someone who doesn't have Internet and just really doesn't have any information about the price except what you tell them.

    Answer:

    A plain, cheese pizza costs $12 and each additional topping is $1.50.
  3. Now, hopefully, that previous exercise wasn't very hard. But, would you believe me if I told you that if you can construct an English description that captures the price, then you can just as easily construct a mathematical formula (or equation) that will do the same thing? Sure you can! You see, an equation is also just a sentence. It's just a sentence constructed from mathematical symbols. For instance, when I say \[ x = 3 \] I have just uttered a sentence with a subject, a verb, and an object. The sentence (as you well know) is just "(The number) \(x\) equals 3." So, the subject of the sentence is \(x\), the verb is "equals", and the object is 3.

    So, your job is to construct a math sentence that does the same thing as the English sentence you already developed. Use the letter \(P\) to represent the price of the pizza and use \(n\) to represent the number of toppings.

    Answer:

    Your equation is going to proceed in the order "subject, verb, object". So, the question is, what is the subject of your math sentence going to be? Well, there are only two nouns that are relevant to the situation: the price \(P\) of the pizza and the number \(n\) of toppings. So, which is the subject of the sentence? Maybe this one is easy. But the answer to this question in other situations can always be answered in the same way. Think about the phrases
    [one variable] is determined by [some other]
    which we just learned can also be phrased
    [one variable] is a function of [the other].
    The way the "[one variable]" gets determined by the other usual involves some details, so you can always start with the "[variable]" as your subject and leave the details for later. So, I start this way: \[ P = \] Next, I take a deep breath and start rewriting my English formula as a math formula, translating each detail faithfully one-by-one. For example, I know that a plain, cheese costs $12, so I start with that. I also know more will have to be added to it, so I get ready for that at the same time. So, I write this next: \[ P = 12 + \] Feeling pretty good! Now, I just have to figure out how to incorporate the idea that "for every additional topping (besides cheese) that I order, it will cost $1.50 more". For example, if I order one topping, I would need to add just 1.5, but if I ordered two toppings, I would add 3. We're talking repeated addition of 1.5—and "repeated addition" is a way of understanding what multiplication about, no? So, yep, I just multiply. "For every additional topping, add another 1.5" gets faithfully incorporated into our formula in this final step: \[ P = 12 + 1.5 n \]
  4. As a follow-up to the previous question, compute the price of a pizza that has (hypothetically-speaking) 10 toppings on it.

    Answer:

    The nice thing about a formula like the one we just developed is that you can just "plug in" to find whatever value you want. So, for instance, when \(n = 10\), we find \[ P = 12 + 1.5 \cdot 10 = 12 + 15 = 27 \] So, the pizza would cost $27.

  1. Write an English sentence that perfectly captures the relationship between the height of a bamboo stalk and the number of days since "Day 0." Someone else should be able to reconstruct your table using just your English sentence.

    Answer:

    The bamboo starts out at height 15 ft on "Day 0", and then grows by 1.1 ft everyday after that.

  2. Develop a "math sentence"—that is, an equation—that captures the relationship between the height of the bamboo on any given day after "Day 0". Use the letter \(h\) for the height of the bamboo and the letter \(x\) for the number of days that have passed since Day 0.

    Answer:

    Once again, I ask myself, "What shall be the subject of my math sentence?" The answer to that will always be "the variable that is being determined by (or, that is a function of) the other." One can readily think of the height as being determined by the number of days it spends growing. The height is a function of the time spent growing. Thus, I have identified that it convenient way to begin my equation is to write \[ h = \]

    And, again, I can construct the equation bit saying in my head a sentence describing the situation and, bit by bit, transcribing the different elements of the sentence. I know the height starts out at 15 ft on Day 0—and then grows from there—so I continue by writing: \[ h = 15 + \] Next, I observe in the table that the bamboo grows 1.1 ft in the first day and continues that pattern from there on. So, it adds 1.1 ft to its height for each additional day. That's repeated addition—that is, multiplication—so I know how to finish it off: \[ h = 15 + 1.1 x \] Note that you can also check this answer by plugging in different values for \(d\) and see if the formula generates the correct height as given in the table. Indeed, when you substitute \(d = 0\), you get \[h = 15 + 1.1 \cdot 0 = 15 + 0 = 15\] Similarly, when \(d = 1\), you get \[ h = 15 + 1.1 \cdot 1 = 15 + 1.1 = 16.1 \] Hopefully, it's easy to see that the entire table could be generated in this manner. Our formula is correct!

  3. Compute the height of the bamboo after 14 days.

    Answer:

    We plug in \(x = 14\): \[ h = 15 + 1.1 \cdot 14 = 15 + 15.4 = 30.4 \] So, the bamboo would be 30.4 ft tall.
