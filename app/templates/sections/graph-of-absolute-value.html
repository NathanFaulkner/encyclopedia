In the sections on simple graphing and point-slope form and others, you will have hopefully gotten a lot better at graphing lines. I hope that you are very comfortable with them. However, not every graph is a line, right? In this section, we will be graphing absolute value functions—and they will not have lines for their graphs!

By absolute value function, I mean a function whose outputs can be obtained using a formula of the form \[ f(x) = a|x-b|+c, \] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) will be some numbers that vary from problem to problem For example, \[ f(x) = -2|x-7|+3 \] is one such function. At some point soon, you will be able to jot down a graph of this without much more trouble than you have in graphing lines!

Whenever you are faced with the task of graphing something new, you should start with the good-old "just plot points" method. What I mean by that is to realize, first of all, that the graph of an equation is just (a picture of) the set of pairs that make the equation true. So, you just plug in values for one of the variables (usually \(x\)) and figure out what the other variable has to be to make it true. Then, you plot those points. Let's do that with the most basic absolute value function via the following example.

You are going to "plot points" until you realize what the graph of \(y=|x|\) looks like. You should repeat this activity on your own whenever you forget what it looks like! However, I want you to know up front that this is not the method I suggest you use on any of the exercises in this section. Instead, this is a tedious method that you do to just get started somewhere. We will adopt a much more stream-lined method in the examples below—but only after going through the tedious, point-by-point process at least once.

All you have to do is to plug in a nice variety of \(x\) values (plug in 0, plug in some positive numbers, plug in some negative numbers), and keep going until a light bulb goes on for you—and you are utterly certain what the graph looks like.

Since this may be your first time in doing such a task, I will guide you through it. But in the future, I expect you to be able to plot points on your own.

Explain how you can know how to connect the dots.

    Answer:

    After having gotten our hands on it by plotting a few points, it is now perfectly clear how the process works: for every positive \(x\), the \(y\)-value coming out of \(y = |x|\) is just \(x\). That means that, for positive \(x\), the graph of \(y = |x|\) will just follow along with the graph of \(y = x\). It is clear that this will be a straight, diagonal line along which you find all the pairs whose \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates are the same. Next, for negative \(x\), the \(y\)-value coming out of \(y = |x|\) will just be the positive version of \(x\). So, the graph will also follow along a straight, diagonal line for negative \(x\)—falling on those pairs whose \(y\)-value has the same magnitude as \(x\) but is positive instead of negative. If you think about it, since the negative of a negative is positive, this means that, for negative \(x\), the graph of \(y = |x|\) falls along the graph of \(y = -x\). These two lines meet in the middle at the origin, the point \((0,0)\), which is, of course, also a pair that satisfies \(y = |x|\) and hence lies on the graph, too.

Now that you know what the graph of \(y=|x|\) looks like, it ends up that—after some practice with a certain set of methods and perspectives—you can graph any function of the form \[ f(x) = a|x-b|+c \] without much trouble. To plot these, we won't just "plot points"—even though you should also keep that method at the ready as a back-up or as a way to check your work—but we are instead going to learn the very useful—and pretty fun!—method of "transformations.""

Before I just come out and show you the method, though, it is very helpful for you to start discovering the method for yourself. That's what the following activities are about.

Graph the function \[ f(x)=-|x| \] Start by "plotting points" but then try to quickly observe what is going on. How does the graph of \(y=-|x|\) relate to the graph of \(y=|x|\)? Describe the transformation in words, and explain what it is going on with it. How does it work?

Answer:

You basically just have to get your order of operations right on this one. One key insight is that the absolute value signs —in addition to being absolute value—work like parentheses for the purposes of correct order of operations. Ask yourself, what happens when you plug in various values for \(x\)? If you plug in, say, 1 for \(x\), then your computation is: \begin{align*} -|x| &= -|1| & \textrm{Plug in 1}\\ &= -1 & \textrm{Computed the value of } |1| \end{align*} If you plug in \(x = -1\), here's what it looks like: \begin{align*} -|x| &= -|-1| & \textrm{Plug in } -1\\ &= -1 & \textrm{Computed the value of } |-1| \end{align*} To clarify, the computation is equivalent to this: \[ -|-1| = -\left({\color{green}|-1|}\right) = -\left({\color{green}1}\right) = -1 \] Of course, if you plug in any other number, then something similar happens: a positive number comes out of the absolute value and then the negative is applied. The end result is that the \(y\)-value comes out negative. (The one exception is \(x=0\), which would go like this: \begin{align*} -|x| &= -|0| & \textrm{Plug in } 0\\ &= -0 & \textrm{Computed the value of } |0|\\ &= 0\\ \end{align*} Note that the negative of 0 is indeed 0. After all, on the real line, "negative" just means "go left". So, \(-0\) means "go left 0 spaces". Of course, that means staying put. So, \(-0 = 0\).) Since negative \(y\)-values are plotted "in reverse order" (relative to the positive values) going down the vertical \(y\)-axis, you get that the graph of \(y = -|x|\) will be a mirror image of that of \(y = |x|\). That is why it is said to be "reflected across the \(x\)-axis". In other words, the \(x\) is like the mirror and the graph of \(y = -|x|\) is like the image of \(y = |x|\) "in the mirror". (In fact, the word "image" is used in a technical sense by mathematicians for this and other similar purposes!)
