Now, what's the purpose of factoring? The really useful thing about factoring is described by the following general fact.

If \(A\) and \(B\) are real numbers and \[ A\cdot B = 0 \] then either \(A=0\) or \(B=0\) (or both).

Proof:

Say that \(A\) and \(B\) are real numbers and that \(A\cdot B = 0\). Suppose, too, that \(A\neq 0\). In that case, we can divide both sides of the equation by \(A\): \begin{align*} &AB = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& \frac{AB}{A} = \frac{0}{A}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & B = 0 \end{align*} So, in case \(A\) isn't \(0\), we find that \(B\) has to be. And, of course, if we had assumed that \(B\neq 0\), then we would discover that \(A=0\)! So, one of the factors has to be \(0\). Finally, note that there is nothing preventing both of them from being 0. □
By the way, what is that little symbol '□'. Is that a typo, you may ask? No, what I am writing there is in place of where the old scribes would have written "Q.E.D.", quad erat demonstrandum or "which is what was to be shown", a phrase that dates back to Euclid (albeit translated from Greek into Latin). So, the point of the symbol '□' or of writing "Q.E.D." is to just provide a a kind of narrative suffix to signal that the proof has stopped and the narrative mode will go back to whatever it was before the proof. After all, a proof is a special narrative mode: things might be declared within a proof that pertain specifically to the proof, and only things declared inside the proof or the statement of the theorem are admissable as evidence within the proof. So, it is a logically "bracketed" thing, if that makes sense. More important question: what does the proof or the "Fact" really mean? What's the point of it? Let's consider an example to see it at work.

Solve the following. \[ (12x-1)(x+5) = 0 \]

Answer:

As always, our solving algorithm begins with the mental declaration,
Assume that \(x\) is an honest, real number (located somewhere on the real line), with the property expressed in the given...
Now, since \(x\) is a real number, so are \(12x-1\) and \(x+5\); I mean they are actual, honest numbers sitting somewhere on the real line, even though we don't know where quite yet. Note that I can view \(12x-1\) and \(x+5\) as my \(A\) and \(B\) to apply the Fact above. What I mean is, we are looking here at a product of two numbers that come out to \(0\). The "Fact" just says that when that happens one (or both) of the numbers has to be \(0\). So, that means \begin{align*} 12x - 1 =0 &\quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x+5 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad 12x = 1 &\quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x = -5 \\ \Rightarrow \quad x = \frac{1}{12} &\quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x = -5 \\ \end{align*} So, how about that? As you can see, if what you have is a product equal to \(0\), then you can just set the individual factors equal to \(0\) and solve each of the resulting equations. (This would even work for a product of more than two factors.) Since the factors will always be simpler than what you started with, this is a helpful approach or algorithm!

Let's do a few more along those lines as a kind of warm-up. (The exercises will have one added step of complication relative to these next few problems.)

Solve each of the following.
  1. \((x-3)(x+4)=0\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & (x-3)(x+4)=0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x-3 = 0 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x + 4 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = 3 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x = -4 \\ \end{align*}
  2. \((12x-3)(x-5)=0\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & (12x-3)(x-5)=0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 12x - 3 = 0 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x - 5 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = \frac{3}{12} = \frac{1}{4} \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x =5 \\ \end{align*}
  3. \((x+10)(5x+2)=0\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & (x+10)(5x+2)=0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 10 = 0 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad 5x + 2 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = -10 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x = -\frac{2}{5} \\ \end{align*}
  4. \((2x-3)(16x+4)=0\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & (2x-3)(16x+4)=0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x-3 = 0 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad 16x+4 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = \frac{3}{2} \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x = -\frac{4}{16} = - \frac{1}{4} \\ \end{align*}

Now, we can combine this basic skill with factoring to solve certain quadratic equations.

Solve the following by factoring. \[ x^2 - 7x - 18 = 0 \]

Answer:

First, I factor. Then I equate the individual factors to \(0\) to find all possible solutions: \begin{align*} & x^2 - 7x - 18 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad &(x-9)(x+2)=0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad &x - 9 = 0 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x + 2= 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = 9 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x =-2 \\ \end{align*}

Solve the following by factoring. \[ x^2 - 8x + 7 = 0 \]

Answer:

First, I factor. Then I equate the individual factors to \(0\) to find all possible solutions: \begin{align*} & x^2 - 8x + 7 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad &(x-7)(x-1)=0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad &x - 7 = 0 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x - 1= 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad& x = 7 \quad \quad \textrm{or} \quad \quad x = 1 \\ \end{align*}
