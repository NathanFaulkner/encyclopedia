What is a rational function? Well, what does the word "rational" mean? Does it mean "sane" or "reasonable"? You may be surprised to learn that that meaning of "sane" is a metaphorical extension of what the word means here—that is, of what "rational" means in the mathematical setting.

The key to understanding it all is the word "ratio." You know what a ratio is! A ratio, of course, involves the comparison of two numbers, of saying how big one is relative to another. For instance, you could say that \(\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\) also means that "2 is half of 4." Of course, you could also say that ratios are about division. So, why do people say "rational" means "sane" or "reasonable"? It is because they are trying to say that you can weigh one thing against another—that you are capable of making decisions or discriminating or judging between choices: "How does option 1 compare to option 2?" Now, on to math. A rational function is a ratio of polynomials. (I trust that you remember what a polynomial is, especially if I remind you that those were the objects we were adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing, "solving," and graphing in the previous chapter.)

A rational function \(f\) is one given by a rule of the form \[ f(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \] where \(p\) and \(q\) are both polynomials. For example, \[ f(x) = \frac{x^3 - 7x^2 -9}{3x^2 +7} \] is a rational function and so is \[ g(x) = \frac{-4x^8 + 6x^5 - 14x^6 - 9.72}{11x^3 - 4.5x^2 + x- 9} \]
