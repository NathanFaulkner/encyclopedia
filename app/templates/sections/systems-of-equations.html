We now turn to the concept of "systems of equations" and solving them. These involve two (or, theoretically, more!) variables at once. It's good to start off with a concrete example before we jump into the "abstract" examples (where we just look at equations).

Suppose that Enzo had a stellar basketball game the other day, but a complete record of his performance wasn't recorded. Instead, all that anyone remembers is that he totaled 37 points, making 15 out of 21 attempts, and that he did not shoot a single free throw. (So, his point total came from a combination of two-point goals and three-point goals). Can you figure out how many of each, two-pointers vs. three-pointers, he made?

Answer:

Well, let's write down what we know and see what we can figure out. Let \(x\) represent the number of two-pointers he made, and let \(y\) represent the number of three-pointers he made. Then, since he scored 37 points, we know that \[ 2x + 3y = 37 \] Also, he made 15 total shots, so it is also true that \[ x + y = 15 \]

A key thing to realize is that \(x\) and \(y\) both represent numbers and that they represent the same numbers in each equation! So, for instance, it must be that \[ y = 15 - x \] But, then, by substitution, \[ 2x + 3(15-x) = 37 \] And this is just an everyday sort of equation that you are good at solving: \begin{align*} &2x + 3(15-x) = 37 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x + 45 - 3x = 37 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & -x + 45 = 37 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & -x = -8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 8 \end{align*} From \(y = 15 - x\) we thus also know that \(y = 15 - 8 = 7\). That is, he made 8 two-pointers and 7 three-pointers.

We will learn how to solve systems of linear equations, which are also sometimes called linear systems. This is systems (collections) of equations of the form \[ \begin{cases} ax + by = c \\ dx + ey = f \end{cases} \] where \(a\), \(b\), \(c\), \(d\), \(e\), and \(f\) will be (filled in) numbers, and \(x\) and \(y\) will be the variables (unknown numbers) we are solving for. A solution to the system is an \((x,y)\)-pair that satisfies both equations when substituted in. The solution set is a nice description of the set of all \((x,y)\)-pairs that satisfies the system—which is to say, makes both equations true at the same time.

Figure out whether each given point is a solution to the given system: \[ \begin{cases} x - 4y = 14 \\ 2x - 3y = 13 \end{cases} \]

  1. Point: \((1,1)\)

    Answer:

    Substitute in \(x = 1\) and \(y=1\) and see if the equations are satisfied: \[ 1 - 4\cdot 1 = 1 - 4 = -3 \neq 14, \] so the first equation is not satisfied. So, \((1,1)\) does not solve the system.

  2. Point: \((18,1)\)

    Answer:

    Substitute in \(x = 18\) and \(y=1\) and see if the equations are satisfied: \[ 18 - 4 \cdot 1 = 18 - 4 = 14, \] so the first equation is satisfied. But \[ 2\cdot 18 - 3 \cdot 1 = 36 - 3 = 33, \] so the second equation is not satisfied. A solution to a system is an \((x,y)\) pair that satisfies both, so \((18,1)\) is not a solution to the system.

  3. Point: \((2,-3)\)

    Answer:

    Substitute in \(x = 2\) and \(y=-3\) and see if the equations are satisfied: \[ 2 - 4\cdot (-3) = 2 + 12 = 14 \] and \[ 2\cdot 2 - 3\cdot (-3) = 4 + 9 = 13, \] so both equations are satisfied. So, \((2,-3)\) is a solution to the system. Does it represent the only solution? We'll have to do some other activities to get a notion of whether is or not.

An equation (in one variable) is said to be linear provided each side of the equation is equivalent to an expression of the form \( mx + b \).

In the section, "Solve for \(x\)", we solved lots of equations of this type, such as \[ 3(x-4) + 4(x-1) = 7(x+5) - 40 \] We observed that there were three types of results we could get:

  1. There could be zero solutions. (0)
  2. There could be one, unique solution. (1)
  3. The solution set could be "all reals"—an infinite set of solutions!(\(\infty\))
What do you anticipate are some different possibilities for the solution set of a system of linear equations? Keep in mind that the solution set to a system (with two variables) is a set of \((x,y)\)-pairs. What do you anticipate we can say about those sets? Actually, even though this question seems wildly open and uncertain, we will soon see that it has a very similar answer to that for equations of one variable! (Actually, we could study systems of many variables and see what can be said about all such problems.) To get an intuitive grasph of the answer, we turn now to studying the graphs of systems of equations.

Consider the system: \[ \begin{cases} 3x + 2y = 10\\ -x + 3y = -18 \end{cases} \] How many solutions does this system have?

Answer:

To find out, we are going to graph the system. All that really means is to graph both lines and then think about what the visual means in terms of the goal of solving the solution.

In order to plot, it may be more convenient to first put the equations into slope-intercept ("\(y=mx+b\)") form. I will do that for both simultaneously: \begin{align*} & \begin{cases} 3x + 2y = 10\\ -x + 3y = -18 \end{cases} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \begin{cases} 2y = -3 x + 10\\ 3y = x - 18 \end{cases} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \begin{cases} y = -\frac{3}{2} x + 5\\ y = \frac{1}{3}x - 6 \end{cases} \\ \end{align*} Now, I can just start at the \(y\)-intercept of each line, and then proceed outward, following the slope.

Now, what does this graph tell us about the solution set? Well, recall from above that a solution to a system of equations is an \((x, y)\)-pair that satisfies both equations. Recall, too, that the graph of an equation is (a picture of) the set of all pairs that satisfy that equation. So, the solution—the pair that satisfies both—will have to lie on both equations. As you can see from the picture, there is only one point that does so: the intersection of the two lines. A bit of reflection will tell you that you will have this situation—a single solution—whenever you have two lines with different slopes. If they have different slopes, they will always intersect a single point! Now, what can happen if they have the same slope? We'll see that in the next examples.

Consider the system: \[ \begin{cases} x + 2y = -1\\ -2x - 4y = -12 \end{cases} \] How many solutions does it have?

Answer:

Again, we can graph it to figure it out. Again, you might find it convenient to first put each equation into slope-intercept form: \begin{align*} & \begin{cases} x + 2y = -1\\ -2x - 4y = -12 \end{cases} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \begin{cases} 2y = -x - 1\\ - 4y = 2x -12 \end{cases} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \begin{cases} y = -\frac{1}{2}x - \frac{1}{2}\\ y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 3 \end{cases} \\ \end{align*} Here are the graphs:

So, as it turned out, the two lines had the same slope, which means they're parallel. That means they never interect! Again, as we noted in the previous example, a solution to a system of equations is an \((x, y)\)-pair that satisfies both equations. And a graph of an equation is (a picture of) the set of all of pairs that satisfies an equation. So, the solution to a system must lie on both graphs. Since they graphs do not intersection, there can be no solution to the system. (It has 0 solutions.)

Consider the system: \[ \begin{cases} x + 2y = -1\\ -2x - 4y = 2 \end{cases} \] How many solutions does it have?

Answer:

Again, we can graph it to figure it out. Again, you might find it convenient to first put each equation into slope-intercept form: \begin{align*} & \begin{cases} x + 2y = -1\\ -2x - 4y = 2 \end{cases} \Rightarrow \quad & \begin{cases} 2y = -x -1\\ - 4y = 2x + 2 \end{cases} \Rightarrow \quad & \begin{cases} y = -\frac{1}{2}x - \frac{1}{2}\\ y = -\frac{1}{2}x - \frac{1}{2} \end{cases} \\ \end{align*} Oh, look!—You get the same equation in both cases!

The graph is a bit redundant in this case, but here it is anyway:

So, how many pairs satisfy this system? Well,the solution set is "all \((x,y)\)-pairs that lie on the graphed line, i.e, that satisfy \(y = -\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{2}\)." So, how many pairs is that? How many pairs lie along this line? An infinite set of course!
