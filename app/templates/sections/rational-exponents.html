In this section, we review the basics and also introduce the more exotic notion of a negative integer power. First of all, ask yourself, what it is basic meaning of a power? What is purpose of exponents? I hope you realize, that, first of all, their purpose is to provide a method of bookkeeping when it comes to "repeated multiplication."

In this section, we consider even more "exotic" types of powers: we begin to make sense of the concept of a rational exponent—a fraction in the exponent. In the previous section, we noted the following analogy:
Exponents : Multiplication :: Multiplication : Addition
Without displaying it, we also discussed this:
Negative Exponents : Division :: Negative Coefficients : Subtraction
The connection in this last statement can be expressed as "subtracting numbers is how you kill off repeated terms in a sum, while dividing is how you kill off repeated factors in a product." By the way, did you know that while a term in a product is called a factor, a term in a sum is called a summand? This may help me to further explain the analogy, which is why I bring it up here. In this section, we further the analogy: You can think of regular division as breaking apart a number into equal summands. For example, the fact that \(\frac{9}{2}= 4.5\) is verified by equation \[ 9 = 4.5 + 4.5 \] Right? That is, "dividing 9 by 2" is to break apart 9 into two parts—which are added together. But, don't you know, there is another way in which we can be said to "break apart 9 into two parts," like so: \[ 9 = 3 \cdot 3 \] Now, we have broken apart 9 in two—into two factors—into repeated factors instead of a repeated sum. For this reason, we say that 2 is worth "one half factor of 9." Since exponents are about counting repeated factors, we thus invent the new notation \[ 9^{\frac{1}{2}} \, \textrm{is defined to be } \, 3 \] Put another way, \(\frac{1}{2}\) in the exponent has the same meaning as "square root," which I know you have studied before. In general, we have the following definition:

For any positive integer \(n\) and any reasonable real number \(a\), we define the symbol \(a^{\frac{1}{n}}\) by the equation \[ \overbrace{a^{\frac{1}{n}} \cdot a^{\frac{1}{n}} \cdot \cdots \cdot a^{\frac{1}{n}}}^{\textrm{\(n\) times}} = a \] That is, \(a^{\frac{1}{n}}\) is the value that helps to break apart the number \(a\) into \(n\) many equal factors. What did I mean by "reasonable" real number?—only that some caution is needed when applying this concept to \(a\lt 0\). For example, \[ 32^{\frac{1}{5}} = 2 \] because \[ 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 32 \] The number 2 is used to break apart 32 into 5 equal factors. There is also alternate notation for this concept. Sometimes, a radical notation will be used to convey the same idea, as in the following: \[ 9^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{9} \] In general, \[ a^{\frac{1}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a} \]

As a recap, fractions in the exponent involve the concept of breaking the number being raised to that exponent into equal factors whereas a fraction in the coefficient ("out front") asks you to break apart the number that follows into equal summands: \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} \cdot 9 &= 4.5 & \textrm{... because } 9 = 4.5 + 4.5 \\ 9^\frac{1}{2} &= 3 & \textrm{... because } 9 = 3\cdot 3 \end{align*} Similarly, \begin{align*} \frac{1}{5} \cdot 32 &= 6.4 & \textrm{... because } 32 = 6.4 + 6.4 + 6.4 + 6.4 + 6.4\\ 32^\frac{1}{5} &= 2 & \textrm{... because } 32 = 2\cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \end{align*}

Now, we can extend the definition of exponents to include an sort of fraction.

For any reasonable real number \(a\) and any positive integers, \(m\) and \(n\), \[ a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \overbrace{a^{\frac{1}{n}} \cdot a^{\frac{1}{n}} \cdot \cdots \cdot a^{\frac{1}{n}}}^{\textrm{\(m\) times}} \] Put another way, \[ a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \left(a^\frac{1}{n}\right)^m \] For example, \[ 8^{\frac{2}{3}} = \left(8^{\frac{1}{3}}\right)^2 = 2^2 = 4 \] Illustrated even better, I am going to highlight \(8^\frac{2}{3}\) in orange in the following: \[ 8 = {\color{orange}2 \cdot 2} \cdot 2 \] So, \(2\cdot 2 = 4\) is literally "two thirds factors of 8"! We have broken 8 into 3 equal parts—multiplication-wise—and taken two of them in a product. Using radical notation, \[ a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m = \overbrace{\sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \cdots \cdot \sqrt[n]{a}}^{\textrm{\(m\) times}} \]

One more extension, which connects back to the previous section, and we're done making up new exponents. (There is only one further extension to make to say how you might define weirder real number exponents like \(\pi\) in the exponent, but most people would overlook it, anyway. Even more suprisingly a meaning can even be given to putting \(i\) in the exponent.)

For any real number \(a\) and any positive integers, \(m\) and \(n\), \[ a^{-\frac{m}{n}} = \frac{1}{a^{\frac{m}{n}}} \] So, \(a^{-\frac{m}{n}}\) is the thing that is worth "minus \(\frac{m}{n}\) factors of \(a\)", which is really just the thing that will kill off \(a^{\frac{m}{n}}\), namely, \(a^{\frac{m}{n}}\) in the denominator.

The following expresses two possible things to try when looking to simplify an expression involving fractions in the exponent.

For any reasonable real numbers \(a\) and \(b\) and any positive integer \(n\), \[ \left(ab\right)^\frac{1}{n} = a^\frac{1}{n} b^\frac{1}{n} \] Expressed using radical notation, \[ \sqrt[n]{ab} = \sqrt[n]{a}\sqrt[n]{b} \]

Proof:

\begin{align*} \overbrace{\left(a^\frac{1}{n} b^\frac{1}{n}\right) \cdot \cdots \cdot \left(a^\frac{1}{n} b^\frac{1}{n}\right)}^{n \, \textrm{times}} &= \overbrace{a^\frac{1}{n} \cdot \cdots\cdot a^\frac{1}{n} }^{n \, \textrm{times}} \cdot \overbrace{b^\frac{1}{n} \cdot \cdots\cdot b^\frac{1}{n} }^{n \, \textrm{times}}\\ & = ab \end{align*} Expressed in words, if you want to break apart a product of two numbers into \(n\) equal parts, then you can just break apart each factor into \(n\) equal parts. □

Of course, if you can distribute a fractional exponent or a radical over a product of two numbers, you can also distribute it over a product of any number of factors. In particular, this means we have the following.

For any reasonable real number \(a\) (\(a\geq 0\) is reasonable) and any positive integers \(m\) and \(n\), \[ \left(a^m\right)^\frac{1}{n} = a^{\frac{m}{n}} \]

Proof:

\begin{align*} \left(a^m\right)^\frac{1}{n} &= \left(\overbrace{a \cdot \cdots \cdot a}^\textrm{\(m\) times}\right)^\frac{1}{n}\\ & = \overbrace{a^\frac{1}{n} \cdot \cdots \cdot a^\frac{1}{n}}^\textrm{\(m\) times} & \textrm{By Remark 1}\\ & = a^\frac{m}{n} & \textrm{By Definition 2} \end{align*}
The following is also true, but it is better just to see it illustrated.

For any reasonable real number \(a\) and any positive integers \(m\) and \(n\), \[ \left(a^\frac{1}{n}\right)^\frac{1}{m} = a^\frac{1}{mn} \]
Put in words, this means that if you want to break apart a number \(a\) into (\(mn\))-many equal factors (which is what the right-hand side is about), you might (do what the left-hand says to do and) first break it into \(n\) many equal factors and then break each of those into \(m\)-many equal factors—resulting in \(m\)-many groups of \(n\) equal factors for a total of (\(mn\))-many equal factors. What does that mean? Well, as an illustration, suppose you wanted to figure out what \(64^\frac{1}{6}\) is. Well, you know that it is \(8\cdot 8\). But you also know you can break down the 8's into equal factors. In all: \( 64 = 8 \cdot 8 = (2\cdot 2 \cdot 2)(2\cdot 2 \cdot 2) \). In words, you can see that \(2\) is "one third of one half of factors of 64," or, put more simply, 2 is "one sixth factor of 64"! Therefore, \(64^\frac{1}{6} = 2\). In math symbols, \(8 = 64^\frac{1}{2}\) and \(2 = 8^\frac{1}{3}\), so it is true that \[ 64^\frac{1}{6} = 2 = 8^\frac{1}{3} = \left(64^\frac{1}{2}\right)^\frac{1}{3} \] Putting all the foregoing results together, the following is true, but the proof would just be annoying to look at.

For any reasonable real number \(a\) and any positive integers \(m\), \(n\), \(p\), and \(q\) \[ \left(a^\frac{m}{n}\right)^\frac{p}{q} = a^\frac{mp}{nq} \] Put another way, if \(r\) and \(s\) are rational numbers, then \[ \left(a^r\right)^s = a^{rs} \] which can be expressed in words: "If you raise a base to a number and then raise it again, then you can multiply the exponents." Here is one illustration that will put you in the right frame of mind for remembering this fact: \[ \left(7^3\right)^5 = \left(7^3\right)\left(7^3\right)\left(7^3\right)\left(7^3\right)\left(7^3\right) = 7^{5\cdot 3} = 7^{15} \]
You may like to have a handy synopsis of everything we could possibly say about the "rules for manipulating exponents."

Rules for Manipulating Exponents:

For any reasonable real number \(a\) (be cautious about \(a\leq0\)), and any rational numbers \(r\) and \(s\) (actually, it works if \(r\) and \(s\) are any real numbers, too):
RuleExample
\(\displaystyle a^r \cdot a^s = a^{r+s}\) \(\displaystyle 5^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot 5^\frac{3}{4} = 5^{\frac{1}{2}+\frac{3}{4}} = 5^{\frac{2}{4}+\frac{3}{4}} = 5^\frac{5}{4}\)
\(\displaystyle \left(a^r\right)^s = a^{rs}\) \(\displaystyle \left(3^\frac{5}{2}\right)^\frac{4}{3}=3^{\frac{5}{2}\cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{10}{3}}\)
\(\displaystyle \left(ab\right)^r = a^r b^r\) \(\displaystyle (2500)^\frac{1}{2} = \left(25 \cdot 100\right)^\frac{1}{2} = 25^\frac{1}{2} \cdot 100^\frac{1}{2} = 5\cdot 10 = 50\)
\(\displaystyle a^{-r} = \frac{1}{a^r}\) \(\displaystyle 36^{-\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{36^\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{6}\)
\(\displaystyle \frac{a^r}{a^s} =a^{r-s} = \frac{1}{a^{s-r}}\) \(\displaystyle \frac{4^{\frac{5}{2}}}{4^{\frac{1}{2}}} = 4^{\frac{5}{2} - \frac{1}{2}} = 4^{\frac{4}{2}} = 4^2 = 16\)
\(\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^r = \frac{a^r}{b^r}\) \(\displaystyle \left(\frac{27}{64}\right)^\frac{1}{3} = \frac{27^{\frac{1}{3}}}{64^{\frac{1}{3}}} = \frac{3}{4}\)
