In this section, we continue to "put it all together"—that is, we continue to use our skills of dealing with rationals that are being added, subtracted, multiplied, or divided by other rationals—but in the context of solving an equation. Actually, this will hopefully be pretty straight-forward: we are just trying to figure out possible values for \(x\) that satisfy a given equation; we are trying to find a simple description of the solution set.

In the first two examples, I will use the "trick" of multiplying both sides of the equation by any denominators that I want to cancel. (In later examples, I will use another method; you'll have to decide which you like better, possibly after stumbling a bit.) After canceling all denominators in this way, what results will either be a linear equation (no \(x^2\)) or a quadratic (contains \(x^2\)). But you have techniques for solving either case. In the first example, a linear equation results after multiplying both sides by all denominators.

Find the solution set. \[\displaystyle \frac{3}{5x} = \frac{2}{x-7} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &\frac{3}{5x} = \frac{2}{x-7} \\ \Rightarrow \quad &\frac{3}{\cancel{5x}} \cdot \cancel{5x} = \frac{2}{x-7} \cdot 5x \\ \Rightarrow \quad &3 = \frac{10x}{x-7} \\ \Rightarrow \quad &3 \cdot (x-7) = \frac{10x}{\bcancel{x-7}} \cdot \bcancel{(x-7)} \\ \Rightarrow \quad &3x - 21 = 10x \\ \Rightarrow \quad & -7x = 21 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -3 \end{align*}

Of course, if you wan,t you can "cross-multiply"—which really means to skip steps as you multiply both sides by any denominators and cancel appropriately. The series of steps that "cross multiplying" skips can be see in the following. \begin{align*} & \frac{A}{B} = \frac{C}{D}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{A}{\cancel{B}} \cdot \cancel{B} = \frac{C}{D} \cdot B\\ \Rightarrow \quad & A = \frac{BC}{D} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & A \cdot D = \frac{BC}{\bcancel{D}}\cdot \bcancel{D}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & AD = BC \end{align*} "Cross multiplying" is when you skip those middle steps (provided they all make sense to you!) and just write the following: \begin{align*} & \frac{A}{B} = \frac{C}{D}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & AD = BC \end{align*} In the second example, a quadratic equation results after mutliplying both sides by the denominators:

Find the solution set. \[\displaystyle \frac{2}{x-4} = \frac{x+5}{5} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &\frac{2}{x-4} = \frac{x+5}{5} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2 \cdot 5 = (x-4)(x+5) \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^2 + x - 20 = 10 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^2 + x - 30 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-5)(x+6)= 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 5 \textrm{ or } x = -6 \end{align*}

Now, we have to do a few more examples, because as we make the problems a little more complicated, some different things can happen. Owing to the fact that we are canceling denominators involving the variable, there can be a loss information—specifically, about \(x\)-values that would create 0 in the denominators of the original equation and therefore cannot be solutions. So, if you don't take care in your method for solving—or, at least, check your answers when you are through—you may get "erroneous" solutions. We'll see that in the next two examples.

Find the solution set. \[\displaystyle \frac{3}{x(x+1)} + \frac{3}{x+1}= -\frac{1}{x} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &\frac{3}{x(x+1)} + \frac{3}{x+1}= -\frac{1}{x} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{3}{x(x+1)} + \frac{3x}{x(x+1)} = -\frac{1}{x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{3x+3}{x(x+1)} = -\frac{1}{x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{3\cancel{(x+1)}}{x\cancel{(x+1)}} = -\frac{1}{x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{3}{x} = -\frac{1}{x}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3 = -1 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \textrm{No solution} \end{align*}

What I am doing in this past example—and will continue to do in subsequent examples—is actually to delay canceling common factors until later steps. This can help to avoid obtaining "erroneous answers." You may find that if you follow another method, you will get erroneous answers. The antidote is either to try my methods or to be sure to check your answer.

Find the solution set. \[\displaystyle \frac{x}{x-1} + \frac{5}{x+2}= \frac{3}{(x+2)(x-1)} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &\frac{x}{x-1} + \frac{5}{x+2}= \frac{3}{(x+2)(x-1)} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{x}{x-1} \cdot \frac{x+2}{x+2} + \frac{5}{x+2} \cdot \frac{x-1}{x-1}= \frac{3}{(x+2)(x-1)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{x(x+2) + 5(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{3}{(x-1)(x+2)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{x^2 + 7x -5}{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{3}{(x-1)(x+2)}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{x^2 + 7x -5}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac{3}{(x-1)(x+2)} = 0\\ {\color{blue}\Rightarrow} \quad & \frac{x^2 + 7x - 8}{(x-1)(x+2)} = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{\cancel{(x-1)}(x+8)}{\cancel{(x-1)}(x+2)} = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \frac{x+8}{x+2} = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x+ 8 = 0 \cdot(x+2)\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x+ 8 = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -8\\ \end{align*}

I marked one of my steps in blue. That's because I want to call attention to what I did there: I combined like terms! Do you see what I mean? I'm going to use that technique again on the next one. But, by the way, there are a large number of ways you might go about solving these equations. I am only showing you one, but you are welcome to try other strategies that come to your mind. Just take my advice and check your answers!

Find the solution set. \[\displaystyle \frac{5}{x} + 2 = \frac{6}{x} \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} &\frac{5}{x} + 2 = \frac{6}{x} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2 = \frac{1}{x} & \bigg(\textrm{Note: I combined like terms: } \frac{6}{x} - \frac{5}{x } = \frac{1}{x}. \bigg)\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 2x = 1\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = \frac{1}{2}\\ \end{align*}
