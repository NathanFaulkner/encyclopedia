I mentioned in the previous section that we are on a quest to learn how to break apart polynomials into "primes." I have alerted you to the fact that we are leading up to the fact that any polynomial can (theoretically) be broken apart in to the following factored form: \[ f(x) = a(x-z_1)(x-z_1) \cdots (x-z_n) \] where \(a\) is some number, as are \(z_1\), \(z_2\),…, \(z_n\). So, factors of the form \(a\) (a number) and \(x-z\), where \(z\) is a number, are the "primes" when it comes to polynomials. Why should we want to do this? Well, for one thing, if we can figure out how to factor a polynomial like that, then we can also solve equations involving polynomials. For instance, ponder how in the world you are supposed to solve the following equation: \[ x^4 + 4x^3 - 34x^2 - 76x + 105 = 0 \] Well, what if I told you that the equation factors like so? \[ x^4 + 4x^3 - 34x^2 - 76x + 105 = (x-1)(x+3)(x-5)(x+7) \] Then, I bet you can solve it! Solve the following: \[ (x-1)(x+3)(x-5)(x+7) = 0 \]

Answer:

As discussed in the section, Solving by Factoring, Part 1 if you have a product of numbers that equals \(0\), as we have here, then it must be that (at least) one of the numbers has to be \(0\). Thus, we make the following deductions: \begin{align*} & (x-1)(x+3)(x-5)(x+7) = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x-1 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 3 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 7 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 1 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 5 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -7\\ \end{align*}

So, you can hopefully see there is a connection between factoring and being able to solve polynomial equations: If you can factor, then you can solve. Actually, the converse is true as well—and just as useful: If you can solve, then you can factor.

For polynomial \(f\) and number \(z\), \(f(z) = 0\) if and only if \(x-z\) divides evenly into \(f(x)\). In other words, if you know that \(f(z) = 0\), then you know \(f(x)\) factors as \[ (x-z)q(x) \] for some polynomial (of degree one less!) \(q(x)\). And, if you know that \(x-z\) divides evenly into \(f(x)\), then you can also conclude that \(f(z) = 0\).

Proof:

The proof is just to think about what doing long division of \(f(x)\) by \(x-z\) would give you. Say that you get quotient \(q(x)\) and remainder \(r\) when dividing by \(x-z\): \[ \frac{f(x)}{x-z} = q(x) + \frac{r}{x-z} \] To put that another way (after multiplying both sides by \(x-z\)): \[ f(x) = (x-z)q(x) + r \] Now, check out what happens when you plug in \(z\) for \(x\): \[ f(z) = (z-z)q(z) + r = 0\cdot q(z) + r = 0 + r = r \] Thus, by transitive property of your equals sign, if \(f(z) = 0\), then \(r = 0\), which means that \(f(x) = (x-z)q(x)\) (\(x-z\) divides into \(f(x)\) evenly). And, if \(r=0\) (that is, if \(x-z\) divides into \(f(x)\) evenly), then \(f(z) = 0\). □
By the way, what is that little symbol '□'. Is that a typo, you may ask? No, what I am writing there is in place of where the old scribes would have written "Q.E.D.", quad erat demonstrandum or "which is what was to be shown", a phrase that dates back to Euclid (albeit translated from Greek into Latin). So, the point of the symbol '□' or of writing "Q.E.D." is to just provide a a kind of narrative suffix to signal that the proof has stopped and the narrative mode will go back to whatever it was before the proof. After all, a proof is a special narrative mode: things might be declared within a proof that pertain specifically to the proof, and only things declared inside the proof or the statement of the theorem are admissable as evidence within the proof. So, it is a logically "bracketed" thing, if that makes sense.

So, what's the point of all of this? What this all amounts too is that, if you want to solve or factor, then you can start by trying to guess some answers and that those answers will actually help you find the others, because they can be used to factor the original polynomial—thus breaking it down in to simpler and, hopefully, easier parts.

Suppose you were able to guess that \(x=3\) is a zero of the following polynomial. Use this to find all solutions to the equation: \[ x^3 - 4x^2 + x + 6 = 0 \]

Answer:

Let's quickly verify that there wasn't a mistake in the prompt by plugging in \(x=3\): We want to make sure it comes out to zero: \[ f(3) = 3^3 - 4\cdot 3^2 + 3 + 6 = 0 \] So, since \(x=3\) is a zero of \(f(x)\), \(x-3\) will divide into it evenly. I'll use synthetic division to carry this division out, since it is faster, and then the resulting quotient will be quadratic, which I could use the quadratic formula to solve if I have to, or maybe I could factor it:
\(3\)\(1\)\(-4\)\(1\)\(6\)
\(3\)\(-3\)\(-6\)
\(1\)\(-1\)\(-2\)\(0\)

Notice that we got \(0\) for the remainder! This verifies that \(x=3\) is a zero of the original function (making our earlier check redundant.) Also, correctly interpreting the quotient line, we can see that \(f(x)\) factors like so: \[ x^3 - 4x^2 + x + 6 = (x-3)(x^2-x+2) \] Also, we luck out on this one since the quotient is easily factorable \[ x^2 - x + 2 = (x+1)(x-2) \] (Sometimes you may have to use the quadratic formula or use vertex form, etc., to find the remaining solutions.) Thus, it is easy to find all solutions to our original equation: \begin{align*} &\sympy{latex(expand(expr))} = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-3)(x+1)(x-2) = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x-3 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x+ 1 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 2 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x= 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -1 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 2\\ \end{align*}

Suppose you were able to guess that \(x = -2\) is a zero of the following polynomial. Use this to find all solutions to the equation: \[ x^3 - 2x^2 - 6x + 4 = 0 \]

Answer:

Let's do synthetic division to discover the quotient that results when we divide the given polynomial by \(x+2\).
\(-2\)\(1\)\(-2\)\(-6\)\(4\)
\(-2\)\(8\)\(-4\)
\(1\)\(-4\)\(2\)\(0\)
We can now proceed to find all solutions: \begin{align*} & x^3 - 2x^2 - 6x + 4 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x+2)(x^2 - 4x +2) = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x+ 2 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x^2 - 4x +2 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x= -2 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4- \sqrt{16 - 4\cdot 2}}{2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4+ \sqrt{16 - 4\cdot 2}}{2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x= -2 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4- \sqrt{8}}{2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4+ \sqrt{8}}{2}\\ \end{align*}

Suppose you were able to guess that \(x=-5\) is a zero of the following polynomial. Use this to find all solutions to the equation: \[ 3x^3 + 11x^2 - 18x + 10 = 0 \]

Answer:

Let's do synthetic division to discover the quotient that results when we divide the given polynomial by \(x+5\).
\(-5\)\(3\)\(11\)\(-18\)\(10\)
\(-15\)\(20\)\(-10\)
\(3\)\(-4\)\(2\)\(0\)
We can now proceed to find all solutions: \begin{align*} & x^3 - 4x^2 + x + 6 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x+5)(3x^2 - 4x +2) = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x+ 5 = 0 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 3x^2 - 4x +2 = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x= -5 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4- \sqrt{16 - 4\cdot 3\cdot 2}}{2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4+ \sqrt{16 - 4\cdot 3\cdot 2}}{2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad &x= -5 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4- \sqrt{-8}}{2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{4+ \sqrt{-8}}{2}\\ \end{align*} Since \(\sqrt{-8}\) isn't a real number, we find that \(x=-5\) is actually the only solution.
