This section continues our study of division of polynomials. But from here forward we concentrate on the most important class of division problems—which are also the simplest ones! The class of problems I have in mind are when you just divide by something of the form \(x-z\), where \(z\) is some number—such as dividing by \(x-3\).

In the section that follows, we will argue that, aside from factoring out a common numerical factor, factors of the form \(x-z\) are like the "primes" when it comes to dividing polynomials—that is, in addition to common numerical factors (think "GCF") like 2 or 3, they represent the most basic elements into which all polynomials can be factored. This enables you to reduce any division problem (or factoring problem!) to dividing by simple factors of the form \(x-z\)—at least, theoretically. Think for a moment about factoring regular, old numbers. For instance, if you were asked to factor \(108\), you could sit there and ponder whether it is divisible by \(18\) if you wanted (it is) but wouldn't it be simpler just to check if 108 is divisible by primes like 2 and 3? Of course, you can easily note that 108 is divisible by 2, factoring it as \(108 = 2\cdot 54\). The rest of the factorization is easy since \(54\) shows up in the times table. So, slowly but surely, you find the following factorization of 108 by dividing by one prime at a time: \[ 108 = 2^2 3^3 \] We are going to do "the same thing" with polynomials—we are going to factor them in to "primes"! That is, we will argue that every polynomial function \(f\) can be written in the following form: \[ f(x) = a(x-z_1)(x-z_2)\cdots (x-z_{n}) \] where \(a\), \(z_1\), \(z_2\),…, \(z_n\) are some numbers (\(z_1\), \(z_2\),…, \(z_n\) are called the "zeroes" of the function) and \(n\) is the degree of the polynomial. (There is a little fine print here concerning polynomials of degree 0—an example of which is \(f(x) = 5\)—but they aren't very important). Carry out the following long division problem. (Determine the quotient and remainder when performing \(1332 \div 11\).)
\(1\)\(1\)\(1\)\(3\)\(3\)\(2\)

Answer:

How does the long division algorithm work? Well, what you are after is to figure out "how many times" \(11\) goes into \(1332\). The answer—which is called the quotient (note that the Latinate word "quotient" just has to do with posing a question, as in "what number?") gets written on the upper line bit by bit. What we do, as you may well know, is that keep adding to the quotient—the thing we multiply \(11\) by—and subtract what results from the dividend (\(1332\)) as we go until the divident is gone—meaning we have added in parts to the quotient until, when multiplied, we get all of the dividend of \(1332\) and nothing is left over; there is no remainder. At each intermediate step, you get a remainder, but you keep adding to the quotient to keep killing off more and more of that ever-dwindling remainder until it is gone.

Each step in the process involves looking at the different place-values—the thousands columns, the hundreds, the tens, and the ones column. You figure out the biggest whole number multiple of each place-value you can multiply the divisor of \(11\) by to kill off as much of the dividend as possible at each step. In this case, our leading place-value of the dividend of \(1332\) is 1,000. Since multiplying this by \(11\) would be to big (11,000 is bigger than 1,332) I actually skip that column and go to the next: I think about the hundreds column. Since 1,100 (eleven hundred!) is less than 1,332 but \(200\cdot 11 = 2,200\) is too big, I know that the leading digit of my quotient is a \(1\) in the hundreds column:

If you think about it, our algorithm is just a demonstration of the fact that \[ 1332 - 11\cdot 1000 - 11\cdot 20 - 11\cdot 1 - 11\cdot \frac{1}{11} = 0 \] whic, using the distributive property, means that \begin{align*} &1332 - 11 \cdot\left(1000 + 20 + 1 + \frac{1}{11}\right) = 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 1332 - 11 \cdot\left(121+\frac{1}{11}\right) = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 11 \cdot \left(121+\frac{1}{11}\right) = 1332 \end{align*} This, I hope you will see in the next example, is precisely how long division of polynomials will also work.
