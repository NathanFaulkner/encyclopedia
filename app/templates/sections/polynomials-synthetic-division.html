This section continues our study of division of polynomials. But from here forward we concentrate on the most important class of division problems—which are also the simplest ones! The class of problems I have in mind are when you just divide by something of the form \(x-z\), where \(z\) is some number—such as dividing by \(x-3\).

In the section that follows, we will argue that, aside from factoring out a common numerical factor, factors of the form \(x-z\) are like the "primes" when it comes to dividing polynomials—that is, in addition to common numerical factors (think "GCF") like 2 or 3, they represent the most basic elements into which all polynomials can be factored. This enables you to reduce any division problem (or factoring problem!) to dividing by simple factors of the form \(x-z\)—at least, theoretically. Think for a moment about factoring regular, old numbers. For instance, if you were asked to factor \(108\), you could sit there and ponder whether it is divisible by \(18\) if you wanted (it is) but wouldn't it be simpler just to check if 108 is divisible by primes like 2 and 3? Of course, you can easily note that 108 is divisible by 2, factoring it as \(108 = 2\cdot 54\). The rest of the factorization is easy since \(54\) shows up in the times table. So, slowly but surely, you find the following factorization of 108 by dividing by one prime at a time: \[ 108 = 2^2 3^3 \] We are going to do "the same thing" with polynomials—we are going to factor them in to "primes"! That is, we will argue that every polynomial function \(f\) can be written in the following form: \[ f(x) = a(x-z_1)(x-z_2)\cdots (x-z_{n}) \] where \(a\), \(z_1\), \(z_2\),…, \(z_n\) are some numbers (\(z_1\), \(z_2\),…, \(z_n\) are called the "zeroes" of the function) and \(n\) is the degree of the polynomial. (There is a little fine print here concerning polynomials of degree 0—an example of which is \(f(x) = 5\)—but they aren't very important). For instance, in a few sections from now, we will be able to determine that the polynomial \[ 2x^4 - 14x^3 - 26x^2 + 206x - 168 \] can be factored \[ 2(x-3)(x+4)(x-7)(x-1) \] This "factored" form, of course, is useful for figuring out the \(x\)-intercepts of the graph and, really, for solving any polynomial equation.

In the exposition below, I will be using the following vocab:

A number \(z\) is called a zero of the function \(f\) if \[ f(z) = 0 \] An important example is when \[ f(x) = a(x-z_1)(x-z_2)\cdots(x-z_n) \] You can see that the numbers \(z_1\), \(z_2\),…, \(z_n\) are zeroes of \(f\). For instance, \[ f(z_1) = a(z_1-z_1)(z_1-z_2)\cdots(z_1-z_n) = a \cdot 0 \cdot (z_1-z_2) \cdots (z_1-z_n) = 0 \] As a concrete example, \(3\), \(-4\), \(7\), and \(1\) are zeroes of the polynomial \[ f(x) = 2(x-3)(x+4)(x-7)(x-1) \] Can you see why? Zeroes are also often called roots.
Since we will be doing a lot of long division problems where we just divide by something of the form \(x-z\), in this section we will learn a stream-lined method for doing so. It may be annoying to learn this method at first, but, like others before you, you will soon grow to like it, because it is so much faster. In fact, it would be quite fun to see you all race on problems—with one person using the old method and one person using the new method, which is called \textbf{synthetic division}. As always, I will begin with a really detailed example, then do a quicker one, and then you'll try some on your own. But, first, you'll appreciate getting your brain warmed-up by executing our first example using the old method of long division

Use synthetic division to determine quotient and remainder resulting from the following: \[ \left(x^3 - 5 x^2 + 25\right) \div \left(x-3\right) \] Put your answer in the form \[ q(x) + \frac{r}{d(x)} \] where \(q(x)\) is the quotient, \(d(x)\) is the divisor, and \(r\) is the remainder.

Answer:

You'll need to build a mental bridge from the old method—long division—to the new method of synthetic division. I'll place the old method on the right for comparision to help me explain the new method. Basically, synthetic division just involves writing down "the highlights" of the process of carrying out long division. The idea is this: when doing regular long division of polynomials, you are already using the columns to mark what powers of \(x\) are involved—for instance, using the third column from the right for the terms involving \(x^2\), just like you use the third column for hundreds (Note: \(100 = 10^2\)). But, since the column tells you what power is inolved there, why do you need to write the \(x\)'s at all?

So, synthetic division involves the discipline of lining up the coefficients of terms according to their powers. The initial set-up is to place the "zero" of the divisor in a box at left. Since the divisor is \(x-3\), the zero is \(3\). Then, extending to the right, you write the coefficients of the dividend in separate columns in descending order of degree—being vigilant about writing in 0's for missing terms:

\(3\)\(1\)\(-5\)\(0\)\(25\)
\(x\)\(-\)\(3\)\(\quad x^3\)\(-\)\(5x^2\)\(+\)\(0x\)\(+\)\(25\)

What is going to happen is that we will place the coefficients of the quotient and remainder (i.e.: "the answer" to the problem) in the spaces below that horizontal line. We will write those coefficients in descending order of their powers. Since we are dividing \(x^3-5x^2+25\) by \(x-3\), and we know that is just the question of what (quotient) to multiply \(x-3\) by to get as close as you can to \(x^3-5x^2+25\), I hope you can see that the leading term of the quotient will be \(x^2\). So, you just place a \(\color{blue}{1}\) in that first slot. You'll have to mentally know that it represents \(\color{blue}{1x^2}\) and that the powers will descend from there.

\(3\)\(1\)\(-5\)\(0\)\(25\)
\(\color{blue}{1}\)
\(\color{blue}{1x^2}\)
\(x\)\(-\)\(3\)\(\quad x^3\)\(-\)\(5x^2\)\(+\)\(0x\)\(+\)\(25\)

Next up, on the right, you multiply \(x-3\) by \(\color{blue}{1x^2}\) and "kill off" some of the dividend. But right here is where some streamlining comes in! Why do we even need to write down the fact that \(x^3\) gets killed off? That was the whole point in multiplying by \(\color{blue}{1x^2}\) in the first place, so it is redundant to write it out. Instead, at left, we write the only interesting thing that happens at this second step: \(\color{magenta} -3x^2\) gets subtracted from \(-5x^2\)—resulting in a new leading term of \(\color{green} -2x^2\), which means \(\color{green} -2x^2\) will have to get killed off next, which means \(\color{green}-2\) will be the next coefficient in our quotient. I record all this activity at left by multiplying my \(\color{blue} 1\) by the positive \(3\) and adding that to \(-5\). You see, subtracting a negative is positive, which means subtracting "\([\textrm{whatever}] \cdot (-3)\)" will be the same as adding "\([\textrm{whatever}] \cdot 3\)". So, the subtraction gets "built in" to the "synthetic" division process by changing that \(-3\) in the divisor \(x-3\) to \(3\) and just adding all the time. So, in the next column over and just below the \(-5\), I write the product of "the number in the box" (the \(3\)) and the previous coefficient (the \(\color{blue} 1\)) of my quotient: \(3\cdot \color{blue} 1 = \color{magenta} 3\). Then I add \(-5\) and \(\color{magenta} 3\) to get \(\color{green} -2\).

\(3\)\(1\)\(-5\)\(0\)\(25\)
\(\color{magenta} 3\)
\(\color{blue}{1}\)\(\color{green} -2\)
\(\color{blue}{1x^2}\)\(\color{green}{-}\)\(\color{green}{2x}\)
\(x\)\(-\)\(3\)\(\quad x^3\)\(-\)\(5x^2\)\(+\)\(0x\)\(+\)\(25\)
\({\color{magenta}-}\left(x^3\right.\)\(-\)\(\left.\color{magenta}3x^2\right)\)
\(\color{green}-2x^2\) \(+\) \(0x\) \(+\) \(25\)

After that, the process repeats: multiply your newest coefficient (in this case \(\color{green}-2\)) on the quotient line (the bottom line) by "the number in the box." Place that product (in this case, \(\color{orange}-6\)) in the next column over directly below the next coefficient in the dividend and add the result to get your next coefficient (in this case, \(\color{purple}-6\)) in the quotient:

\(3\)\(1\)\(-5\)\(0\)\(25\)
\(\color{magenta} 3\)\(\color{orange}-6\)
\(\color{blue}{1}\)\(\color{green} -2\)\(\color{purple}-6\)
\(\color{blue}{1x^2}\)\(\color{green}{-}\)\(\color{green}{2x}\)\(\color{purple}-\)\(\color{purple}{6}\)
\(x\)\(-\)\(3\)\(\quad x^3\)\(-\)\(5x^2\)\(+\)\(0x\)\(+\)\(25\)
\({\color{magenta}-}\left(x^3\right.\)\(-\)\(\left.\color{magenta}3x^2\right)\)
\(\color{green}-2x^2\) \(+\) \(0x\) \(+\) \(25\)
\(\color{orange}-\) \(\left(-2x^2\right.\) \(+\) \(\left.\color{orange}6x\right)\)
\(\color{purple}-6x\) \(+\) \(25\)

The steps are the same until the last step. But since the last step involves finding the remainder, it is common to separate it out visually. Your remainder will always be the last number in the quotient line, but I recommend putting a box around it:

\(3\)\(1\)\(-5\)\(0\)\(25\)
\(\color{magenta} 3\)\(\color{orange}-6\)\(\color{brown}-18\)
\(\color{blue}{1}\)\(\color{green} -2\)\(\color{purple}-6\)\(\color{red}7\)
\(\color{blue}{1x^2}\)\(\color{green}{-}\)\(\color{green}{2x}\)\(\color{purple}-\)\(\color{purple}{6}\)\(\color{red} +\) \(\color{red}\frac{7}{x-3}\)
\(x\)\(-\)\(3\)\(\quad x^3\)\(-\)\(5x^2\)\(+\)\(0x\)\(+\)\(25\)
\({\color{magenta}-} \left(x^3\right.\)\(-\)\(\left.\color{magenta}3x^2\right)\)
\(\color{green}-2x^2\) \(+\) \(0x\) \(+\) \(25\)
\(\color{orange}-\) \(\left(-2x^2\right.\) \(+\) \(\left.\color{orange}6x\right)\)
\(\color{purple}-6x\) \(+\) \(25\)
\(\color{brown}-\) \(\left(-6x\right.\) \(+\) \(\left.\color{brown}18\right)\)
\(\color{red}7\)
\(-\) \(7\)
\(0\)

The very last step---which is redundant in this case since we also carried out the long-division—is to interpret the numbers on the quotient line as the coefficients of the quotient listed in descending order with the final remainder listed last. The thing to realize here is that the degree of the quotient will be one less than the degree of the original thing you divided—the dividend—because the divisor was degree 1. Since the dividend in this case is degree 3 (\(x^3-5x^2 + 25\)) the quotient is degree 2; you start with \(1x^2\) and continue in decreasing order. So, the quotient with final remainder is \[ x^2 - 2x - 6 + \frac{7}{x-3} \]

Use synthetic division to determine quotient and remainder resulting from the following: \[ \left(3x^5 - 8x^3 + 2x^2 - x + 1\right) \div \left(x+2\right) \] Put your answer in the form \[ q(x) + \frac{r}{d(x)} \] where \(q(x)\) is the quotient, \(d(x)\) is the divisor, and \(r\) is the remainder.

Answer:

As a quick recap, put the "zero" of the divisor in the box. Since \(x+2\) is the divisor, its zero is \(-2\). Put the coefficients of the dividend in order, placing \(0\)'s for any missing terms. Bring down the leading coefficient of your dividend as the leading coefficient of the quotient. Multiply each coefficient o f the quotient by the zero in "the box", adding this to the next coefficient of the dividend to get the next coefficient of the quotient and finally the remainder:
\(-2\)\(3\)\(0\)\(-8\)\(2\)\(-1\)\(1\)
\(-6\)\(12\)\(-8\)\(12\)\(-22\)
\(3\)\(-6\)\(4\)\(-6\)\(11\)\(-21\)

Finally, interpret the numbers on the bottom line as the coefficients of the quotient, plus remainder, with terms listed in descending order of power. Since the dividend was degree 5, the quotient is degree 4: \[ 3x^4 - 6x^3 + 4x^2 - 6x + 11 - \frac{21}{x+2} \]
