As a combination of two previous sections, we now graph the solutions to what you already encountered in solving compound inequalities. For instruction, please consider these examples, which are the same that we solved in the section on compound inequalities, though, here we graph them.

Consider this very basic compound inequality: \[ x \lt -3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x \gt 4 \]
  1. What does it mean?

    Answer:

    This is shorthand for the narrative "\(x\) is a real number that is (either) less than \(-3\) (to the left of \(-3\), on a horizontal real line) or greater than 4". It is critical that we contemplate what this means.

    Again, you ought to look at this inequality as a "test of membership" in a club. So, take some random real number, like 5. We test it for membership by considering each of the inequalities in the compound expression but also carefully processing the meaning of the logical connector. So, is \(x\) less than -3? *No*, of course not. Does that mean that \(x\) is out of the club? No! That's because of the 'or': to be in this "club", 5 only needs to meet one of the conditions in the compound expression---not both! So, "and" means "has to satisfy both" whereas "or" means "only needs to satisfy one of the conditions". Indeed, as \(5 \gt 4\), 5 is indeed in the club; it is part of the set defined by this compound expression.

    We'll graph this thing on the real line in a moment—which may be of some help to you, but another good visual comes in the form of a Venn diagram. Just take a look at this link for 'and' and this link for 'or' .

Solve the inequality. Give your answer both in inequality notation and by graphing the solution on a number line. \[ -3 \lt 3x - 12 \leq 18 \]

Answer:

It's time to take a step back and consider the underlying narrative of the solving process; this is just something we need to do each time something new is introduced—to carefully consider the question, "What does it mean?"

In solving, we are first saying, "Suppose there is a real number \(x\) with the given property." In this case, we are imagining that if you take 12 away from the product of 3 times \(x\), you get something that falls between -3 and 18 but possibly equal to 18. Let me point out that the inequalities we are studying in this section are called "compound". That really means that they include more than one statement about \(x\)—added information, if you will!—which are joined together by "logical connectors", 'and' or 'or'. See the first two examples for a definition of these.

In the context of this problem, the 'and' connector (which is always implied when only a single \(x\) is used—\(x\) must satisfy both statements!) means that both, whatever \(x\) ends up being, it the value of \(3x - 12\) must be greater than \(-3\) but also less than or equal to \(18\). So, we have two separate inequalities that we can solve in turn: \begin{align*} & 3x - 12 \gt -3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad 3x-12 \leq 18 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x \gt 9 \quad \textrm{and} \quad 3x \leq 30 & \textrm{For each, add 12 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \gt 3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad x \leq 10 & \textrm{For each, divide both sides by 3.}\\ \end{align*}

Of course, there is a certain amount of redundancy in this process—in that we end up doing the same thing to both sides of each inequality at each step. So, there is a more streamlined way of writing this:

\begin{align*} & -3 \lt 3x - 12 \leq 18 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 9 \lt 3x \leq 30 & \textrm{I added 12 to all three columns.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3 \lt x \leq 10 & \textrm{I divided all three columns by 3.}\\ \end{align*}

Solve the inequality. Give your answer both in inequality notation and by graphing the solution on a number line. \[ 3x - 12 \lt -3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 3x - 12 \geq 18 \]

Answer:

Of course, this one isn't very different. I have switched the inequalities and changed it to an "or". (I changed it to an "or", so that you get to compare it to the previous one; I switched the inequalities because otherwise the answer would be rather confusing! See future examples for an explanation of what I mean here!)

In the context of this problem, the 'or' connector (which will always be explicitly given) means, whatever \(x\) ends up being, it will (either) be true that the value of \(3x - 12\) will be less than \(-3\) or that the value of \(3x - 12\) will be greater than or equal to \(18\). So, again, we have two separate inequalities that we can solve in turn: \begin{align*} & 3x - 12 \lt -3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 3x - 12 \geq 18 & \textrm{Given}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3x \lt 9 \quad \textrm{or} \quad 3x \geq 30 & \textrm{For each, add 12 to both sides.}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x \lt 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x \geq 10 & \textrm{For each, divide both sides by 3.}\\ \end{align*}
