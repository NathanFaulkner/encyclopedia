You are (hopefully) now capable of solving a large class of quadratic equations. But what about the following? \[ 2x^2 - 7x +1 =0 \] Could you solve that? Well, maybe—but one problem is that that quadratic is not factorable. In this section, we start building a process that will enable us to solve any quadratic. It will not (seem to) involve factoring at all. But since we aren't starting with the hardest problems, some of these could also be solved by factoring, even though we will be using a new method.

By the way:

A quadratic equation is any equivalent to one of the form \[ ax^2 +bx +c = 0, \] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are numbers, but \(a\neq 0\). This presentation is called standard form.

After reading the foregoing definition, you should ask yourself what I mean by "equivalent to" in the above definition? That is, what does it mean for two equations to be equivalent?

To learn our new method, we will be starting with very basic examples but making them increasingly complex.

Solve each of the following by taking square roots.
  1. \(x^2 = 4\)

    Answer:

    As always, an equation—and the prompt to solve it—invites us to initiate a narrative by saying, "Suppose that \(x\) is an honest, real number with the given property." This equation poses the question, what number times itself comes out to 4? Of course, one answer is "\(2\)". But, the important thing to realize is that there is another possibility: \(x\) could be \(-2\) as well.
  2. \(3x^2 = 12\)

    Answer:

    Again, we are supposing that \(x\) is a real number (sitting somewhere on the real line) with the property given by the equation. This means that \(3x^2\) is also a real number; indeed, we know that this real number is \(12\)! Of course, we can then divide both sides to learn that \[ x^2 = 4 \] and thus \[ x = 2 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -2 \] as before.
  3. \(3(x-5)^2 = 12\)

    Answer:

    As a first step, I'd divide both sides by \(3\): \begin{align*} & 3(x-5)^2 =12 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-5)^2 = 4 \end{align*} Next, it is helpful to recall the secret narrative behind this process that begins by declaring that \(x\) is a real number. Since \(x\) is a real number (or so we are assuming) \(x-5\) is also a real number. That's good to know since we are told that the square of \(x-5\) is \(4\). That means that \(x-5\) has to be either \(2\) or \(-2\), though it really isn't possible to know which it is based only on our assumptions. So, I continue: \begin{align*} & (x-5)^2 = 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 5= -2 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5= 2\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 7 \end{align*}
  4. \(3(x-5)^2 + 7= 19\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & 3(x-5)^2 + 7= 19 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 3(x-5)^2 =12 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x-5)^2 = 4 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x - 5= -2 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - 5= 2\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = 3 \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = 7 \end{align*}

It's also important to realize that this process works even in more awkward cases:

Solve each of the following by taking square roots.
  1. \(x^2 = 3\)

    Answer:

    Again, this equation poses the question, what number times itself comes out to \(3\)? The answer this time is not so obvious. Upon a little reflection, you will hopefully agree that it must be a number between \(1\) and \(2\), since multiplying \(1\) by itself is too small and multiplying \(2\) by itself is too big. However, we will content ourselves with writing a symbol for this number. (Actually, if you think about it, '2' is just a symbol for a number as well, isn't it? We might use another symbol, like \(x\), for this number if we so choose.) The symbol, as you will probably know is \(\sqrt{3}\), which is read "the square root of 3". Of course, you might also realize that it is called "the square root" because it is the number that "gives rise to 3" when squaring. The image suggested is that this unknown number lying buried underground gave rise to the part that we can see (like the stalk of a plant above ground)—namely 3—through squaring. The important thing to realize is that there really is a real number with this property. You can imagine it as the side length in inches of a square whose area is 3 square inches. That has to exist at least in an ideal sense, no? One more important thing to realize: if you take the negative of whatever that number is and multiply it by itself, then it will come out positive; it will come out to \(+3\) as well. So, there are two solutions: \[ x = \sqrt{3} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\sqrt{3} \]
  2. \(4(x+5)^2 + 13 = 25\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & 4(x+5)^2 + 13 = 25 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 4(x+5)^2 =12 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x+5)^2 = 3 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x + 5= -\sqrt{3} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x + 5= \sqrt{3} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -5 -\sqrt{3} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -5 + \sqrt{3} \end{align*}

In yet other cases, you will find that there is no (real) solution to the quadratic:

Solve each of the following by taking square roots.
  1. \(x^2 = -3\)

    Answer:

    Since the square of any (real) number is positive (or at least 0), there is no real number that will solve this equation.
  2. \(4(x+5)^2 + 13 = 1\)

    Answer:

    \begin{align*} & 4(x+5)^2 + 13 = 1 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & 4(x+5)^2 = - 12 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x+5)^2 = -3 \\ \end{align*} Since the square of any (real) number is positive (or at least 0), there is no real number that will solve this equation.

We will learn later—in a concept that you will at first find to be bizarre—that there is actually a far-reaching utility in "pretending" that equations such as those in the last example do have a solution—albeit one which is not a real number. This leads us to study "imaginary numbers" —and what you might prefer to call "the imaginary element," \(i\). The point of \(i\) is that \(i^2 = -1\) (which means \(i\) certainly isn't a real number, to that extent that you insist on calling it a number at all), and so it provides a "solution" to equations such as \[ x^2 = -1 \] The real purpose in inventing such a bizarre concept lies "outside the scope of our present study," which you should regard as the teacher's cop-out.

Let's do one more example, one that involves fractions at some point but otherwise is really just the same as those above.

Solve for \(x\) by taking square roots. (Find the solution set.) \[ - 16 \left(x + 9\right)^{2} = -8 \]

Answer:

\begin{align*} & - 16 \left(x + 9\right)^{2} = -8 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & (x+9)^2 = \frac{1}{2} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x+9 = -\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x+9 = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x = -9-\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -9+\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}\\ \end{align*}
