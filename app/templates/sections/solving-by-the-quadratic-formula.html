No matter what way you do it, you can put any standard form quadratic into vertex form. As we saw in the section on solving by completing the square, you can "solve" any quadratic equation in the sense that you can carry out the solving process—accepting that sometimes you won't get any real solution. Indeed, you can even "solve" the generic case. When you do so, what you get is a formula—a famous formula. Here is the "generic" case that I have in mind: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = 0, \] in which \(a \neq 0\).

Solve the following. \[ ax^2 + bx + c = 0, \]

Answer, via Method 1:

As discussed in the section entitled, Quadratics in Standard Form we can take any quadratic given initially in standard form, \[ ax^2 + bx + c \] and rewrite it in vertex form \[ a(x-h)^2 + k \] There are different ways to accomplish this that you might review by clicking the link just referenced, but I will review one way here. Start by writing out what your goal is, namely to figure out what \(a\), \(h\), and \(k\) will make this equation true: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = a(x-h)^2 + k \] One approach is to multiply out the right side and then make a comparison of the coefficients on each side: \begin{align*} ax^2 + bx + c &= a(x-h)^2 + k \\ &= a\left((x-h)(x-h)\right) + k \\ &= a\left(x^2 - 2hx + h^2\right) + k \\ &= ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \end{align*} It also helps to rewrite the left side right next to the end result from rewriting the right side: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 - 2ah x + ah^2 + k \] The first easy observation to make is that we are talking about the same \(a\) on both sides. (A good mathematician would observe that I potentially introduced an element of confusion by using the symbol '\(a\)' in both the standard form and the vertex form, but you can make the observation from this stage that the two numbers represented by '\(a\)' on the left and the right will indeed be the same, so no untruth comes out of the potential confusion introduced there.) Next, I compare the coefficients of \(x\) on the left and the right sides. On the left, we have \(bx\) (so, the coefficient is \(b\). On the right we have \(-2ahx\). The scenario that we are trying to express with this "generic example" is that \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are known numbers; they are the givens. The goal is to find \(h\) and \(k\); they are like the unknowns. So, what can we set \(h\) to to make this work out? Evidently, we just need to figure out what real number \(h\) makes \(-2ah = b\). All you have to do is to solve for \(h\)! I can do that by taking the negative of both sides and then dividing by \(2a\): \begin{align*} & -2ah = b\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & 2ah = -b \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & h = \frac{-b}{2a} \end{align*} In the section on Quadratics in Standard Form we noted that \(k\) is really just the \(y\)-coordinate that gets paired with \(x=h\), so you can obtain \(k\) by just plugging in your value for \(h\) for \(x\). However, I am not going to do that here. Instead, I am going to find an expression for \(k\) by plugging my expression for \(h\) in for \(h\) and then comparing the constant term: So, I make that substitution, \(h = \frac{-b}{2a}\): \begin{align*} & ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 -2ahx + ah^2 + k\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 -2a \left(\frac{-b}{2a}\right) x + a\left(\frac{-b}{2a}\right)^2 + k\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 +b x + a\frac{b^2}{4a^2} + k\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & ax^2 + bx + c = ax^2 +b x + \frac{b^2}{4a} + k\\ \end{align*} The key step there was carrying out the computation \[ \left(\frac{-b}{2a}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{-b}{2a}\right)\left(\frac{-b}{2a}\right) = \frac{(-b)(-b)}{(2a)(2a)} = \frac{b^2}{4a^2} \] The final step is to compare the constant terms—that is, the terms without an \(x\). On the left we have just \(c\) and on the right we have \(\frac{b^2}{4a} + k\). The point is to imagine that \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are givens and that \(k\) is what we are after. Our observation here is that if \[ c = \frac{b^2}{4a} + k \] then all will work out. Thus, for any given \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\), all we need is to make \[ k = c - \frac{b^2}{4a} \] In all, we now have formulas for the coordinates \(h, k\) for the vertex depending on the coefficients of the quadratic given in standard form: \[ ax^2 + bx + c = a(x-h)^2 + k \] where \(h = \frac{-b}{2a}\) and \(k = c - \frac{b^2}{4a}\) (and "\(a\) is \(a\)".) Now, we recall that our intent was to solve the equation \[ ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \] But, we also know that the left side is equal to \(a(x-h)^2 + k\) with \(h\) and \(k\) as just described, so we can make that change. That is, it is equivalent to solve this: \[ a(x-h)^2 + k = 0 \] We do this by following the approach modeled in the section, Solving Quadratics: Only One x: \begin{align*} & a(x-h)^2 + k = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & a(x-h)^2 = -k \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & (x-h)^2 = \frac{-k}{a} \\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x-h = -\sqrt{\frac{-k}{a}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x - h = \sqrt{\frac{-k}{a}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = h-\sqrt{\frac{-k}{a}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = h+\sqrt{\frac{-k}{a}}\\ \end{align*} To complete our generic solution, we might plug in the formulas for \(h\) and \(k\) that we obtained above, namely \(h = -\frac{b}{2a}\) and \(k = c - \frac{b^2}{4a^2}\). Actually, it is traditional to first rewrite \(k\) by finding a common denominator: \[ k = c - \frac{b^2}{4a} = \frac{4ac}{4a} - \frac{b^2}{4a} = \frac{4ac - b^2}{4a} \] Here is my solution with these expressions plugged in: \begin{align*} & x = h-\sqrt{\frac{-k}{a}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = h+\sqrt{\frac{-k}{a}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\sqrt{\frac{-\left(\frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}\right)}{a}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\sqrt{\frac{-\left(\frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}\right)}{a}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\sqrt{\frac{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a}}{a}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\sqrt{\frac{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a}}{a}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\sqrt{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\sqrt{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}}\\ \end{align*} We are well on our way to deriving the famous quadratic formula. Really, we have already done that, except that it isn't quite presented in the traditional manner yet. All that's left is to realize that "the square root of a quotient will be the quotient of the square roots of the factors." For example, the square root of \(\frac{9}{4}\) will be \(\frac{3}{2}\). This is just a consequence of how multiplication of fractions works: "straight across". That is, since \[ \frac{3}{2}\cdot \frac{3}{2} = \frac{3\cdot 3}{2 \cdot 2} = \frac{9}{4} \] then it follows that \[ \sqrt{\frac{9}{4}} = \frac{\sqrt{9}}{\sqrt{4}} = \frac{3}{2} \] This means that we can rewrite \[ \sqrt{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}} = \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4a^2}}. \] That's nice—but what exactly was the point of that, you might ask? The payoff is to realize that we can simplify the expression \[ \sqrt{4a^2} \] I mean, it's just posing the question, "what times itself is \(4a^2\)?" If you answer, "\(2a\)", then you just might be right. After all, \[ (2a)(2a) = 4a^2 \] On the other hand, the square root of a number is defined as the positive number that can be multiplied by itself to obtain the target number. For example, the square root of \(4\) is 2 even though \(-2\) times itself is also \(4\). I mention this only because we are trying to tackle the generic problem—which is a way of saying that we are trying to derive a formula that will provide the solution to a quadratic equation in absolutely any situation. And, the problem is, half of all cases will involve \(2a\) being positive (when \(a\) is positive) and half will involve \(2a\) being negative. If \(a\) is positive, we have that \[ \sqrt{4a^2} = 2a \] but if \(a\) is negative, it will actually be that \(-2a\) is positive and hence \(-2a\) is "the positive number that you can multiply by itself to obtain \(4a^2\)", so \[ \sqrt{4a^2} = -2a \] in that case. But, it doesn't matter; here's why: If \(a\) is positive, then we can write our solutions as follows, after substituting \(2a\) for \(\sqrt{4a^2}\): \begin{align*} & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4a^2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4a^2}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b - \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b + \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \end{align*} On other hand, if \(a\) is negative, then we substitute \(-2a\) for \(\sqrt{4a^2}\): \begin{align*} & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4a^2}} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4a^2}}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{-2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{-2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b}{2a}+\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b}{2a}-\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = -\frac{b + \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = -\frac{b - \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \end{align*} So, in the end, all that happens is our two solutions switch places, but our solution set remains the same.

So, that's it! That's the quadratic formula!

Answer, via Method 2:

The more traditional approach to derive the quadratic formula is "completing the square". Again, this involves adding or subtracting numbers from both sides until one side follows either the "special pattern" \[ (x+B)^2 = x^2 + 2Bx + B^2 \] or \[ (x-B)^2 = x^2 - 2Bx + B^2 \] It may be helpful to review the section on factoring special patterns. Following the traditional approach, I will "complete the square": \begin{align*} & ax^2 + bx + c = 0\\ \Rightarrow \quad & ax^2 + bx = -c \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x \,+ \quad= -\frac{c}{a} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x \,+ \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2= -\frac{c}{a} + \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 \\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2= -\frac{c}{a} + \frac{b^2}{4a^2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2= -\frac{4ac}{4a^2} + \frac{b^2}{4a^2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & \left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2= \frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2} \\ \Rightarrow \quad & x+\frac{b}{2a}= -\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x+\frac{b}{2a}= \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x=-\frac{b}{2a} -\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x=-\frac{b}{2a} + \frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x=\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \end{align*} That's it—that's the quadratic formula!

Now, what's the point of all this? Nothing much: all we are doing is solving equations of the type that we already solved in the section on solving by completing the square. The thing is, real life problems often aren't all that fun to perform completing the square on; the numbers just aren't nice. In those cases, you might find that you prefer referring to (or, traditionally, memorizing) the quadratic formula:

The quadratic formula is used to find the solution to quadratic equations expressed in standard form: \[ ax^2 + bx +c = 0 \] (\(a\neq 0\)) if and only if \[ x=\frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x=\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \] These two solutions are often presented in a lazy way (which I do not encourage a new student of this subject matter to use): \[ x = \frac{-b\pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \] (There is a little fine print here: We have elsewhere observed that sometimes you don't get a real solution. It can also happen that \(b^2 = 4ac\) which zeroes out the "\(\pm\)" term, which reduces the set to a single solution.)

Then, instead of following a process to solve, you just plug in the given numbers to get your solution. This is isn't necessarily a better approach than "solving by hand", but it can be convenient. That's why we are studying it here. Indeed, the problems I will be presenting in this section will be pretty arbitrary—meaning, I provide no guarantee that they would be fun to solve using completing the square or by putting the quadratic into vertex form. On the contrary, I encourage you to practice the quadratic formula here.

Use the quadratic formula to solve. \[ 2x^2 - 8x + 5 = 0 \]

Answer:

Note that \(a=2\), \(b=-8\), and \(c=5\). So, \begin{align*} & x=\frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x=\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-(-8)-\sqrt{(-8)^2-4\cdot 2 \cdot 5}}{2 \cdot 2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-(-8)+\sqrt{(-8)^2-4\cdot 2 \cdot 5}}{2 \cdot 2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{8-\sqrt{64-40}}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{8+\sqrt{64-40}}{4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{8-\sqrt{24}}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{8+\sqrt{24}}{4}\\ \end{align*} Often, you can further simplify an answer (such as this one) but the relevant skills appear in another section, and I am neglecting that here.

You can also use the quadratic formula to solve equations that are not in standard form—or, rather, you can if you first put them into standard form.

Use the quadratic formula to solve. \[ x^2 + 27x = 15x - 13 - x^2 \]

Answer:

First, pull all terms to one side (with $0$ on the other) and put it in the standard form order: \[ 2x^2 + 12x + 13 = 0 \] Now use the quadratic formula (or complete the square, etc., if you prefer): Note that \(a=2\), \(b=12\), and \(c=13\). So, \begin{align*} & x=\frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x=\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-12-\sqrt{(12)^2-4\cdot 2 \cdot 13}}{2 \cdot 2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-12+\sqrt{(12)^2-4\cdot 2 \cdot 13}}{2 \cdot 2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-12-\sqrt{144-104}}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-12+\sqrt{144-104}}{4}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-12-\sqrt{40}}{4} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-12+\sqrt{40}}{4}\\ \end{align*} Often, you can further simplify an answer (such as this one) but the relevant skills appear in another section, and I am neglecting that here.

Use the quadratic formula to solve. \[ x^2 + x + 1 = 0 \]

Answer:

Note that \(a=1\), \(b=1\), and \(c=1\). So, \begin{align*} & x=\frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x=\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-1-\sqrt{(1)^2-4\cdot 1 \cdot 1}}{2 \cdot 1} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-1+\sqrt{(1)^2-4\cdot 1 \cdot 1}}{2 \cdot 1}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-1-\sqrt{1-4}}{2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-1+\sqrt{1-4}}{2}\\ \Rightarrow \quad \quad & x = \frac{-1-\sqrt{-3}}{2} \quad \textrm{or} \quad x = \frac{-1+\sqrt{-3}}{2}\\ \end{align*} Note that \(\sqrt{-3}\) can't be a real number because there is no real number that multiplies together to give you a negative such as \(-3\).

A key observation you need to make is that there will be no real solution to a quadratic precisely when the number under the square root symbol is negative. Why? Well, because the question of a square root of a negative number is what real number times itself comes out negative. But we know that you cannot multiply any real number times itself to yield a negative. By the way, a "square root sign" is often called a "radical". Really, the word is the same: "radical" means "root" as in "radish." Etymologies can be very instructive!
